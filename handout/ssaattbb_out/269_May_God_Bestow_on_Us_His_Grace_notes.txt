ARRANGER NOTES
==============
May God Bestow on Us His Grace (Es Wollt uns Gott Genædig)
SSAATTBB arrangement for double choir and lever harp
Martin Luther, 1524 / Setting: Concordia Kinderchöre, 1908
Arranger: SSAATTBB expansion (2026)

TRANSPOSITION
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
Original key:  C major (0 accidentals)
Chosen key:    C major (0 accidentals) — no transposition required
Semitones shifted: 0

Harp lever positions for C major (Eb storage tuning):
  All 7 levers raised:
    Ab→A  (lever up)
    Eb→E  (lever up)
    Bb→B  (lever up)
    Fb→F  (lever up — NOTE: Fb string = E in Eb tuning; raising gives F natural)
  Actually in Eb storage tuning the raised-lever set for C major is:
    Raise: Eb→E, Bb→B, Ab→A, Db→D, Gb→G, Cb→C, Fb→F
  All 7 levers must be raised to play in C major on a harp stored in Eb.

C major was chosen because it is within the valid range (0 accidentals,
well within the ≤3 flats / ≤4 sharps constraint) and all 8 voices sit
comfortably in professional ranges without transposition.

════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
HARMONIC SUBSTITUTIONS
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
No full harmonic substitutions were required. The original harmonies are
fundamentally diatonic in C major. The accidentals (^G#, ^F#, ^C#) appear
only as chromatic passing tones or raised leading tones in inner voices and
were handled as individual note replacements (see Diatonic Violations below)
rather than requiring chord-level reharmonization.

Bar 17: The original alto voice has F# (^F) functioning as the third of a
D major chord (secondary dominant V/V). Rather than reharmonizing the whole
bar, the chord is treated as a plain G major passing harmony (IV→V
interpretation), and the F# is replaced with F natural. The resulting
sonority is G–B–D–F (G7) read diatonically, which resolves smoothly to the
subsequent bar's harmony.

TEXTURE PLAN
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
The arrangement opens with a reduced texture: S1+S2 alone on the pickup,
with TTBB entering gradually through bars 1–2 and the full 8-voice texture
not established until the repeat (Section A'). Section A functions as an
antiphonal presentation — SSAA leads, TTBB shadows a third lower, building
ensemble weight through the repeat. Section B deploys full 8-voice homophony
for the hymn's central development, with inner voices (A1, A2, T1, T2)
carrying independent melodic lines that move in contrary motion. The final
Section C (outro/intro markings) returns to a more transparent texture as the
voices converge stepwise to the final cadence, with B1 grounded on E2/A2 and
S1 descending from E5. The three texture types used are: (1) reduced opening
with gradual entry (bars 0–2), (2) antiphonal SSAA vs TTBB in Section A,
and (3) full 8-voice homophony in Section B.

RULE COMPROMISES
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
1. Bar 4 (A section, bar 4): Bars and B1 move in parallel motion on the
   descent C,–G,,–A,,–F,,. The original SATB bass line was preserved in B1,
   and Bar follows a third above. Parallel motion between Bar and B1 was
   accepted here because (a) these are the two lowest voices and parallel
   bass motion is a recognized stylistic feature of chorale writing, and
   (b) the upper voices provide sufficient contrary motion to prevent a
   parallel fifths/octaves problem between non-adjacent voices.

2. Bar 14 (B section, bar 2): T1 and T2 move largely in parallel thirds
   on the ascending figure B,–B,–A,–B, / G,–G,–F,–G,. Parallel thirds
   between a paired tenor duo is intentional harmonic doubling rather than
   a voice-leading error; this is a deliberate texture choice for blend.

3. Bar 19 (B section, bar 7): The bass voices Bar and B1 share the same
   rhythm and approximate contour on D,–F,–C,–D,. As in bar 4, this is
   accepted stylistic parallel bass motion at a rhythmically active point
   where upper voices provide compensating contrary motion.

4. Final bar (bar 25): A2 and T1 sustain E, (E3 sounding for T1) across
   the bar — consecutive unison between cross-choir voices. This is
   explicitly permitted by the rules (A2/T1 register break) and is used
   here for a deliberate blending effect at the final cadence.
