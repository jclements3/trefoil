The Haupt setting uses repeated secondary-dominant coloring: A
major/dominant-7th chords (V/ii in C major) created by ^F (F#) and
^C (C#) in inner voices. All such instances in voices other than
S1 are replaced with diatonic substitutes per the diatonic
constraint. Soprano I retains all notes from the original soprano,
which happens to be entirely diatonic in this piece.

Bar 3, beat 4 — Original chord: A7 (implied by ^F in alto).
  Replaced with: G major (V in C). Result: diatonic color; the
  soprano's G–G motion across the bar confirms the V sonority.

Bar 4, beat 2 — Original chord: A7 again (^F in alto passing to G).
  Replaced with: passing IV6 sonority (F in alto creates F–A–C).
  The alto sustains D–D through the beat, producing a ii7 flavor
  over the descending bass that resolves naturally to the fermata.

Bar 5, beat 4 — Original chord: A7 (^F in alto at end of phrase).
  Replaced with: subdominant passing harmony. Alto moves G–A–F,
  diatonic; the resulting harmony is IV (F–A–C) approaching the
  bar-6 cadence.

Bar 6, beats 3–4 — Original chords: A7 (^C in alto, ^F in tenor
  together form a tonicization of D minor, i.e., V/ii).
  Replaced with: ii–I cadence in C (D minor → C major). Alto holds
  A3–G3 (neighbor motion), tenor descends F3–E3. The cadence
  retains its weight without the chromatic color.

Bar 7, beats 1–3 — Original chords: A major (^C in alto) and A7
  (^F in tenor) on consecutive beats, setting up a ii-chord arrival.
  Replaced with: Am (vi) → C (I) → G (V) over three beats, all
  diatonic. The ascending soprano A–A–A–d still implies a harmonic
  intensification; the diatonic sequence substitutes the chromatic
  original with a modal plateau.

Bar 8, beats 2–4 (tenor) — Original tenor had ^F (F#3) on two
  tied beats through the barline into bar 9.
  Replaced with: F natural passing motion in T2 (G–A–B diatonic);
  T1 holds B–C–D resolving upward. Both are entirely diatonic.

Bar 8, beat 3 (bass) — Original bass had ^C, (C#3) on a sub-beat.
  Replaced with: C natural (C3). The bass line B2–C3 is a natural
  half-step rise using the built-in leading tone B (diatonic in C
  major), so no harmonic weight is lost.

TEXTURE PLAN
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

The arrangement opens with 4 voices (S1, A1, T1, B1) on the
pickup only, expanding to 6 (SSAA + T1/B1) through bar 1 and then
to full 8 at bar 2. This graduated entry builds ensemble weight
gradually, reserving the full 8-voice texture for the stronger
phrases.

Phrase 1 (pickup through bar 3): Antiphonal texture — SSAA choir
presents the melody with harmonization on beats 1–2 of bar 1 while
TTBB enters on beats 3–4. Both choirs merge into homophonic block
from bar 2 onward. Alto voices move in contrary motion to soprano
throughout bar 3 for textural interest.

Phrase 2 (bars 4–6): Call-and-response between outer voices (S1
and B1) carrying the outer melodic line while inner voices sustain
harmonic support. Fermatas at bars 4 and 6 create space for 4–3
suspensions to sound and resolve in the upper voices.

Phrase 3 (bars 7–10): Canonic imitation — T1 echoes S1's ascending
A–A–A–d line at the tenth below (E–E–E–F), creating a stretto
effect. Alto voices move in contrary motion (A1 descends E–C–D–D;
A2 ascends A–G–F–G). The climax arrives at bar 8 beat 1 (S1 = C5,
T1 sounding E4) with all 8 voices active. Final bar gathers into a
full 8-voice block chord with B1 anchored at C2.

Texture types used:
  1. Full 8-voice homophony (bars 2, 10)
  2. Antiphonal exchange SSAA vs TTBB (bar 1)
  3. Canonic imitation S1/T1 (bars 7–8)
  4. Call and response S1/B1 with inner sustain (bars 5–6)
  5. Stretto convergence at final cadence (bars 9–10)

RULE COMPROMISES
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Baritone third-above-B1 rule, multiple bars:
  The rule states Bar should harmonize "a third above B1." In six
  places, a strict third above B1 would have produced a prohibited
  T-voice/B-voice unison (Baritone unison with T1 or T2). In each
  case, Bar was displaced by a step (to a second or fourth above
  B1 instead) to break the unison:

  Bar 3, beat 2: B1=G3, strict third=B3 (=T2 pitch). Bar→A3.
  Bar 4, beat 3: B1=G3, strict third=B3 (=T1 pitch). Bar→A3.
  Bar 5, beat 2: B1=G3, strict third=B3 (=T2 pitch). Bar→A3.
  Bar 6, beat 1: B1=E3, strict third=G3 (=T2 pitch). Bar→A3.
  Bar 6, beat 3: B1=D3, strict third=F3 (=T2 pitch). Bar→E3.
  Bar 8, beat 3: B1=E3, strict third=G3 (=T2 pitch in passing).
    Bar→A3.
  Bar 9, beat 2: B1=E3, strict third=G3 (=T2 sounding). Bar→A3.

  In all cases the displacement is minimal (one step) and preserves
  the Baritone's position above B1.

Bar 3, octave parallels S2/T1:
  S2 (sounding G4–B4–C5–B4) and T1 (sounding G3–B3–C4–B3) move
  in parallel octaves through bar 3. This is a deliberate octave
  coupling across the choir boundary (S2 in SSAA, T1 in TTBB),
  used to strengthen the ascending phrase before the voices
  separate at bar 4. Parallel octaves between non-adjacent voice
  pairs are widely accepted in 8-voice chorale writing at this
  texture density; the effect is one of structural reinforcement
  rather than contrary to good voice leading.

S2 low register, bar 6:
  S2 descends to C4 on beats 3–4 of bar 6, slightly below the
  most comfortable part of S2 tessitura. The descent supports the
  4–3 suspension resolution in the upper voices and the proximity
  to A1 (A3) below creates a tight cadential close. No rule is
  broken; the color is intentional and brief.
