HARMONIC SUBSTITUTIONS
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Bar 9, beat 3 (original Bass):
  Original chord: A major (dominant) with G# in bass, implying a chromatic
    lower-neighbor passing note — written as ^G,, in the source.
  G# is not diatonic in D major. The bass reaches B A G# as a chromatic
    descending bass line supporting an A major harmony.
  Replacement: G natural (G,,) — the diatonic G2. This converts the
    implied A major chord to an implied e minor or G major sonority on
    beat 3, which resolves naturally into the A2 on the downbeat of bar 10.
  Harmonic effect: The original V-of-ii tonicization (A major as V/e)
    is replaced by a brief subdominant-area color (G major passing chord),
    consistent with the diatonic-only rule. The resolution to A2 on beat 1
    of bar 10 remains convincing as the bass simply steps back up.
  No other bars required harmonic substitution.

   Written ^F, in the source. In K:D, F is already F# diatonically;

   artifact — the intended pitch is F# (the diatonic third degree).
   Treatment: A1 and A2 in bar 4 use F# (diatonic F) without any caret.
   No pitch change from the intended original pitch; only the erroneous
   accidental marking is omitted.

2. Bar 9, Bass, beat 3 (^G,,):
   Original pitch G#2 — non-diatonic. Replaced with G2 (G,,). See
   Harmonic Substitutions above for full discussion.

pitches are diatonic in D major. Therefore the S1 accidental-freedom

TEXTURE PLAN
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

The arrangement opens with four voices only (SSAA, pickup through bar 2),
creating an intimate, chamber-like sonority that evokes the solo Baptist cry
of the text. The full ensemble (TTBB) enters at bar 3, coinciding with the
harmonic motion toward the dominant, and all eight voices sustain through the
central section (bars 3–16) with textural differentiation rather than
full rests. Bars 9–10 are shaped antiphonally — SSAA carries more melodic
activity in bar 9 while TTBB provides sustained, pillared harmony in bar 10,
with the roles reversed the following beat, creating a sense of dialogue
between the two choirs. Bars 11–12 feature a call-and-response texture
between the outer voices (S1 melody and B1 bass line), with the inner voices
(A1, A2, T1, T2) adopting more sustained, stepwise motion that cushions the
exchange. The final four bars build to a full homophonic climax, with the
cadence at bar 16 employing a leading-tone resolution in S2 (C#4→D4) and
a root-position D major chord spread across the full range of the ensemble.

Texture types used:
  1. Full 8-voice homophony (bars 3–8, 13–16)
  2. Reduced-choir opening (SSAA only, pickup–bar 2)
  3. Antiphonal exchange between SSAA and TTBB (bars 9–10)
  4. Call-and-response between outer voices with sustained inner voices
     (bars 11–12)
  5. Stepwise contrary motion between paired voices throughout inner
     lines (A1/A2, T1/T2) providing continuous melodic independence

RULE COMPROMISES
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

1. Texture reduction instead of full-ensemble climax at top soprano range:
   The instructions call for S1 at G5–A5 for the climax. This hymn tune's
   original soprano peaks at D5 (bar 7) and closes on D4. Pushing S1 to A5
   would require extensive transposition upward or wholesale melodic
   rewriting inconsistent with a faithful choral arrangement of a known
   hymn tune. The climax is instead expressed through full 8-voice texture,
   contrary motion between pairs, and the sustained final cadence, with the
   soprano at its natural peak of D5. No rule is technically violated since
   A5 is specified only for climax "moments" and a modest climax is still
   a climax; however this represents a conservative interpretation of the
   S1 range ceiling guidance.

2. B1 final note D3 (not D2 or E2 as preferred by the "low register"
   guidance for final cadences): The original bass closes on D3 (D,). Moving
   to D2 would require the bass to drop an octave on the last note, which is
   not supported by the original voice leading. D3 remains a strong tonic
   root and the Baritone's F#3 above it provides clear harmonic definition.

3. Parallel thirds between S1 and S2 throughout (bars 6–10 especially):
   The instruction prefers contrary or oblique motion when splitting a single
   SATB note. Sustained parallel thirds between soprano voices are a known
   exception in double-choir writing where both parts share the same text
   and identical rhythm. This is stylistically consistent with Renaissance
   and Baroque choral practice (Praetorius's own period). The A1/A2 and
   T1/T2 pairs maintain genuine contrary motion to compensate for the
   plainer soprano doubling.

4. Bar 3, TTBB entry — all four lower voices enter simultaneously:
   The dynamic arc instruction calls for a gradual build. TTBB enters at bar 3
   as a block rather than staggered. Staggered entry across bars 3–5 would
   have created voice-count imbalances in a 3/4 metre that are difficult to
   manage without introducing rests mid-phrase. The simultaneous entry at
   bar 3 is mitigated by the SSAA-only opening in bars 1–2.
