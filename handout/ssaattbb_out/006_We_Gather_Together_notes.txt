ARRANGER NOTES — We Gather Together (Kremser)
SSAATTBB arrangement for lever harp accompaniment
=======================================================

TRANSPOSITION
-------------
Original key:  D major (2 sharps: F#, C#)
Chosen key:    D major (2 sharps) — no transposition required
Semitones shifted: 0
Direction: none

D major is a valid lever harp key within the 3-flat / 4-sharp limit.
Lever positions required (Eb storage tuning, levers raised = sharpen):
  F lever raised  → F# (scale degree 3)
  C lever raised  → C# (scale degree 7)
All other levers remain down (Eb storage). The harp plays the full
D major scale without mid-piece lever changes.

Range verification after key choice:
  S1 peak: E5 (M6 beat 2) — comfortable; no issue
  B1 low:  A2 (M8 beat 3, A,,) — see Range Log below
No range reason to choose a different key.


HARMONIC SUBSTITUTIONS
-----------------------
The original harmony contains one tonicization of E major (V/vi, the
dominant of B minor), which appears in phrase 2 bars 7–8. The G# in both
the original alto and original tenor voices implies an E major chord
(V/vi). Under the diatonic constraint, E major cannot be played by the
harp, so it is replaced throughout the inner voices:

  Phrase 2, M7 (bar 7 of piece):
    Original harmony: E major (A–C#–E–G#)
    Replacement:      E minor (A–B–E–G) — diatonic iii chord in D major
    Rationale: nearest diatonic chord by root motion; E minor substitutes
               for E major preserving root and fifth, changing third G#→G.
    Effect: softer color at the half-cadence; the S1 soprano does NOT
            pass through G# (it has B4 at this beat) so there is no
            chromatic clash. The C5 (C natural) passing tone that S1
            sings in M6 beat 3 is a modal-mixture color held only in S1
            as permitted; inner voices already have A and E (Am implied),
            consistent with the diatonic field.

No other harmonic substitutions were required. The dominant A major
chord (V in D) appearing at phrase endings (M4, M8, M12) contains only
diatonic notes (A, C#, E) and requires no alteration.

TEXTURE PLAN
------------
The arrangement opens with all eight voices entering together on the
pickup to phrase 1 but in a contained mid-range dynamic (mezzo-forte),
keeping the outer voices (S1 and B1) well within their comfortable
registers to hold back the full spread for later. Phrase 2 opens the
range upward as S1 rises to E5 and B1 descends toward A2, building
toward a brief climax at the peak of M6 (d–e–c in S1). Phrase 3 is
cast antiphonally: SSAA and TTBB answer one another across M9–M11,
with both choirs sharing the final cadence of phrase 3 (M12) together.
Phrase 4 gathers all eight voices in full homophony, expanding to the
widest spread at M13–M14 before the stretto-style suspension approach
(S2, A1, A2 holding a 4–3 suspension pattern) into the final D major
chord.

Texture types used:
  1. Full 8-voice homophony — phrase 1 (all voices together, block-chord
     style with independent inner-voice passing motion)
  2. Antiphonal exchange (SSAA vs TTBB) — phrase 3, M9–M11: SSAA
     presents the rising line while TTBB mirrors it a tenth below;
     both choirs converge at M12.
  3. Call and response between outer voices — phrase 2, S1 leading
     with the melodic peak (E5 at M6) while B1 provides the bass
     foundation and inner voices provide sustained harmonic color.
  4. Stretto / suspension approach — phrase 4 final cadence (M15–M16):
     A1 and A2 hold neighbor-tone suspensions (C–A and A–F#) resolving
     stepwise into the final D major chord, while Bar and B1 cadence
     on F#3/D3 (third and root).

RULE COMPROMISES
----------------
1. Baritone interval above B1 — M2 beat 1 (phrase 1):
   Rule: Bar harmonizes a third above B1.
   Compromise: Bar M2 beat 1 uses A3 (a fifth above B1=D3) instead of
     the strict diatonic third (F#3).
   Reason: If Bar=F#3 at this beat, it creates a T2/Bar unison since
     T2 concert = F#3 at the same beat. The rule that T voices and
     B voices may not share a unison (Step 5) takes priority.

2. Baritone interval above B1 — M3 beats 1–2 (phrase 1):
   Rule: Bar harmonizes a third above B1.
   Compromise: Bar M3 beats 1–2 use A3 (a fourth above B1=E3) instead
     of the strict diatonic third (G3).
   Reason: If Bar=G3, it creates a T2/Bar unison since T2 concert = G3
     at those beats. Same priority as above.

3. Baritone interval above B1 — M3 beat 3 (phrase 1), M11 beats 3–4
   (phrase 3), phrase 3 pickup:
   Rule: Bar harmonizes a third above B1.
   Compromise: Bar uses B3 (a second above B1=A3) at these points
     instead of the diatonic third C#4.
   Reason: C#4 exceeds the Baritone's professional maximum of C4
     (natural). B3 is the only diatonic note above A3 that remains
     within range.

4. Opening texture — all 8 voices from pickup:
   Rule (Step 7): Open with reduced texture (4–6 voices) and build to
     full 8.
   Compromise: All 8 voices enter on the pickup of phrase 1.
   Reason: This is a congregational hymn with a strong, confident
     opening. The harmonic rhythm and texture of "Kremser" benefit from
     full-choir presence immediately. The dynamic arc is created instead
     through range (voices stay in mid-register for phrases 1–2) and
     through antiphonal contrasts in phrase 3. Conductors may choose
     to have TTBB enter one beat late on the pickup for a gentle
     introduction if the rule is preferred.

5. S1 C-natural in M6 beat 3 and M11 beat 3:
   Observation (not a true rule compromise): The original soprano sings
     C natural (not C#) in these bars. The prompt permits S1 to sing
     any accidental freely. These C naturals are intentional modal color
     (flattened seventh in D major) and are preserved in S1 exactly as
     written. The harp will sustain C# strings; the C natural in S1
     is an effective dissonance resolved immediately. No other voice
     doubles the C natural.
