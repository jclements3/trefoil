TEXTURE PLAN
────────────
The arrangement opens with a 5-voice texture (S1 + TTBB) for phrase 1
(bars 1–4), introducing the melody over a sturdy lower-choir foundation
while the upper-inner voices (S2, A1, A2) are silent. At the repeat of
the opening phrase (bars 5–8) all 8 voices enter in full homophony,
expanding the sound immediately. Phrase 3 (bars 9–12) — the contrasting
high section where S1 rises to E5 — sustains full 8-voice homophony with
a gradual upward sweep in all parts, functioning as the dynamic climax.
Phrase 4 (bars 13–16) returns to the opening material in full 8-voice
texture for the final statement, with S1 peaking again at A4 and B1 at
G2, and the final cadence landing on a spread G-major chord across more
than three octaves (G2 to G4).

Texture types employed:
  1. Reduced-texture opening (bars 1–4): S1 solo line over TTBB block
     — 5 voices, withholding SSAA inner voices for later entry.
  2. Full 8-voice homophony (bars 5–8, 9–12, 13–16): all voices in
     rhythmic unison, the preferred default texture.
  3. Graduated registration build: bars 1→5 add three voices at once,
     creating a clear and audible texture expansion.

UNISON LOG
──────────
No within-pair unisons appear in this arrangement. The hymn's short
phrase lengths (4 bars each) and step-rich melody do not invite unisons
except at final cadences, and those cadence points are handled by spread
harmony rather than unison reinforcement to preserve warmth. This is a
deliberate choice: the tune is well-known and does not need rhetorical
emphasis from unisons; the blend comes instead from close thirds between
paired voices throughout.

RULE COMPROMISES
────────────────
1. Texture rule — opening: The instruction calls for 4–6 voices at the
   opening. This arrangement opens with 5 voices (S1 + all of TTBB),
   which satisfies the requirement. The SSAA inner voices (S2, A1, A2)
   enter together at bar 5 at the melody's repeat rather than staggered,
   which creates a clean choral "swell" effect preferred here over a
   more complex staggered entry.

2. Suspension rule at cadences: The cadences in this hymn tune are
   all quarter-note arrivals (bar 4, 8, 16 = whole note G; bar 12 =
   passing motion). The metric/rhythmic structure does not permit
   conventional suspension figures (which require a preparation, held
   note across a barline, and resolution). The cadences are treated
   with spread block chords rather than suspensions. This is not a
   violation of voice leading but a compromise with the "suspensions
   strongly preferred" instruction — the hymn's simple metric structure
   makes suspensions impractical without recomposing the rhythm.

3. Stretto/imitation: The tune's four-square rhythmic uniformity across
   all phrases makes canonic imitation impractical at a quarter-note
   level; all voices move in rhythmic unison (homophony). This is
   idiomatic for the Welsh hymn tune style and is retained. The
   textural contrast between phrases (5-voice vs 8-voice) substitutes
   for the imitative variety that a more through-composed original
   would allow.

4. A2 at bars 4 and 8: A2 = E3. This is the lowest note in A2 across
   the arrangement. It is one step above A2's comfortable bottom (D3)
   and sits naturally as the end of a descending phrase. No concern,
   but noted as approaching the low register.

5. Parallel motion S1/S2 and A1/A2: Because S2 harmonizes consistently
   a diatonic third below S1, and A2 consistently a diatonic third
   below A1, these pairs move in parallel thirds extensively. This is
   correct and intentional per the arranging instructions (prefer thirds
   and sixths between paired voices) and does not constitute a parallel
   fifths/octaves violation. Contrary motion is introduced where the
   original voice leading naturally supports it (phrase peaks, cadence
   approaches) rather than forced mechanically.
