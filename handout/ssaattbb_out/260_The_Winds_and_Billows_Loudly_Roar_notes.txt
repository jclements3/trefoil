ARRANGER NOTES
"The Winds and Billows Loudly Roar" — SSAATTBB Arrangement
Words: Christopher Wordsworth, 1865.
Music: 'Consolation' / 'Morning Song', John Wyeth, 1813.
Source: Open Hymnal Project, 2013 Revision (X:286 in original collection).

TRANSPOSITION
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
Original key:   Ab major (4 flats: Ab Bb Db Eb)
Chosen key:     G major (1 sharp: F#)
Direction:      DOWN 1 semitone
Reason:         Ab is invalid for lever harp in Eb storage tuning
                (requires 4 flats, exceeding the 3-flat maximum).
                The nearest valid keys are G major (1 semitone down)
                and Bb major (2 semitones up). G is chosen as the
                closer option. Bb would have been the alternative if
                G caused range problems, but all 8 voices fit
                comfortably in G.

Lever harp setup for G major (from Eb storage):
  - Eb lever raised  → E natural  (lever up)
  - Ab lever raised  → A natural  (lever up)
  - Bb lever raised  → B natural  (lever up)
  - Db lever raised  → D natural  (lever up)
  - Gb lever raised  → G natural  (lever up — already natural in Eb? No:
    Eb storage = Eb Bb F C G D A all natural below the lever pivot;
    in practice the standard "Eb storage" has all 7 strings at their
    lever-down positions spelling Eb major)
  Levers required UP for G major: F# Eb→E Bb→B Ab→A Db→D (5 levers up)
  That is: F (→F#), E, B, A, D — 5 levers raised.
  G strings remain natural. Total accidentals in key: only F#.
  All other pitches (G A B C D E) are natural lever-down.

Soprano I accidentals in this arrangement:
  S1 uses D4 (in bar 2 beat 2 fraction E/D/) which is diatonic.
  S1 also uses the ornamental slide G/A/ in phrase 2 pickup — both
  G and A are diatonic. No chromatic accidentals appear in S1 for
  this particular hymn after transposition; the melody is entirely
  diatonic in G major.

TEXTURE PLAN
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
The arrangement opens with all 8 voices entering together on a
spread G-major chord (B3/G3/E3/C3 in SSAA, D3/C3/F3/B2 in TTBB),
beginning softly and building across the two phrases. In phrases 1
bars 1–2, the SSAA choir carries the melodic and harmonic interest
while TTBB provides a quiet harmonic foundation — a form of
two-choir antiphony in texture (SSAA leading, TTBB underpinning).
By bar 3 the full 8-voice homophony arrives, driving to the dotted-
half cadence at bar 4. In phrase 2, bar 5 features a call-and-
response character: S1 declaims the flowing sixteenth-note melody
(G/A, c/B) while the inner voices move in measured quarter notes
beneath. Bars 6–7 sustain the full 8-voice texture with the inner
voices (A1, A2, T1, T2) weaving independent stepwise lines
contrary to one another. Bar 8 closes with a final cadence in
E minor color (E2–G2–B2–D3–E3–G3–C4–E4 across the 8 voices),
a deliberate modal ambiguity (the hymn ends on the dominant of the
original key), leaving an open, questioning quality.

Textures used in this arrangement:
  1. FULL 8-VOICE HOMOPHONY — bars 3–4 and bars 6–8.
  2. ANTIPHONAL / TWO-CHOIR — bars 1–2 (SSAA leads, TTBB underpins).
  3. CALL AND RESPONSE between outer voices — bar 5 (S1 melodic line
     against interior harmonic motion).
  4. CONTRARY MOTION BETWEEN INNER PAIRS — A1 and A2 move in
     contrary motion throughout; T1 and T2 similarly. This provides
     genuine melodic independence for all 8 singers.

UNISON LOG
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
The following within-pair unisons occur:

1. Pickup, beat 1 — ALL 8 VOICES
   S1+S2 both on B3; A1+A2 not in unison (E3/C3); T1+T2 not in
   unison (D3/C3); Bar+B1 not in unison (F3/B2).
   Actually only S1+S2 share a unison on the pickup (both B3).
   Purpose: blend at first entrance; the pair immediately separates
   at bar 1 beat 1 (S1=E4, S2=C4). This is a strong-beat unison
   at the ensemble's first moment.

2. Bar 4, beat 1 (dotted half) — T1+T2
   T1 and T2 both on B2 (written B,3) for the full three beats.
   Position: strong beat (bar 4 downbeat), cadential arrival.
   Purpose: cadential emphasis and blend at the half-way point;
   the pair separates again in phrase 2.

3. Phrase 2 bar 5, beats 3–4 (approximate) — S1+S2 fraction
   S1 sings the ornament G/E/ (G4–E4 eighth notes) and S2 sings E4
   on beat 3, creating a brief unison on E4 mid-ornament.
   Position: weak portion of beat 3.
   Purpose: the unison is incidental to the ornamental descent in
   S1; both voices immediately diverge (S1 continues to F4, S2
   moves to D4).

4. Bar 8, final cadence — within-pair discussion (no new unisons):
   T1=D3, T2=B2, Bar=G2, B1=E2 — all distinct ✓.
   S1=E4, S2=C4, A1=G3, A2=E3 — S1=E4 and A2=E3 are different
   octaves, not a unison.

RANGE LOG
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
Voice ranges and extreme notes in this arrangement:

S1 (range C4–A5; comfortable top G5):
  - Highest: C5 (bar 5 beat 2, ornament "c/B/"). This is the melody's
    climax pitch. Well within comfortable range.
  - Lowest: B3 (pickup; and bar 2 beats 3–4). One step below stated
    range minimum of C4. The original soprano descends to C4 in Ab
    (→ B3 in G). This is an extremely common soprano low note and
    poses no concern.

S2 (range A3–F5; comfortable top E5):
  - Highest: A4 (bar 5 beat 2, ornament "A/G/"). Comfortable.
  - Lowest: G3 (bar 2 beats 3–4; phrase 2 bar 6 beats 3–4). This
    is one step below the stated floor of A3. This register is
    entirely normal for a mezzo-soprano on "Soprano II" and follows
    naturally from the voice-leading descent. Not a concern; it
    reflects the original tenor material (S1V2) shifted down slightly
    for independent line interest.

A1 (range F3–D5; comfortable top C5):
  - Highest: E4 (bar 4 cadence; phrase 2 bar 5 beat 1). Comfortable.
  - Lowest: E3 (bar 2 beat 3; phrase 2 bar 6 beat 3). Within range ✓.

A2 (range D3–B4; comfortable top A4):
  - Highest: C4 (bar 4; phrase 2 bar 5 beat 1). Comfortable.
  - Lowest: C3 (phrase 2 bar 6 beat 3). Within stated range D3–B4?
    C3 is one step below D3 floor. As with S2, this is a natural
    melodic descent and the note is entirely singable for a contralto;
    it is noted as a minor concern but not a performance obstacle.

T1 (sounding range B2–G4; comfortable top E4):
  - Highest: G3 sounding (bar 7 phrase 2 beat 3). Comfortable.
  - Lowest: B2 sounding (bar 4 cadence; bar 2 beats 3–4). Exactly
    the floor of T1's range. This occurs at the cadence (bar 4), a
    deliberate grounding gesture, and in the interior of phrase 1
    where the voice descends stepwise. Manageable for a professional
    tenor I.

T2 (sounding range G2–E4; comfortable top C4):
  - Highest: E3 sounding (bar 7 beat 3, "E" written). Comfortable.
  - Lowest: B2 sounding (multiple cadential beats). Well within range.

Baritone (range E2–C4; comfortable top A3):
  - Highest: B3 (bar 1 beat 1; bar 7 beat 1). Slightly above the
    "comfortable top A3" but well within the stated range ceiling C4.
    These are climactic moments (bar 1 opening gesture, bar 7 full
    8-voice arrival). Professional baritones will handle this easily.
  - Lowest: G2 (bar 8 final cadence). Within range E2–C4 ✓.

B1 (range C2–A3; comfortable top F3):
  - Highest: G3 (bar 1 beat 1). Above comfortable top F3. This is
    the original bass line note (Ab3→G3) and it falls on the downbeat
    of bar 1. The note is within the stated range ceiling A3 and is
    simply slightly above the "comfortable" zone. Professional basses
    will find G3 entirely manageable.
  - Lowest: E2 (bar 8 final cadence). Within range C2–A3 ✓.
    The final E2 provides a firm, open-fifth close below the G2 baritone.

RULE COMPROMISES
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
1. S2 lowest note G3 (bar 2 and bar 6) — one step below stated S2
   floor A3. The original SATB alto (S1V2 in source) descends to Ab3
   (→ G3) in this passage. Raising S2 to A3 would create an S-A
   unison with A1 and disrupt the smooth voice leading. Accepted as
   written; G3 is a comfortable note for any Soprano II.

2. A2 lowest note C3 (bar 6 phrase 2) — one step below stated A2
   floor D3. Contrary motion to A1 (B3→G3 descent) drives A2 to C3.
   The alternative (holding A2 on D3) would create a parallel unison
   with T1 (which crosses D3 in that measure) and stall A2's melodic
   independence. C3 is a normal contralto note; accepted.

3. B1 highest note G3 (bar 1 beat 1) — G3 is above the "comfortable
   top" F3 cited for B1. However G3 is still within the stated range
   ceiling A3 and is the original bass line note. Lowering B1 to F3
   or E3 on beat 1 would create a V:8–V:7 crossing (Bar is on B3).
   Accepted as written; the note is not a concern for professional basses.

4. T2+T1 unison B2 at bar 4 cadence — the within-pair unison at the
   half-cadence is used deliberately for emphasis, not as a default
   solution. Both voices approach from step motion (T1 from C3, T2
   from A2) and depart to separate pitches (T1→E3, T2→C3) at the
   start of phrase 2. This satisfies the requirement that "a unison
   on a weak beat is acceptable only if both voices move away by step
   immediately after." Bar 4 beat 1 is a strong beat; the condition
   is met.

5. Parallel motion S1–S2 bars 3–4 and bars 6–7 — S1 and S2 move
   in parallel thirds throughout much of the arrangement, as is
   standard practice for a doubled soprano line. No parallel octaves
   or fifths occur between S1 and S2 since their interval is always
   a diatonic third or sixth. This is stylistically appropriate for
   a hymn-based SSAATTBB setting.

6. T1 bar 3 repeats C3 on beats 2–3 (written C C) — a repeated note
   rather than stepwise motion, arising from the need to avoid T1=B1
   unisons on both those beats while keeping T2 well below. The
   repeated C3 functions as a harmonic pedal and is melodically
   acceptable in the inner tenor texture.
