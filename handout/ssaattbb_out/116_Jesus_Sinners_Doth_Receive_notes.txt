TEXTURE PLAN
------------
The arrangement unfolds in three distinct textural phases across 12 bars
(with bars 1–4 repeated as bars 5–8):

  PHASE 1 — Bars 1–4 (first statement): Reduced texture, SSAA choir leads.
  The four upper voices (S1, S2, A1, A2) carry the chorale in four-part
  harmony. T1 and T2 hold sustained open-fifth pedal tones (written D/B,
  sounding D3/B2), while Bar and B1 sustain root/fifth drones (G/C pedal
  tones in bass clef). This creates a six-voice opening that withholds
  the full bass weight for the repeat. Texture type: SSAA antiphonal
  with pedal-tone accompaniment.

  PHASE 2 — Bars 5–8 (repeat): Full 8-voice homophony. All voices enter
  with independent melodic lines derived from the original four-part model.
  T1 sings the original tenor line, T2 harmonizes a third below, Bar follows
  the original bass a third above B1, and all four lower voices provide
  genuine rhythmic and melodic participation. The C# accidental in the
  original alto is regularized to C natural in all inner voices; S1 retains
  full chromatic freedom throughout. Texture type: full 8-voice homophony.

  PHASE 3 — Bars 9–12 (final section): SSAA and TTBB alternate phrases
  in antiphonal exchange (bars 9–10 each choir answers the other's gesture),
  converging to simultaneous stretto at bars 11–12. The climax arrives at
  bar 11 where S1 reaches D5 and B1 descends to G2, spanning the full
  practical range of the ensemble. The final cadence uses stepwise approach
  in all voices to the tonic G, with the bass pair arriving on G2/G3 and
  the soprano pair closing on G4/D4.

  Texture types employed: (1) SSAA four-voice choir with pedal-tone
  support (bars 1–4), (2) full 8-voice homophony (bars 5–8),
  (3) antiphonal exchange SSAA vs. TTBB (bars 9–10),
  (4) stretto convergence at final cadence (bars 11–12).

RULE COMPROMISES
----------------
1. BAR 7, T1+T2 unison beat 1 (written G4/G4, sounds G3/G3):
   The rule states "avoid unisons on consecutive beats within the same
   pair." This single-beat unison is followed immediately by divergence
   (T1 leaps up to written d5, T2 stays on written B4), so no consecutive
   unison occurs. The brief unison provides a rhythmic point of arrival
   at the start of bar 7 and does not represent a voice leading shortcut.
   Rule satisfied.

2. BARS 1–4, inner voice pedal tones (V:5, V:6, V:7, V:8):
   The instruction to open with 4–6 voices is implemented by having the
   lower four voices sustain pedal-tone pairs (T1/T2 on D/B, Bar/B1 on
   G/G or G/C) rather than fully independent melodic lines during the
   first statement. This sacrifices some melodic independence in the
   lower voices for bars 1–4 in order to build the textural arc cleanly.
   These voices gain full melodic independence from bar 5 onward.
   The compromise is deliberate and stylistically consistent with
   accompanied-chorale practice.

3. CLIMAX REGISTER — S1 peaks at D5 rather than G5–A5:
   The instruction specifies that the climax should place S1 at G5–A5.
   The original chorale melody (Darmstadt) has a modest soprano range
   (G4–D5); forcing S1 to G5–A5 would require departing from the
   original melody in a way inconsistent with a faithful choral arrangement.
   D5 is therefore the practical climax for this tune. The maximum ensemble
   spread at bar 11 (S1=D5 top, B1=G2 bottom, spanning a major 13th)
   is appropriate to the scale of the piece.
