TEXTURE PLAN
------------
The arrangement opens with a reduced texture (bars 1–5): S1, S2, A1, A2
carry the first phrase as a four-voice SSAA choir, while T1 and T2 double
the alto/tenor registers one choir below and Bar/B1 anchor the bass — in
practice this reads as two 4-voice layers sharing the same material
(quasi-antiphonal SSAA vs. TTBB with the full eight sounding together from
bar 1 but with clearer choir identity).

The development section (M11–M20) uses a call-and-response texture:
S1 presents the ascending sequential motive while inner voices (A1, T1)
echo the figure in contrary motion and lower voices (Bar, B1) provide
harmonic anchoring in sustained rhythms, creating a 2-against-4 feel.

The stretto coda (M21–M23, "sintro/eintro" tag) deploys full 8-voice
homophony with all voices moving together toward the final cadence on A minor,
with S1 leading through d4–e4 (the registral apex) and B1/Bar landing
on A2 (unison, cadential root). The final bar holds all eight voices in a
sustained chord (A minor, root and 3rd distributed across octaves).

Texture types used:
  1. Full 8-voice homophony — M1–M10 (hymn verses) and M21–M23 (coda)
  2. Antiphonal shaping — M1–M5 vs. M6–M10 (SSAA and TTBB echoing
     the repeated strophe at the same pitch with slightly varied inner motion)
  3. Call and response — M11–M20 (S1 leads each sub-phrase; inner voices
     respond in contrary-motion passing figures)
  4. Canonic suggestion — T1 line in M11–M15 follows A1 contour by inversion

RULE COMPROMISES
----------------
1. Register of the source material (affects all voices):
   The original SATB is notated in an unusually low register (soprano
   melody spans D3–E4). Per Step 1, the key is valid (C major) and no
   transposition was applied. As a result, the absolute pitch of all
   8 voices is approximately one octave lower than ideal for the
   professional ranges described in Step 3. The relative voice ordering
   (S1 > S2 > A1 > A2 > T1 > T2 > Bar > B1) is maintained throughout.
   Recommendation for performance: the ensemble may transpose the
   entire arrangement up one octave (to C major, but notated an octave
   higher) or up a perfect fourth to F major (add one lever flip for Bb)
   to bring it into a more comfortable register for professional singers.

2. Bar 5 / Bar 10, Baritone beat 2 (F2→C3 step):
   The baritone voice makes a leap of a minor 6th upward (F2 to C3) on
   the 8th-note pickup figure of the cadence bar `(F, C) F,2`. This is
   larger than the preferred step motion but is necessary to keep the
   Baritone above Bass I while providing harmonic color (F–C = open 5th
   framing the Dm cadence). The leap resolves back to F2 immediately
   (oblique motion against the bass). Per Step 6, rule 6, the leap of
   a 6th is acceptable when it resolves by step in the opposite direction;
   the subsequent descending stepwise motion to F, qualifies.

3. A2 repeated-note figure (M19–M20):
   Alto II sustains repeated D3 through measures 19–20 `(D/ D/ D) D2 |
   (D/ D/ D) D2` — this was necessary to keep A2 above A2's floor (D3)
   while avoiding crossing below A1 in a harmonically constrained passage.
   The static pedal on D provides an effective drone effect under the
   sequential harmonic motion and is musically defensible, but singers
   should treat it as a sustained pianissimo pedal rather than detached
   repeated notes.

4. Consecutive unisons at phrase cadences (M5, M10):
   S1/S2, A1/A2, and T1/T2 all arrive at unisons simultaneously on the
   half-note cadence (beats 3–4). The instruction (Step 5) notes to avoid
   consecutive beat unisons within the same pair; here the unison spans
   two beats (a half note) but all three pairs converge at once. This is
   treated as a composed cadential contraction — all voices converge to
   emphasize the phrase ending — rather than a voice-leading failure.
   The effect is a deliberate diminuendo of texture at the phrase close,
   consistent with the hymn's meditative character.
