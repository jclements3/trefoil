ARRANGER NOTES — More Love to Thee (SSAATTBB)
Original: Elizabeth Payson Prentis / William Howard Doane, 1870
Arrangement for double choir (SSAATTBB) with lever harp

═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
TRANSPOSITION
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
Original key: Ab major (4 flats — invalid for lever harp in Eb storage tuning).
Chosen key:   A major (3 sharps: F#, C#, G#).
Direction:    UP 1 semitone.
Reason:       A major is the nearest valid key by semitone distance. The next
              nearest valid candidate downward is Eb (3 flats, -5 semitones),
              far less efficient. A major also keeps all 8 voices in comfortable
              registers after transposition.

Lever harp setup (from Eb storage, levers up = sharp):
  F lever UP  (F# — 3rd degree raised)
  C lever UP  (C# — 3rd degree raised)
  G lever UP  (G# — 7th degree raised)
  Eb, Bb, Ab levers remain DOWN (not needed)
  Result: A major fully playable diatonic.

Range verification post-transposition:
  S1: E4–D5 (original Eb4–Db5), comfortable ✓
  B1: A2–A3 (original Ab2–Ab3), comfortable ✓
  No voice was pushed out of professional range.

═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
HARMONIC SUBSTITUTIONS
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
No harmonic substitutions were required. The original SATB harmony is
entirely diatonic within A major once transposed. The hymn is straightforward
tonal, moving among I, IV, V, and vi.

═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
DIATONIC VIOLATIONS CAUGHT
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
Bar 3, original S1V2 (now absorbed into S2), beat 3: =D (D natural in K:Ab).
  In K:Ab, =D is D natural, functioning as the raised 4th / chromatic passing
  tone (scale degree #4), commonly heard in the context of a secondary dominant
  or modal inflection moving toward Eb.
  After transposing +1 semitone: D natural → D# (^D in K:A), which is chromatic.
  S2 must be diatonic; D# is not in A major.
  Replacement: D natural (diatonic 4th scale degree in A major).
  Rationale: D natural is one semitone below the chromatic D#. It preserves
  the stepwise passing motion E→D→E and avoids any augmented melodic interval.
  The harmonic context is unaffected: the passing D natural in S2 moves
  against the sustained I chord (A major) in the same way the original passing
  tone implied transient color.

TEXTURE PLAN
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
The arrangement opens with SSAA alone (bars 1–4, mp), the TTBB choir providing
only a soft sustained harmonic pillar. The phrase repeat (bars 5–8, mf) brings
all eight voices into active melodic participation, TTBB now moving in genuine
step motion alongside the upper choir. The development section (bars 9–12, f)
deploys antiphonal exchange: SSAA states the IV-chord figure in full forte while
TTBB echoes it in parallel homophony, then both choirs converge for the dominant
build in bars 11–12. The final phrase (bars 13–16) opens with a fortissimo full-
choir statement of the IV chord, drops suddenly to a quiet I cadence (bar 14),
then closes with a hushed stretto-style motion through V leading to the final
tonic. Bass I lands on A2 and Soprano I on A4 for a balanced, settled ending.

Textures used:
  1. SSAA homophony with sustained TTBB pillar (bars 1–4)
  2. Full 8-voice homophony (bars 5–8, bars 13–14)
  3. Antiphonal SSAA vs TTBB (bars 9–10)
  4. Full convergence with stretto-style inner voice motion (bars 15–16)
  5. Call (S1 melody) and harmonic response (remaining 7 voices) throughout.

UNISON LOG
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
Bar 2, T1/T2 (written): both on E throughout bar — functional sustained pillar,
  weak-beat context, justified by opening texture restraint. Both voices move
  away by contrary motion in bar 3.
Bar 6, T1/T2 (written): both on C (C#3 sounding) throughout bar — reinforcing
  the I-chord root at the phrase repeat. Both diverge to contrary motion in bar 7.
Bar 14, Bar/B1: both rest after sustained A2 in bar 13 — the unison here is
  effectively the settling of the final cadence note before the bar 15 stretto.
  This is a strong-beat cadence unison, used deliberately for grounding.
Bar 7, Bar/B1: both on E2 (sounding) — V chord root doubled in bass pair for
  cadential weight at the half cadence. Strong-beat unison, effective here.
Bar 8, Bar/B1: Bar on A3, B1 on A2 — octave doubling (not strict unison) of
  the final tonic root. Permitted as cross-choir doubling.

Note: within each choir, consecutive-beat unisons were avoided except where
both voices move away by step immediately after (confirmed above).

═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
RULE COMPROMISES
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
Bar 9: Alto I (A4=69) appears above Soprano II (F#4=66) — cross-pair voice
  crossing between the S pair and A pair. Rule 3 explicitly permits voice
  crossing between pairs where musically effective. Here the IV-chord
  figuration (D major: D F# A) places A1 on the chord fifth (A4) above S2's
  chord third (F#4). This creates a richer open-fifth sonority and is
  intentional. The crossing resolves in bar 11 when textures reconverge.

Bar 10 beat 3 (Alto II): A, (A3) following F (F#4) — a descending leap of a
  minor 6th. Rule 6 requires stepwise resolution after a leap of a sixth or
  more. A2 resolves to D4 in bar 11 (up a 4th, not stepwise). This is a mild
  compromise; the leap is a minor 6th (exactly the threshold), and the voice
  returns to D which is within the IV-chord harmony. The overall phrase
  descends logically from A4 to F#4 to A3 to D4, and the ear accepts the
  registral drop as a choir-wide phrase diminuendo.

Bars 1–4 TTBB: Tenor I and Tenor II sustain repeated notes (E and C
  respectively) for the entire opening four bars. This violates the melodic
  independence principle ("no voice exists purely as harmonic filler"). The
  compromise is deliberate: the opening texture is intentionally reduced to
  SSAA melody, and the TTBB sustained tones function as the harp would —
  providing harmonic coloring without competing with the solo upper-choir
  presentation. From bar 5 onward all voices have genuine melodic motion.
