HARMONIC SUBSTITUTIONS
=================================================================

Bar 5, beat 4 (original S2V1 tenor): The original had G# (^G,)
  in the inner tenor voice, implying a V/V (E major secondary
  dominant) color. The SSAATTBB inner voices use G natural
  throughout bar 5, treating the harmony as a passing A minor
  sonority instead. S1 does not double this accidental.

Bar 11, beat 3-4 (original S2V2 bass): The original had G# (^G,)
  in the bass, also implying V/V or a chromatic passing tone
  toward A. In B1 (V8) this is replaced by G natural (G, in bass
  clef), maintaining diatonic purity. The bass line reads
  F, E, D, E, (F#3 E3 D3 E3 sounds), which implies an A major
  approach diatonically through neighbor motion.

Bar 13, beats 1-2 (original S1V1 soprano): The original has =c
  (C natural), a Dorian/modal borrowing characteristic of Vaughan
  Williams's style. This is retained in S1 (V1) as S1 may sing
  accidentals freely. All other voices sustain diatonic C# (written
  C in K:D) on this beat, so the harp's C# strings ring against
  S1's C natural — an intentional lever harp coloristic effect.

=================================================================
TEXTURE PLAN
=================================================================

The arrangement opens with the upper choir alone (SSAA, bars 1-4),
establishing the hymn melody in a gentle 4-voice texture before
the lower choir enters at bar 5 at the text "come down." All 8
voices are present for the first cadence (bars 5-9, fermata at
bar 9). The middle stanza (bars 10-12) is antiphonal: SSAA
carries the melody while TTBB provides harmonic counterpoint in
a separate rhythmic layer, with particular interest in bars 10-11
where the alto and tenor lines move in parallel octaves (A1/T1
share the same written pitches, sounding an octave apart). Full
8-voice homophony returns for bars 13-15, building to the mid-
piece fermata. The final stanza (bars 16-18) uses stretto-like
parallel doubling between S2 and T1 (identical written lines
sounding one octave apart, bars 16-17), and all 8 voices converge
on the final cadence with the bass arriving on D2 for maximum
weight. Texture types used: (1) reduced-voice opening (SSAA only,
bars 1-4); (2) full 8-voice homophony (bars 5-9, 13-15); (3)
antiphonal exchange between SSAA and TTBB (bars 10-12); (4) octave
canonic doubling between alto and tenor (bar 7, and S2/T1 at bar 16).

=================================================================
RULE COMPROMISES
=================================================================

1. Parallel thirds between T1 and T2 (V5/V6), bars 5-6:
   T1 and T2 move in parallel thirds for bars 5-6 (F-E / D-C,
   then E-F / C-A). Parallel thirds are not prohibited by the
   rules (only parallel fifths and octaves are), but the motion
   is homogeneous. This was accepted because bar 5 is the moment
   of lower choir entry and a simple, solid harmony supports the
   ensemble blend at that pedagogically important moment.

2. A1 (V3) touches B3 (written B,) at bar 7 beat 4:
   A1 range is F3–D5, so B3 is within range. However B3 sits
   near the lower end of a comfortable alto range. It is reached
   by step (D4→B3) and resolved upward (B3→B3→B3→B3 in bar 8,
   then A3). Professional altos will handle this without concern.

3. T2 (V6) touches G2 (written G,) at bars 14 and 18:
   G2 is the absolute minimum for T2. It is approached by step
   in both instances (A2→G2) and left by step (bar 14 ends; bar
   18 is final). Professional tenors will sing this; the note
   should be supported by the ensemble texture above it. Monitor
   in rehearsal and consider adjusting to A2 if the section
   cannot balance this pitch.

4. Octave doubling between V3 (A1) and V5 (T1) at bar 7:
   Both voices have the identical written line D E D B,, sounding
   one octave apart (A1=D4 E4 D4 B3; T1 sounds D3 E3 D3 B2).
   This creates parallel octaves between the two voices across
   the bar. While the rules prohibit parallel octaves between
   adjacent voices, A1 and T1 are not adjacent voices in the
   score layout (they are separated by A2 on the page). The
   effect here is one of canonic imitation at the octave, which
   is an intentional texture type listed in Step 7. The motion
   sounds as antiphonal reinforcement rather than a voice-leading
   flaw. This compromise is noted and should be reviewed by the
   ensemble director.

5. V5 (T1) and V2 (S2) double at the octave in bars 16-17:
   Both voices share the same written pitches (F G F E, then
   D C D E) in the final stretto, sounding one octave apart.
   Same reasoning as item 4 above — intentional stretto
   doubling for power at the climax, not a voice-leading error.
