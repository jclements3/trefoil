ARRANGER NOTES
Gentle Mary Laid Her Child
SSAATTBB arrangement — Tempus Adest Floridum / Joseph S. Cook (1919)
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

TRANSPOSITION
─────────────
Original key:  A major (3 sharps: F#, C# G#)
Chosen key:    A major — no transposition required.
Reason:        A major is within the valid range (≤4 sharps) for lever harp in Eb
               storage tuning. All 7 diatonic pitches of A major are produced by
               raising the Ab, Eb, Bb, F, C, G, and D levers.
Lever settings on Eb-storage harp:
  Ab lever → A natural
  Eb lever → E natural
  Bb lever → B natural
  F  lever → F#
  C  lever → C#
  G  lever → G#
  D  lever → D natural
All 7 diatonic notes (A B C# D E F# G#) are available. No mid-piece lever
changes are required; the harp plays the entire piece without adjustment.


HARMONIC SUBSTITUTIONS
───────────────────────
None required. The original SATB hymn ('Tempus Adest Floridum' in A major)
is fully diatonic. No secondary dominants, raised leading tones in inner
voices, or modal mixture appear in the source material. Every chord in the
source is a root-position or first-inversion triad built on diatonic scale
degrees of A major: I (A), IV (D), V (E), and vi (F#m). No harmonic
substitution was necessary.


DIATONIC VIOLATIONS CAUGHT
───────────────────────────
None. The source SATB inner voices (S1V2 = original alto, S2V1 = original
tenor, S2V2 = original bass) contain no accidentals beyond the key
signature. All original pitches are diatonic in A major. All derived voices
in this arrangement (S2, A1, A2, T1, T2, Bar, B1) are therefore fully
diatonic by construction.


TEXTURE PLAN
────────────
The arrangement opens with six voices (SSAA + Baritone + Bass I, bars 1–4),
building a clear arc from intimate to full texture. The second statement of
the A section (bars 5–8) introduces all eight voices in block homophony for
the first full tutti. The contrasting B section (bars 9–12) maintains all
eight voices but differentiates the choirs: the SSAA carry the descending
soprano line (e–d–c–B–A) while the TTBB sustain steady quarter-note chord
tones, creating a call-and-response balance between the two choirs. The
final C section (bars 13–17) builds urgently through an ascending figure in
the upper voices (bars 13–14), reaches climax at bar 15 with S1 on E5 and
B1 on A2 simultaneously (a spread of nearly three octaves), then settles
into a broadly sustained bar 16 with a soprano suspension (d resolved to A),
before the final A major cadence.

Texture types employed:
  1. Full 8-voice homophony (bars 5–8, 13–17)
  2. Antiphonal / stratified texture: SSAA melody vs. TTBB sustained harmony
     (bars 9–12)
  3. Reduced-voice opening for dynamic shape (bars 1–4, 6 voices)
  4. Suspension at final cadence (bar 16, S1 d→A; S2 B→A; A1 F#→E; T1 F#→E)
  5. Converging contrary motion at final bar (outer voices close inward to
     the A-major unison / octave on A)


UNISON LOG
──────────
Bars 1–8 (both statements of section A):
  Bar 4, bar 8 — V7 (Bar) and V8 (B1): both on A2 (A,,) for the
    half-note cadence points (2 half notes each). Strong-beat phrase-end
    unison; grounds the harmonic arrival. Purpose: root doubling, bass
    solidity at phrase close.

Bars 9–12 (section B):
  Bar 12 — V7/V8: both A2, same as bars 4 and 8. Purpose: phrase-end
    grounding.

Bars 13–17 (section C):
  Bar 17 — V5 (T1) and V6 (T2): both on E (written, sounds E3) for the
    final whole note. Rare tenors-in-unison on the final chord, providing
    strength to the V(5th) in the bass register. Strong-beat, final bar;
    purpose: cadential emphasis.
  Bar 17 — V7 (Bar) and V8 (B1): Bar = A3 (A,), B1 = A2 (A,,); NOT a
    unison — they are an octave apart, providing root in two bass
    registers. No unison violation.

Cross-choir unisons (A2 / T1 register break, freely permitted):
  These voices share the same pitch class on multiple beats throughout
  bars 5–8 and 13–17 due to the natural overlap of A2 (C#4 range) and
  T1 (C#3/E3 range). No rule concern; per instructions, A2 and T1 may
  freely share pitches at the register break.

Unisons avoided within SSAA and TTBB pairs per prompt rules:
  S1 / A1 unison would occur at bar 3 beat 1 (original alto F#4 = soprano
    F#4); resolved by dropping A1 to A3–B3 in bars 3, 7, and 11 (see
    Rule Compromises below).
  S2 / A1 unison would occur at bar 3 beat 1 (original alto D4 = S2 D4)
    and beat 3 (D4 = S2 D4); same resolution applies.
  S2 / A1 unison would occur at bar 15 beat 4 (G#4 shared); resolved by
    taking A1 to F#4 and A2 to D4 on that beat.


RANGE LOG
─────────
Bar 15 — V1 (Soprano I): e (E5). This is near the upper limit (C4–A5 range;
  A5 is the absolute maximum). E5 is comfortable and is the climactic high
  point of the piece, placed intentionally on the first beat of the climax
  bar. Not a concern; serves the arc.

Bar 15 — V8 (Bass I): A,, (A2). This is the tonic root, placed at the low
  end of B1's comfortable range (C2–A3). A2 is within range and at the
  phrase climax simultaneously with S1 E5, producing the maximum ensemble
  spread. Not a concern; matches the arranging instruction for the climax.

Bar 3, 7, 11 — V3 (Alto I): A, (A3), B, (B3). Alto I descends to A3 and
  B3 in these bars to avoid unison collisions (see Rule Compromises). A3 is
  above A1's lower limit of F3, so it remains in range. However, it is lower
  than the alto part might naturally sit. Singers should be aware of the dip.

Bars 13–14 — V4 (Alto II): F, (F#3), G, (G#3). Alto II opens bar 13 with
  an ascending line from F#3 — at the low side of A2's range (D3–B4) but
  within spec. Adds a rich tenor-like quality at the start of the final
  section. Not a concern.

Bar 16 — V2 (Soprano II): B (B4). This is the suspended 2nd above the final
  tonic, at the top of S2's comfortable range (A3–F5; B4 is within spec).
  Effective as a suspension note; resolves down to A4 in bar 17. Flagged
  for awareness only.

Bar 17 — V8 (Bass I): A,, (A2). Final root, held for a whole note. Lowest
  note in the piece for B1. Effective final bass sonority; within range.


RULE COMPROMISES
────────────────
1. Alto I (V3) voice crossing below Soprano II (V2), bars 3, 7, 11.
   Rule: "S voices and A voices may NOT share a unison with each other."
   The original alto voice (F#4 E4 D4) collides in unison with both S1
   (F#4, E4) and S2 (D4, C#4) in the bar-3 pattern. To eliminate all
   three unison violations simultaneously, A1 was dropped to A3–B3 for
   these four beats. This places A1 below S2 (D4–E4) on those beats —
   a cross-pair voice crossing — which is explicitly permitted by rule 3
   ("Voice crossing between pairs is permitted where musically effective").
   The A3–B3 pitches are strong chord tones (root and 2nd/passing) and
   create a pleasing temporary thickening of the lower alto texture.

2. Baritone (V7) bar 3 / bar 7 / bar 11 beat 4: original bass = B2 (B,,).
   The Baritone holds B2 (within its E2–C4 range ✓). Bass I (V8) cannot
   go to B1 (below C2 minimum), so V8 uses D2 on that beat as a diatonic
   passing note leading to A2 in the following bar. This is a one-beat
   departure from strict octave doubling; musically it reinforces the
   D→A bass motion at the phrase cadence and is harmonically idiomatic.

3. Bar 9 Tenor I (V5) written G A G G (sounds G#3 A3 G#3 G#3): beat 2
   written A (sounds A3) places T1 momentarily at the same pitch as A2
   (bar 9 A2 = A4 on beat 2, different octave — no unison in fact). No
   actual rule break; noted here for transparency.

4. Soprano II (V2) bar 17 holds A4 for a full 4 beats while S1 also
   holds A4. This S1/S2 unison on the final note is deliberate — a
   blended unison on the tonic at the close, which is standard choral
   practice and explicitly suggested by the prompt ("S1+S2 may share
   unisons occasionally for blend or emphasis"). The final cadence is
   the strongest possible position for this effect.
