Lever harp setup (Eb storage tuning, all levers down = Eb major):
  Raise the Bb levers  → Bb major requires levers raised on Bb and Eb strings.
  Specifically, from Eb storage tuning raise: Eb→E (i.e. raise Eb levers to E) is WRONG.
  Correct: Eb storage = Eb Ab Bb. To play Bb major (Bb Eb F):
    Eb storage gives: Eb F G Ab Bb C D
    Bb major needs:   Bb C D Eb F G A
    Adjustments needed: Ab→A (raise A levers) and no change to Bb, Eb.
    Lever positions for Bb major from Eb storage:
      Eb strings: levers DOWN (Eb = diatonic ✓)
      Bb strings: levers DOWN (Bb = diatonic ✓)
      Ab strings: levers UP → A natural (diatonic 6th degree ✓)
      All other strings: no levers needed
    Result: Bb C D Eb F G A — exactly Bb major ✓

Nearest skipped key: n/a (original key is valid).

The original tenor's raised F# (^F,) in Phrases A, B, C, D bar 1 implies a
secondary dominant: the chord is C major (V/V) or A dominant 7th (V/iii).
Per the diatonic constraint, this was replaced with F natural, yielding a
Bb chord or Bb/F harmony. The bass continues on Bb2 pedal at these points,
confirming Bb I as the substitute.

Phrase C bar 1: soprano sings D/Eb/F# — a chromatic run that tonicizes the
iii chord briefly. Only S1 carries these accidentals; all other voices
remain on Bb-major diatonic tones, creating a characteristic lever-harp
color where the diatonic strings sound against S1's chromatic motion.

======================================================================
TEXTURE PLAN
======================================================================
The arrangement opens with a reduced antiphonal texture in Phrase A: the
SSAA choir leads with the full soprano line and diatonic alto support while
TTBB mirrors the soprano at the octave (T1), with T2 providing contrary
motion beneath. Phrase B introduces all eight voices in full homophony as
the dynamic arc builds. Phrase C shifts to a call-and-response texture
between the soprano melody (S1) and the tenor doubling (T1) at the octave
below, while the inner voices (S2, A1, A2, T2) provide sustained harmonic
color and the baritone and bass maintain the structural bass line. Phrase D
brings the entire ensemble to its climax in full 8-voice homophony,
concluding with a firm Bb major cadence.

Texture types used:
  1. Antiphonal exchange (SSAA vs TTBB) — Phrase A
  2. Full 8-voice homophony — Phrases B and D
  3. Call-and-response between outer melodic pairs (S1/T1) with inner
     harmonic support — Phrase C
  4. Contrary motion within pairs (A1/A2, T1/T2) throughout

RULE COMPROMISES
======================================================================
1. Phrase C, T1/T2 momentary octave parallel with S1/S2:
   Tenor I mirrors Soprano I at the octave throughout Phrases A, B, D. This
   creates octave doubling between the two voices (S1 and T1). This is
   intentional and stylistically standard in hymn arranging; it is not a
   voice-leading rule violation between adjacent voices (S1/S2 is the
   relevant adjacent pair). S1 and T1 are cross-choir; octave doubling
   between them is a feature of the call-and-response texture.

2. Phrase A, A2 bar 3 descends to D3 (minimum of A2 range):
   The descent to D3 is a passing tone and resolves upward to F3 on the
   next beat. The rule states leaps of a 6th or more must resolve stepwise
   in the opposite direction; this descent is stepwise throughout and poses
   no concern. Noted as a low-range approach only.

3. Phrase C, S2 bar 1 descends to Bb3:
   When S1 descends to D4 in Phrase C bar 1, S2 moves to Bb3 (a 3rd below),
   which places S2 in the alto register. This is acceptable because S2 moves
   back up stepwise in bar 2 (Eb4–F4–G4) and never crosses A1 or A2.

4. T1 doubles S1 at the octave throughout (Phrases A, B, D):
   This creates canonic-like doubling between soprano and tenor, a classical
   SSAATTBB texture technique. Strictly the soprano and tenor are not
   "adjacent" voices in the same choir; no voice-leading rule is broken.
   Noted for transparency.

5. Phrase A, bar 3, Baritone: E, (Eb3) above B1 C, (C3) = 3rd interval.
   No issue. Documented because E, is the written accidental character for
   Eb3 in Bb key and may appear to be an accidental at a glance; it is fully
   diatonic (Eb is the 4th degree of Bb major).
