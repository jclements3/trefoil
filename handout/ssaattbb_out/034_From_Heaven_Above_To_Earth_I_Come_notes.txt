ARRANGER NOTES
034 — From Heaven Above To Earth I Come
"Vom Himmel Hoch" (Luther / Winkworth / Common Service Book 1917)
SSAATTBB arrangement for 8-voice ensemble with lever harp
============================================================

TRANSPOSITION
-------------

Semitones:      0 (identity transposition)

  Ab lever: UP  (raises Ab → A natural ... wait — in Eb storage tuning, raising lever 1 = Ab→A)

HARMONIC SUBSTITUTIONS
----------------------
No harmonic substitutions were required. The original SATB harmonization is
entirely diatonic in D major except for the chromatic passing tone G# in the
tenor voice (see Diatonic Violations below). No secondary dominants, augmented
sixths, or modal mixture chords appear in the original that would require
reharmonization.


DIATONIC VIOLATIONS CAUGHT
---------------------------
The following accidentals appear in the original SATB and were replaced in all
voices except Soprano I:

  Bar 1, beat 2:  Original Tenor (S2V1): ^G, (G#3)
    — G# is a chromatic lower-neighbor passing tone (A3–G#3–A3).
    — Replaced with G natural (G3) in Tenor I (V:5) and all dependent voices.
    — The diatonic G natural still functions as a lower-neighbor and preserves
      the melodic shape without chromaticism.

  Bar 5, beat 2:  Original Tenor (S2V1): ^G, (G#3) — same pattern
    — Replaced with G natural (G3) in Tenor I (V:5).

No other non-diatonic notes appear in the original inner voices.

Note on C naturals: The original S2V2 (Bass) uses "C," which in ABC key D
notation = C#3 (diatonic leading tone). No C-naturals appear in the original
bass voice — this was confirmed before arranging.


TEXTURE PLAN
------------
The hymn is through-composed in two parallel 4-bar phrases (plus an 8th-note
pickup). The arrangement opens in full 8-voice homophony but in close voicing,
preserving the intimate character of a Luther family chorale. Phrase 1 (bars 1–4)
features the lower four voices (Tenor I, Tenor II, Baritone, Bass I) providing
a solid harmonic foundation with stepwise independent lines, while the upper four
voices (S1, S2, A1, A2) carry the melody and inner harmony with contrary motion
between paired voices. Phrase 2 (bars 5–8) opens up in register as S1 rises
toward the climax on D5 (bar 6 beat 3) and B1 anchors on B2–D2, then converges
on the final D major cadence with a stepwise soprano descent (G4–F#4–E4–D4) and
a 4-3 suspension in Alto I (E4 resolving to D4 on beat 3 of bar 8). Texture
types employed: (1) full 8-voice homophony throughout; (2) contrary motion
splitting within each voice pair (A1/A2, T1/T2) for inner-voice melodic
independence; (3) suspension at the final cadence (A1 beat 2 bar 8 = E4 over
the tonic bass D3, resolving to D4 on beat 3).

RULE COMPROMISES
----------------
1. "Open with reduced texture (4–6 voices)" — the pickup and bar 1 use all 8
   voices simultaneously. Given that this is a very short 8-bar chorale with
   a 1-beat pickup, reducing to 4–6 voices would have required rests in multiple
   inner voices, leaving uncomfortably empty texture. Instead, the opening
   8-voice voicing is kept in close range (S1 on D5 down to B1 on D2) with
   no extreme high or low notes, achieving the "intimate opening" effect through
   register rather than reduction. This is a deliberate relaxation of the
   "reduced texture" guideline applied on musical grounds.

2. "Climax places S1 at G5–A5 and B1 at C2–D2 simultaneously" — S1's highest
   note is D5 (the melodic peak of the original hymn). The source melody never
   rises above D5 in D major, and S1 is instructed to "follow original SATB
   soprano closely." Pushing S1 to G5–A5 would require composing entirely new
   melodic material for the climax, which contradicts the instruction to follow
   the original. Bass I reaches B2 (bar 5) and D3 (bar 8) — not as low as C2,
   but the original bass does not descend below G2 in this hymn. No rule
   compromise is needed here — the source material defines the range ceiling.

3. Alto I bar 3 beat 2: A4 — this is the upper comfortable limit for A1 (F3–D5,
   comfortable top C5). A4 is well within range and presents no concern.

4. Parallel motion check — Alto II (V:4) moves with Alto I (V:3) in parallel
   thirds in several measures rather than strict contrary motion. The instruction
   says "recompose in contrary motion to A1 where possible," acknowledging that
   contrary motion is not always achievable. In bars where the harmony constrains
   both voices to stepwise descent (e.g., bar 7 inner cadence), parallel thirds
   are used as a professional idiomatic solution. No parallel perfect fifths or
   octaves occur between any two adjacent voices.

5. Tenor II (V:6) bar 5 beats 1–2: B3 concert on both beats (static) while
   Tenor I holds D4 concert. This creates two beats of a sustained major sixth,
   which is harmonically rich but lacks contrary motion. The static B3 on T2
   is intentional — it provides a pedal-like anchor beneath the T1 descent
   (D4–D4–C#4–A3) and supports the Bar/B1 foundation. No voice leading rule
   is broken; this is an expressive choice.
