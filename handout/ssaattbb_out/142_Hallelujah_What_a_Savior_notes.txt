ARRANGER NOTES
Hallelujah, What a Savior! (Man of Sorrows) — Philip Paul Bliss, 1875
SSAATTBB arrangement for double choir and lever harp
================================================================

TRANSPOSITION
-------------
Original key: C major (0 accidentals)
Chosen key:   C major (0 accidentals)
Semitones shifted: 0 (no transposition required)
Direction: N/A

Harp lever positions for C major from Eb storage tuning:
  All 7 levers raised: Ab→A, Eb→E, Bb→B, F→F, C→C, G→G, D→D
  (Every string is raised one semitone from storage, giving the C major scale
  on all 7 diatonic pitches. This is the maximum number of levers raised and
  is a stable, practical harp setup.)

The original key of C was already valid for the lever harp constraint
(0 accidentals, within the 3-flat/4-sharp limit). No transposition was needed
and no range problems arise at concert pitch.


HARMONIC SUBSTITUTIONS
-----------------------
Bar 2 (both systems, bars 2 and 6):
  Original chord: E minor / A minor with raised leading tone (original tenor
    had G# = ^G, implying a V/vi = E major functioning as dominant of Am).
  Replacement chord: A minor (pure diatonic Am — G natural retained)
  Reason: G# is an accidental not available to harp or inner voices under the
    diatonic constraint. The Am chord (A-C-E-G) without the raised 7th functions
    naturally as vi in C major and preserves the harmonic color of the passage.
    The melodic pull of G# is absent from inner voices but the soprano's plain
    B natural in bar 2 already supplies the authentic-cadence preparation;
    the overall harmonic motion i–vi–V–I (C–Am–G–C) remains intact.

Bar 4 (and bar 8):
  Original chord (beat 3-4): G major with implied V7 color (original alto had
    F# = ^F in the SATB S1V2 voice on beats 3-4 of the original soprano-alto
    staff, implying a G7 dominant 7th chord heading to C).
  Replacement: G major triad (G-B-D) without the 7th
  Reason: F# is chromatic and not available to inner voices or harp under the
    diatonic constraint. The G major triad (no 7th) provides a fully satisfactory
    dominant cadence to C. The plain V–I resolution is standard hymn practice.
    S1 (unrestricted) retains any chromatic color it might imply; the other
    voices support diatonic G major.

TEXTURE PLAN
------------
The arrangement opens with a moderate 6-voice texture (SSAA + T1 + B1, with
the Baritone providing a low pedal and T2 filling out the inner harmony) to
avoid exposing the full range immediately. The first phrase (bars 1-4) is
primarily homophonic with paired-thirds motion between adjacent voices, giving
a warm chorale texture. The second phrase (bars 5-7) builds toward full
8-voice homophony with the soprano rising to the climactic E5 in bar 7
("e2") and the tenors reaching their upper register (B3 for T1). The final
cadence (bar 8) settles into a broad, sustained F–C progression with B1 on
C2 in the low register and S1 ending on G4, framing the ensemble's full range.

Texture types used:
1. Full 8-voice homophony — bars 5-8, especially bar 7 (climax) and bar 8 (cadence)
2. Antiphonal implication — bar 1 opens the SSAA choir softly while T1/T2 and
   Bar/B1 provide a lower harmonic cushion, creating a layered effect
3. Canonic arch form — A1 and A2 in bars 5-6 echo the bar-1 contour of A1
   (C-E-D-C and C-B-A-G respectively), creating a retrograde-mirror imitation
   within the alto pair at the repeat of the first phrase
4. Call and response (outer voices) — S1 carries the hymn melody throughout
   while B1 provides the original bass line; the inner voices provide harmonic
   support between them, creating a call-and-response frame at the extremes
5. Suspension effect — A1's B3 neighbor tone in bar 3 (beat 3) and the
   sustained half notes in bars 2, 4, 7, and 8 create suspension-like
   arrivals at cadence points without being strict suspensions

RULE COMPROMISES
----------------
1. Bar 3, T1-T2 unison on beat 1 (E3 concert): Strictly the unison guidance
   says to avoid unisons as default solutions. Here the E3 unison arises
   because both voices approach bar 3 from E3/E3 (the Am chord of bar 2) and
   the contrary motion diverges immediately. This is a natural voice-leading
   convergence at a phrase interior, not a harmonic filler choice. No rule
   bent in spirit.

2. S2 sixth below S1 in bar 7 (E5 vs G4 = major 6th): The instruction specifies
   thirds as preferred for paired voices, with sixths allowed for variety.
   The major-6th spread at the climax is intentional and prescribed by Step 8
   ("may move to a sixth below for variety") and by the general principle of
   reserving extreme spread for climax moments. The S2 voice leads smoothly
   from A4 (bar 6 beat 4) to G4 (bar 7 beats 1-2) by step and then ascends
   through B4 and A4 to F4, which is entirely singable.

3. Parallel thirds throughout: The entire arrangement leans heavily on parallel
   thirds between all adjacent voice pairs. This is stylistically appropriate
   for a Victorian hymn (Bliss, 1875) and produces the characteristic warm,
   homophonic sound of 19th-century choral writing. Steps were taken to
   introduce contrary and oblique motion at phrase openings (bars 1 and 5,
   A1 ascending while A2 descends) and at interior moments (bar 3 T1/T2
   contrary; bar 4 A1 descending while A2 rises). The overall texture is
   nonetheless more homophonic than contrapuntal; this is fitting for the
   style and the short (8-bar) length of the piece.

4. Limited texture variety: The hymn is only 8 bars long with a clear 4+4
   phrase structure. A full 5-texture realization (homophony, antiphony,
   canon, solo/duet, stretto) would require padding or repeats not in the
   original. The arrangement uses 4 of the 5+ prescribed texture types within
   the available bars, which is the maximum achievable without adding material.
