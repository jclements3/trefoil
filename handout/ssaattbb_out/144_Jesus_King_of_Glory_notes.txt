HARMONIC SUBSTITUTIONS
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Bar 10:
  Original chord: D major functioning as V/vi (secondary dominant of Em).
    Evidence: original inner voices had =F (F natural, lowered 7th of G),
    and bass had ^G# (G-sharp), suggesting V7/vi = D major (D F# A C) or
    an augmented 6th approach to Em.
  Replacement chord: Em (iii) — E G B, purely diatonic in G major.
  Reason: the harp cannot play both G and G# simultaneously, and F natural
    is not available in G major without lever flip. Em preserves the descending
    harmonic motion from I toward ii and provides smooth voice leading.
  Effect: the passage loses its chromatic sharpness but gains a modal,
    folk-hymn character that suits the diatonic lever harp idiom.

Bar 22:
  Original chord: implied V/vi (secondary dominant of Em) via ^G# in tenor
    and chromatic passing motion C–(G#)–A in the bass.
  Replacement chord: Em (iii) — same substitution as bar 10, maintaining
    symmetry. The chromatic passing G# is replaced by the diatonic G,
    and the bass descends C–B–A diatonically.
  Reason: identical to bar 10 — lever harp diatonic constraint.
  Effect: the cadential approach retains a vi-area colour via Em,
    softening the original secondary-dominant tension.

TEXTURE PLAN
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

The arrangement opens with all 8 voices at piano/mezzo-piano (bars 1–4),
building through the first phrase with a generally homophonic texture that
establishes the G major harmonic language. The antiphonal exchange between
SSAA and TTBB emerges at bar 6 (SSAA presents the C major phrase,
TTBB echoes), providing the first textural contrast. A canonic element
appears in bars 9–10, where T1 echoes the S1 descent at a one-bar delay
and an octave below. The central climax lands at bar 11 (E5 in S1) and
again, more fully, at bar 19 where T1 doubles S1 at the octave below,
filling the full ensemble range from G2 (T2) to E5 (S1). The stretto
entries at bar 23 (all voice pairs entering the D-major scalar run
successively over a pedal D) provide the final intensification before
the full-ensemble root-position close at bar 24.

Texture types used:
  1. Full 8-voice homophony — bars 8, 19, 24
  2. Antiphonal exchange (SSAA vs TTBB) — bar 6
  3. Canonic imitation (S1 leading, T1 following one bar behind) — bars 9–10
  4. Call and response (outer voices S1 and B1, inner voices sustain) — bars 17–18
  5. Stretto entries at cadence — bar 23

RULE COMPROMISES
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

1. B1 final note raised (bar 24, and bar 3):
   Rule: B1 follows original bass closely.
   Compromise: The source bass has G,, (G1) in bars 3 and 24 — a basso
     profundo pedal below the B1 range minimum C2 (MIDI 36). These were
     raised to G, (G2) in the arrangement. The original source also has
     G,, in bar 17 (beat 1), raised similarly.
   Reason: Strict range compliance. If the ensemble includes a true basso
     profundo capable of G1, the arranger endorses restoring G,, in bars
     3, 17, and 24 for maximum resonance.

2. B1 additional sub-range notes raised (bars 13, 14, 17, 21, 22):
   The source bass uses A1 (A,,) in bar 13 and B1 (B,,) in bars 14, 17,
   21, 22 — all below C2 minimum. All were raised one octave to A2 and B2.
   Reason: same as above. A skilled professional bass may restore the lower
   octave if it falls within individual range.

3. Octave-displacement of original alto throughout:
   The source SATB alto habitually used D3–E3 (below A1 minimum F3).
   In every such instance, the note was raised to D4–E4. This means A1
   in this arrangement is functionally an upper-alto part sitting in the
   E4–G4 area for much of the piece, rather than the low-register inner
   voice the original alto provided. The consequence is that A2 (which
   must stay below A1) also rides higher than a traditional second alto,
   sitting around C4–G3 rather than G3–D3.
   This is not a voice leading error but an inherent property of the
   A1 range constraint (F3 minimum) conflicting with the low original alto.

4. Baritone D2/C2 notes replaced:
   Wherever a strict 3rd-above-B1 calculation yielded D2 (e.g., over a D
   bass) or C2 (e.g., over a C bass), the Baritone was raised to F#2 or E2
   respectively (the nearest in-range diatonic pitch). This creates
   occasional open-5th or open-octave sonorities between Bar and B1
   (e.g., bar 4: B1=D2, Bar=F#2, a major 3rd; bar 6: B1=C2, Bar=E2,
   a major 3rd) which are musically clean.

5. Bar 18, A1 C5 above S2 D4 — inter-pair voice crossing:
   In the weaving texture of bar 18, A1 briefly touches C5 while S2
   is on D4 or E4. This creates a cross-pair crossing (Alto I above
   Soprano II). The instructions explicitly permit voice crossing
   between pairs "where musically effective." Bar 18 is designated
   a weaving/passing texture moment, so this is intentional.

6. Bar 7, Bar and B1 on identical pitches (D2 E2 C2 D2):
   The Baritone line F#2 G2 E2 F#2 sits a 3rd above B1 (D2 E2 C2 D2)
   cleanly. However, the B1 C2 (beat 3) is exactly at the range minimum.
   This is retained from the source and is appropriate for a trained bass.
