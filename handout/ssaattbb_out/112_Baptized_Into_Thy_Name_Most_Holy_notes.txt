ARRANGER NOTES
Baptized Into Thy Name Most Holy
SSAATTBB arrangement for double choir and lever harp
Source: Open Hymnal Project (X:136), O Dass Ich Tausend Zungen Hätte (Dretzel), 1731

TRANSPOSITION
─────────────
Original key:   F major (1 flat: Bb)
Chosen key:     F major (unchanged)
Semitones:      0 (no transposition)
Direction:      N/A

F major is valid for lever harp in Eb storage tuning (all levers
down = Eb major). Raising the Bb lever restores Bb to the strings,
giving the 7 diatonic pitches of F major: F G A Bb C D E.

Lever positions required for F major:
  Eb → Bb lever: raised (sharped from Eb to Bb — wait, Eb storage
  means all strings are at Eb/Ab/Bb/Eb/Bb/F/C = Eb major).
  Actually: Eb storage has strings tuned to the Eb major scale.
  To play in F major the following levers must be raised:
    Ab → A  (raise Ab lever)
    Eb → E  (raise Eb lever)
  Bb is already in the Eb major scale and does not require adjustment.
  Correct harp lever setup for F major from Eb storage:
    Raise: Ab → A natural, Eb → E natural
    All other levers down (Bb, F, C, G, D strings unchanged from Eb tuning)
  Result: strings sound F G A Bb C D E = F major ✓

No range problems encountered at pitch. All 8 voices sit comfortably
in their professional ranges throughout.


HARMONIC SUBSTITUTIONS
──────────────────────
No reharmonizations were required. The original hymn's SATB harmony
is almost entirely diatonic in F major. The sole harmonic color
requiring attention is the =B (B natural) appearing in the soprano
in bars 7 and 15, and in the original inner voices in bars 5, 17,
and the bass throughout. These are handled as diatonic violations
(see below) rather than reharmonizations, because:

  — In S1, the =B is a modal/chromatic passing tone within a C-major
    or G-minor tonicization. It is permitted freely in S1.
  — In inner voices and bass, the =B resolves the non-diatonic
    raised leading tone and is replaced by Bb (diatonic in K:F).
    The surrounding harmony (C major chord or passing chord) is
    retained; only the inflection of the third/seventh is adjusted.

No chord substitutions involving root changes were made.


DIATONIC VIOLATIONS CAUGHT
───────────────────────────
In K:F, the diatonic scale is F G A Bb C D E. Any B natural (=B)
in inner voices must be replaced by Bb (written as plain B in K:F,
since B is already Bb by key signature).

Bar 5 (original S2V2): C, =B,, C, — bass line C–Bnat–C.
  Replacement: B1 (V:8) sings C, B, C, = C–Bb–C.
  Reason: =B,, (B natural) is non-diatonic. Nearest diatonic neighbor
  is Bb (B, in K:F). The Bb functioning as a passing tone between two
  C's is equally smooth and avoids the chromatic inflection.
  Baritone (V:7) follows with E, D C = E–D–C, a stepwise descent
  that replaces any dependency on the leading-tone Bn.

Bar 5 equivalent (bar 13, original S2V2): same situation.
  Same replacement applied.

Bar 17 equivalent (original S1V2): C =B, C — alto line C–Bnat–C.
  Voice: V:3 (Alto I).
  Original note: =B, (B natural below middle C, concert B3).
  Replacement: B, (= Bb3) — diatonic neighbor one half-step lower.
  The motion C–Bb–C is diatonic and musically equivalent.
  (In the original SATB, S1V2 at the equivalent point of phrase 3
  contained this passing tone. In the SSAATTBB, V:3 bar 17 reads
  C B, C, retaining the neighbor-tone figure with Bb.)

Bar 7 and 15 (original S1V1 and S1V2): =B in the treble.
  S1 (V:1) retains =B freely as melodic color.
  S2 through B1 (V:2–V:8): replaced as above at all occurrences.
  No instance of =B appears in V:2–V:8 in this arrangement.

TEXTURE PLAN
────────────
The arrangement opens with SSAA choir alone (bars 1–2) at a restrained
dynamic (pp–mp), allowing the chorale melody to emerge clearly before
TTBB enters at bar 3 to complete the first full 8-voice statement.
Phrase 2 (bars 7–12) is a sustained full-homophony climax followed by
an exact harmonic repeat of the opening material, now at full texture.
Phrase 3 (bars 13–18) is antiphonal: SSAA sings bars 13–14 alone (mf),
TTBB answers bars 15–16 alone (mp), then both choirs unite for the
final two bars of the phrase (f). The coda (bars 19–24) opens with
a call-and-response between the outer voices S1 and B1 (bars 19–20),
giving way to full 8-voice homophony at the sintro marking (bars 21–23)
before the fermata cadence (bar 24).

Texture types used:
  1. Reduced 4-voice opening (SSAA only, bars 1–2)
  2. Full 8-voice homophony (bars 7–8, 9–12, 21–23)
  3. Antiphonal exchange SSAA vs TTBB (bars 13–16)
  4. Call-and-response outer voices S1/B1 (bars 19–20)
  5. Stretto/convergence to full texture at cadence (bars 23–24)

RULE COMPROMISES
────────────────
1. Bar 24 — S1 and S2 in unison (F4):
   Rule 5 specifies "prefer thirds and sixths between paired voices."
   Both voices arriving on the root F at the final cadence was judged
   more effective than forcing S2 to a C or A below, which would weaken
   the sense of resolution. This is an intentional blend cadence, not a
   voice-leading failure.

2. Bars 7–8 — T1 and T2 sharing G/E in full parallel motion through
   the cadential bar (g3 / e3 written):
   The two tenors move in parallel thirds throughout bars 7–8, which is
   technically permitted (thirds are the preferred interval). However,
   the lack of contrary motion here means T1 and T2 do not function
   as independent melodic lines during these two bars. This was accepted
   to provide a strong block of tenoral support under the S1 chromatic
   =B passage, where harmonic clarity is more important than contrapuntal
   independence.

3. Bars 19–20 — Baritone and Bass I are relatively close in range:
   The instruction to "not open the arrangement at full range" was
   observed; however, in the coda the lower voices are intentionally
   kept low to support the final approach to the cadence. The Baritone
   moving through Bb2–A2–E2 and B1 through G2–F2–C2 creates a
   narrowing effect before the unison F2 final — this is deliberate.

4. Bar 5 — Diatonic replacement of =B,, in B1:
   The original chorale bass has a chromatic passing tone (Bn natural)
   creating a leading-tone motion to C. Replacing with Bb produces a
   descending half-step (C–Bb–C) that is diatonic but reduces the
   tension. This is unavoidable given the harp's diatonic constraint.
   The loss of the raised leading tone in the bass is partially
   compensated by the Baritone's stepwise descent (E–D–C) which
   provides forward motion across the same beat.

5. Bars 13–14 — TTBB rests (antiphonal texture):
   During the antiphonal section, the resting choir has no notes,
   which means there are two bars in which those voices have no melodic
   activity. This violates the spirit of "every voice must be a singable
   independent melodic line" but is a deliberate texture device
   (antiphonal exchange) rather than a voice-leading failure.
