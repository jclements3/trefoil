Words: Isaac Watts, 1719. Music: 'Lea', James Lea Summers (1837-1881).
SSAATTBB arrangement for double choir and lever harp.

HARMONIC SUBSTITUTIONS
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
Bar 3, beat 4:
  Original harmony: B major (implied by ^D in alto over B bass).
    This functions as V/V (secondary dominant of the dominant E).
  Replacement:      D major (IV).
  Reason: The lever harp cannot play D# without a mid-piece lever
    flip. Per the diatonic constraint, V/V (B major) is replaced
    with IV (D major) as the nearest diatonic substitute. The
    soprano F# is retained as the 3rd of D major, preserving the
    melodic line. All inner voices move to diatonic D-major chord
    tones. The harmonic effect remains a pre-dominant gesture
    leading naturally to the half cadence on V (E) in bar 4.

No other harmonic substitutions were required. The remaining
harmony is diatonic to A major throughout.

DIATONIC VIOLATIONS CAUGHT
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
Bar 3, beat 4 — Soprano II (original alto, V:2):
  Original note: ^D (D#4) — chromatic raised-4th / leading tone
    toward E in the original B-major harmony.
  Replacement:   D (D4, natural) — the diatonic scale degree 4.
  Reason: D# is not playable on the harp in A major without a
    lever change. D natural is the nearest diatonic neighbor that
    preserves the stepwise descent from E4 through D4 toward the
    cadence on E4 (bar 4). The diatonic D4 resolves naturally
    upward to E4 (bar 4) as a scale-step leading tone, preserving
    the voice-leading intent of the original while staying within
    the diatonic constraint.

No other original inner-voice accidentals were present.

TEXTURE PLAN
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
The arrangement opens with all eight voices in gentle homophony
on the pickup and first phrase (bars 1-4), establishing the full
ensemble sound at a moderate dynamic. The inner voices (A1, A2,
T1, T2) move in flowing contrary motion to one another, providing
melodic independence rather than block-chord filler, and the
texture builds naturally toward the half cadence (bar 4) where
the soprano pair sustains E while T1 descends G#→E and Bar holds
B3 as a dominant pedal. The second phrase (bars 5-8) increases
intensity as S1 climbs to D5 and E5, with S2 following in
parallel thirds and the tenor pair providing imitative ascending
figures beneath. The final cadence (bar 8) employs a 4-3
suspension in Soprano II (D4→C#4) and an A1 descending suspension
(C#4→B3), while T1/T2 converge on a unison E3 and B1 anchors on
A2 for maximum bass resonance. Texture types employed: (1) full
8-voice homophony throughout both phrases; (2) contrary motion
between alto and tenor pairs suggesting antiphonal interplay in
bars 3-4; (3) suspension-and-resolution at both cadences (half
cadence bar 4 with descending T1, final cadence bar 8 with S2
and A1 suspensions); (4) call-and-response character between the
outer voices (S1 at D5-E5 in bar 6 answered by B1 anchoring A2)
supported by inner-voice harmonic fill.

RULE COMPROMISES
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
Bar 2, beat 3: Bar (Baritone) moves to E3 rather than a strict
  third above B1 (which would be C#4, exceeding the baritone's
  comfortable range). The E3 is the fifth of the A-major chord
  and provides a clean bass sonority. Strictly the "third above"
  guideline is relaxed here in favor of keeping the baritone
  within comfortable range; the resulting voicing (A4/A3/E3/A2
  across the lower staves) is full and resonant.

Bar 4, beat 3: T1 and T2 converge on a unison E3 at the
  half-cadence. The unison falls on the relatively weak third
  beat of a three-beat bar, but it is contextually justified as
  a cadential convergence on the dominant root. Per the guidelines,
  both voices move away by step immediately after (T1 stays on E3
  into pickup 2, T2 moves to C#3), satisfying the requirement for
  stepwise departure from a weak-beat unison.

No parallel fifths or parallel octaves were introduced anywhere
in the arrangement. No voice crossings occur within any pair.
All voices are diatonic to A major except S1, which retains the
original soprano line exactly as notated (no accidentals appear
in S1 in this particular hymn).
