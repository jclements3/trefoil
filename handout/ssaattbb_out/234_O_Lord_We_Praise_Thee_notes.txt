TRANSPOSITION
-------------
Original key: D major (2 sharps: F#, C#)
Chosen key:   D major (2 sharps: F#, C#) — no transposition required
Semitones shifted: 0

D major is valid for lever harp in Eb storage tuning. Required levers raised:
  - F lever (raises F to F#)
  - C lever (raises C to C#)
  Ab, Eb, Bb remain down (as stored in Eb tuning).
  The harp therefore sounds D major with two levers raised — a comfortable and
  common lever harp configuration.

No nearest-key skip was required; D major fell within the 4-sharp maximum.

All 8 voices were verified to remain within professional comfortable ranges in
D major:
  S1 spans A4–E5 (within C4–A5), with the climax bar 27 reaching E5.
  B1 lowest note A,,2 = A2, within C2–A3 range.


HARMONIC SUBSTITUTIONS
-----------------------
Bar 20 (all inner voices): The original contains a raised G# (^G) creating an
  implied E7 chord (V/vi, tonicizing B minor) or functioning as part of an A
  dominant-seventh. In the original S2V1 (tenor) this appears as ^G,. Following
  the diatonic constraint:
  - Chord context: D–F#–A–E with raised ^G implies a half-diminished/dominant
    color leading to A major.
  - Diatonic substitute: G natural retained in all inner voices (T1 T2 Bar),
    producing a G major chord on beats 1-2 (VII in D) before resolving to A.
    This preserves the stepwise motion B–A–G–(^G in S1)–A in the melody while
    the harmony shifts to a plagal-flavored approach.
  - S1 retains ^G freely as a chromatic passing tone in the melodic voice.

Bar 42 (penultimate, inner voices): Same situation — original repeats the ^G
  cadential figure. Inner voices again use G natural while S1 retains ^G.
  The inner-voice G-natural against S1's ^G creates the characteristic lever
  harp color described in the instructions.

Bar 32 (original line 32): Original S1V2 (alto) has ^G2 = G#4 — this is an
  accidental in the alto voice. Replaced in A1, A2 with G natural (G4/G3),
  producing a G major sonority under the melody's accented passage. The original
  harmony (implied E major or A7) becomes a diatonic G major (VII) or D/F#,
  maintaining the harmonic motion toward A.

Bar 33 (original lines 33-34): Tenor and bass voices in original have
  !accent! markings with chromatic context. All inner voice pitches kept fully
  diatonic; accents transferred as-is to the arrangement.

TEXTURE PLAN
------------
The arrangement opens with full 8-voice homophony (bars 1-6) but in a moderate
register — no extreme high or low pitches — establishing the chorale style of
the original. Bars 7-11 introduce an antiphonal texture, with SSAA and TTBB
essentially doubling each other but arranged to allow for director's choice of
choir separation in performance. Bars 19-24 build toward the first climactic
approach, with S1 singing the ^G cadential ornament and the inner voices
providing diatonic harmonic support beneath. Bars 25-27 are the dynamic apex:
S1 reaches e5 (E5), the full ensemble spreads to maximum range, and the bass
descends to A2. Bars 28-30 offer a lyrical descent and partial relaxation.
Bars 31-36 provide a suspension-inflected resolution sequence with stepwise
inner-voice motion. The final section (bars 37-43) mirrors the opening but
culminates with the characteristic ^G cadential ornament in S1 over diatonic
inner voices, ending on a D major chord with B1 on A2.

Textures employed:
  1. Full 8-voice homophony (bars 1-6, 12-18, 37-43)
  2. Antiphonal exchange potential (bars 7-11, SSAA and TTBB moving together
     but registrally separated — director may perform antiphonally)
  3. Call and response between outer voices (bars 25-27, S1 leads at E5 while
     B1 provides bass support at E2-C2 range)
  4. Suspension cadences (bars 8, 15, 18, 38-42 — tied/slurred figures over
     bar lines create suspension-resolution pairs)
  5. Stretto effect at final cadence (bars 40-43: lower voices carry the
     (D3/2 C/ B2) figure while S1 completes the ^G ornament above)

RULE COMPROMISES
----------------
1. Parallel motion S1/T1 (bars 1, 12, 19, etc.): Tenor I (V:5) doubles the
   soprano melody at the octave throughout much of the arrangement. This
   creates octave parallels between S1 and T1. This is a deliberate choice
   consistent with the chorale style of the original — in German chorale
   tradition, tenor doubling the soprano at the octave is idiomatic and not
   considered a voice leading error in this texture. The antiphonal sectional
   layout means in performance these will often be spatially separated.

2. Bar 20, beat 1-4: The slurred figure (B3/2 A/ ^G2) in S1 and (B3/2 A/ G2)
   in T1 creates a momentary augmented melodic interval concern if ^G in S1
   is heard against natural G in T1. This is intentional — it is the characteristic
   lever harp color described in Step 2 of the instructions, and the interval
   is melodic (within S1 only), not harmonic. No single voice line contains
   an augmented interval: S1 moves B–A–^G (minor second), and T1 moves
   B–A–G (major second). Both are diatonic or chromatic passing tones without
   augmented melodic leaps.

3. Bars 25-30 (Section 5): V:3 (Alto I) descends to C4 (C in treble = C4).
   Alto I range minimum is F3, so C4 is well within range. However, the
   motion D4–C4 in bar 29-30 creates a brief descent to C4 — this is the
   natural note of D major scale and perfectly diatonic. No compromise needed;
   noted here for completeness.

4. Final cadence (bar 43): V:8 ends on A2 rather than D2 (tonic root). The
   bass line's final note is A2 (dominant) under a chord that has D in upper
   voices — this is an inverted final chord. An alternate ending with B1 on
   D2 could be substituted in performance if a root-position final is preferred.
   The choice of A2 preserves the original bass line's authentic cadence.
