ARRANGER NOTES
Jesus, Tender Shepherd, Hear Me
Words: Mary L. Duncan, 1839. Music: John Stainer, 1898 ("Evening Prayer").
SSAATTBB arrangement from Open Hymnal Project SATB source.

═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
TRANSPOSITION
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
Original key : F major (1 flat — Bb)
Chosen key   : F major (no change)
Semitones    : 0 (no transposition)
Direction    : N/A

F major is a valid lever-harp key (1 flat = Bb).
Lever positions required:
  Raise the Bb lever on every Bb string (storage tuning gives Eb major;
  raising Ab, Eb, Bb brings the harp to F major with Bb in key signature).
  Specifically raise levers on: Ab → Eb → Bb strings.
  Result: F major diatonic set (F G A Bb C D E).

No range problems arose that would have required choosing a different key.

═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
HARMONIC SUBSTITUTIONS
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
The original hymn is entirely diatonic within F major except for two
chromatic passing tones in inner voices (see Diatonic Violations below).
No reharmonization of any beat was required. The dominant seventh (V7 =
C–E–G–Bb) and other harmonies present are all fully diatonic to F major.

═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
DIATONIC VIOLATIONS CAUGHT
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
Two accidentals appear in the original SATB inner voices. Both are in
voices that map to non-S1 parts and must be replaced by diatonic neighbors.

1. Bar 7, original Tenor (S2V1): =B, (B-natural, canceling key-sig Bb)
   Context: A3 – Bnat3 – C4 – F3 (a chromatic passing tone from A to C)
   Replacement: Bb3 (the diatonic scale degree 4)
   Result in V5 (Tenor I): A – Bb – C – F (written A – B – c – F in 8vb
   treble notation). The Bb passing tone preserves the stepwise motion
   A→Bb→C perfectly and is indistinguishable musically. No reharmonization
   was needed.

2. Bar 6, original Bass (S2V2): ^C, (C-sharp, bar 6 beat 3)
   Context: F3 – D3 – C#3 – A2 (a chromatic passing tone implying V/ii)
   Replacement: C3 (natural, the diatonic scale degree 5)
   Result in V8 (Bass I): F – D – C – A (all natural, all diatonic to F).
   The descending bass line F–D–C–A retains its stepwise character and
   the chromatic color of C# is not missed given the full 8-voice texture.
   The harp cannot play C# without a lever flip, so this replacement is
   essential.

TEXTURE PLAN
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
The piece is two 4-bar phrases; the textural arc moves from reduced to
full scoring across each phrase, with antiphonal contrast at the seam.

PHRASE 1 (bars 1–4):
  Bar 1   — SSAA only (upper choir, 4 voices). Gentle, chamber-like opening.
  Bar 2   — SSAA + Bass I (5 voices). Bass I enters with the original
             bass line to anchor the harmony before the full choir arrives.
  Bars 3–4 — Full 8 voices (homophony + independent inner lines). Tenor I
             and II enter in bar 3 with a rising line (C–D–E) against the
             soprano's F–G–A ascent. Baritone enters on bar 3 beat 1.

PHRASE 2 (bars 5–8):
  Bar 5   — TTBB antiphonal (lower choir only, SSAA silent). TTBB echoes
             the opening soprano motive (S1 bar 1) at the lower register,
             Baritone entering on a dramatic C4 (top of range). This creates
             a call-and-response arch: upper choir opens phrase 1, lower
             choir opens phrase 2.
  Bar 6   — Full 8 voices return simultaneously.
  Bars 7–8 — Full 8 to the end. Bar 7 recapitulates bar 3 material in all
             voices. Bar 8 cadences with a quasi-suspension in the Baritone
             (A3→G3→A3 over the final tonic-dominant bass motion C3→F3),
             and an A1–A2 unison on beat 2 (A3) that resolves outward to the
             final chord (C4 and Bb3) for blend at the close.

Textures used (4 of 6 specified types):
  (1) Full 8-voice homophony — bars 3–4 and 6–8
  (2) Antiphonal exchange SSAA vs TTBB — bars 1 and 5
  (3) Reduced upper-choir texture with bass foundation — bars 1–2
  (4) Call and response between outer choirs (SSAA bar 1 / TTBB bar 5)
      with inner voices providing harmonic support

  Bar 2 beat 1: V3 (A1) = D4, V4 (A2) = Bb3 — no unisons in bars 1–2

    Purpose: cadential blend before final half note. Both voices move away
    in the half note (A1→C4 up, A2→Bb3 up by step). Flagged: beat 2 is a

Within TTBB:
  Bar 5 beat 3 (weak): V6 (T2) = A3, V7 (Bar) = A3 — cross-choir unison

  Bar 5 beat 4 (weak): V6 (T2) = G3, V7 (Bar) = G3 — same cross-choir

RANGE LOG
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
Soprano I (V1):
  Bar 1 beats 1–3: C5 — within comfortable range (comfortable top G5, no
    concern). The piece does not approach A5; S1 remains in the middle of
    its range throughout, appropriate for a gentle evening prayer.

Alto II (V4):
  Bar 1 beat 1: D4 — within comfortable range. V4 descends to G3 (bar 2
    beat 4), near the lower end of comfortable A2 range. Not a concern for
    a professional singer.
  Bar 3 beats 3–4: Bb3 — comfortable.

Tenor I (V5, concert pitch):
  Bar 4 beat 1: E4 (written e in 8vb treble) — at the top of comfortable
    T1 range (comfortable top E4). This is the highest note in the
    arrangement for any tenor voice. It falls on the return of the soprano's
    pickup gesture (c/B/ = C5/Bb4) and is appropriately climactic within
    bar 4. Not a concern at performance tempo.
  Bar 5 beat 1: C4 (written c) — moderate, entry of antiphonal TTBB phrase.

Baritone (V7):
  Bar 5 beat 1: C4 — top of Baritone range (comfortable top A3, absolute
    top C4). This is the most extreme note in the entire arrangement. It
    occurs at the dramatic TTBB antiphonal entry and functions as a deliberate
    climax note to mark the moment the lower choir takes over. A professional
    baritone can reach this comfortably; in performance, allow a slight
    swell on this note for effect.

Bass I (V8):
  Bars 3–4: A2 and E2 (written A,, and E,,) — low but within B1 range
    (bottom C2). The original bass line was transposed down an octave here
    (original had F3–A3 and E3–F3) to (a) keep Bar always above B1 and
    (b) place the bass in a more characteristic low register for bar 3–4
    full-choir entry. Not a concern for a professional bass.
  Bar 5: A3 (written A,) — at the very top of comfortable B1 range
    (comfortable top F3, absolute top A3). Acceptable at the antiphonal
    TTBB entry; moves downward immediately.

RULE COMPROMISES
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
1. Bar 3 beat 1, A1–A2 pair (V3–V4): A1 = C4, A2 = A3. V4 (A2) enters on
   the same note A3 that V3 (A1) uses on bar 2 beat 4 (Bb3 → A3 is a step
   for A1 entering bar 3). There is no within-pair unison here, but A2 = A3
   could imply close proximity to the A1 = C4 (a minor third). No rule is
   technically broken; noted for transparency.

2. Bar 8 beat 2, A1–A2 unison (V3–V4): As logged in Unison Log above.
   This falls on beat 2 (weak beat). Both voices move away in the following
   half note (A1 up a third to C4; A2 up by step to Bb3). The instruction
   requires stepwise departure in both voices; A1's departure by a third is a
   minor relaxation of "by step." The cadential context (bar 8, penultimate
   beat) justifies it as a deliberate closing blend effect.

3. Bars 3–4 and 7–8, V3 (A1) and V2 (S2) move in parallel seconds at
   certain beats (e.g., bar 4 beats 3–4: S2 = E4, A1 = D4; bar 7 beat 1:
   S2 = D4, A1 = C4). Parallel seconds between adjacent non-paired voices
   (S2 and A1) are not explicitly prohibited by the rules (only 5ths and 8ths
   are listed). This close inner voicing is characteristic of warm choral
   harmony in a hymn setting and is retained intentionally.

4. Bass I (V8) bars 3–4 transposed down an octave from the original bass
   line (S2V2). The original bass reached A3 and C4 in these bars, which
   would place B1 above Baritone (V7) and violate the no-crossing-within-pair
   rule. The octave transposition (to A2, G2, F2 in bar 3; E2, F2, C3 in
   bar 4) resolves the crossing and places B1 appropriately in the bass
   register. The harmony is unaffected (same scale degrees, lower octave).
