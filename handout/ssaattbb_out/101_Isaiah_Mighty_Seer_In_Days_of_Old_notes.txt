TRANSPOSITION
─────────────
Original key:  Eb major (3 flats)
Chosen key:    Eb major (3 flats) — NO TRANSPOSITION REQUIRED
Semitones shifted: 0

Eb major is a valid key for lever harp in Eb storage tuning (all levers down).
No levers need to be raised for Eb major: the open-string tuning IS Eb major.
All 7 diatonic pitches (Eb F G Ab Bb C D) are available on open strings.

HARMONIC SUBSTITUTIONS
──────────────────────
The original hymn is largely diatonic in Eb major. Two chromatic moments appear:

m.31/37 (repeated phrase), tenor (S2V1): =B natural (Bb raised to B♮).
  This figure G–A–Bb–G with the raised =B creates a momentary V/iv color.
  Substitution: replaced =B with Bb throughout (diatonic neighbor down by half step).
  Harmonic effect: the phrase remains on the tonic/submediant motion without the
  secondary dominant inflection. The diatonic Bb preserves smooth voice leading.

m.42, tenor (S2V1): _D (Db) on beat 3 of the final approach to cadence.
  Db is not a diatonic pitch in Eb major (the scale has D natural).
  Substitution: replaced Db with D natural (diatonic). This removes a momentary
  Neapolitan/modal-mixture color and keeps the approach cadence within the key.

m.42, alto (S1V2): _D (Db) — same measure, same substitution issue.
  Replaced with D natural for the same reason.

TEXTURE PLAN
────────────
The arrangement opens with SSAA alone (mm.1–2), introducing the chorale texture
with only four voices before TTBB enters at m.3, achieving a gradual crescendo
of texture from 4 to 8 voices. Phrase 3 (mm.13–18) employs antiphonal exchange:
SSAA carries mm.13–14, TTBB answers mm.15–16, then all 8 voices converge at
mm.17–18 for the first full climax. The repeated phrases in mm.31–36 and 37–42
(phrases 6 and 7) use canonic imitation between S1/T1 at the octave, with SSAA
and TTBB presenting the same melodic material in rhythmic unison but registral
separation — a kind of double-choir echo texture. The final phrase (mm.43–48)
drives to full 8-voice homophony at the fermata in m.45, then the intro passage
(m.46 onward) opens into call-and-response between the outer voices (S1 and B1),
with inner voices sustaining the harmonic field. The final cadence has all voices
converge in a sustained suspension resolution.

Texture types employed:
  1. Full 8-voice homophony (mm.17–18, 23–24, 43–45)
  2. Antiphonal SSAA vs TTBB exchange (mm.13–16)
  3. Reduced opening texture — 4-voice SSAA (mm.1–2)
  4. Canonic imitation at the octave between S1 and T1 (mm.31–42 repeated phrases)
  5. Call and response between outer voices S1/B1 (mm.46–48 final cadence)

RULE COMPROMISES
────────────────
1. Bar mm.31 and 37, Voice V:7 (Baritone), beat 3: =B,, (B♮ in Eb)
   The original tenor figure (G–A–B♮–G) was so idiomatic and characteristic
   of the hymn tune that carrying it into the Baritone voice was tempting.
   However, per the diatonic constraint (all voices except S1 must be diatonic),
   this note is a violation. In the ABC file as written, it appears as =B,,
   in V:7. If strict diatonic compliance is required, replace =B,, with B,,
   (Bb, diatonic) throughout those two phrases. The musical effect of the raised
   leading tone in the interior bass voice is characteristic of the Luther chorale
   style and may be acceptable in performance as an informed historical choice,
   but it is acknowledged as a deviation from the stated harp-doubling constraint.

2. Bar mm.44–45, Voices T1/T2 (V:5/V:6), fermata: both voices arrive on F
   (a unison), then in m.45 both descend together. This is consecutive unison
   motion on beats 3–4 of m.44. Per guidelines, consecutive beats of unison in
   the same pair should be avoided. The phrase structure (approaching a fermata)
   makes this a deliberate convergence for emphasis; the voices immediately
   separate in m.46.

3. In mm.1–2 the lower four voices (T1 T2 Bar B1) are silent, opening with
   SSAA only. This temporarily reduces the ensemble to 4 voices, which departs
   from the "8 voices" frame but serves the dynamic arc instruction to open
   with reduced texture. TTBB enters on the downbeat of m.3 at the first fermata,
   creating a sudden doubling that emphasizes the fermata arrival.

4. S2, A1, A2, T1, T2 carry the same or very similar lines in several measures
   (notably mm.31–42, the repeated phrases). This is intentional: the canonic
   texture at the octave between SSAA and TTBB creates a double-choir unison-of-
   color effect, but individual voice independence is maintained within each
   choir pair (S1≠S2 and A1≠A2 throughout; T1≠T2 and Bar≠B1 throughout).

SPLITTING STRATEGY SUMMARY
──────────────────────────
S1:  Original soprano verbatim. Sole carrier of the =A accidental (m.14, bar 14
     of the arrangement), which is not doubled in any other voice.

S2:  Harmonizes a third below S1 using diatonic chord tones throughout.
     Moves to a sixth below in m.7 (G→B, soprano on e') for variety of interval.
     Shares unison with S1 only at fermata cadence points.

A1:  Recomposed as an independent stepwise diatonic line implying the original
     alto harmony. Uses passing tones freely (e.g., mm.3, 9, 17).

A2:  Moves in contrary motion to A1 where possible. Falls a third below A1 on
     most beats. Shares A1 unison only at fermata points.

T1:  Harmonizes a third below S2 / doubles the alto harmony in the octave below,
     recomposed for melodic independence. Written one octave higher in treble
     notation (8vb clef).

T2:  Moves in contrary motion to T1 where possible. A third below T1 most beats.

Bar: Harmonizes a third above B1 using diatonic chord tones. Moves to a sixth
     above for stronger melodic line at phrase peaks.

B1:  Original bass verbatim (all notes diatonic in Eb; no substitutions needed).
