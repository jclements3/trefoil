HARMONIC SUBSTITUTIONS
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
No full harmonic reharmonisation was required. The original hymn is
fundamentally diatonic in D major throughout. The single chromatic event
(raised leading-tone G# in the tenor, bars 1/3/8) was handled as a
diatonic violation (see below) rather than a chord substitution, because
the surrounding harmony (A major) is itself fully diatonic; only the inner
voice chromatic passing tone was non-diatonic.

The implied harmony at the A-G#-A figure (tenor bars 1, 3) is:
  Original: A major with raised leading tone in tenor (V with #7 colour)
  Treatment: A major harmony retained; G# passing tone in inner voice
             replaced by G natural (scalar passing tone A→G→A, step-wise).
  The characteristic sonority of A major (A C# E) is preserved in the
  surrounding voices; the passing-note colour shifts from chromatic to
  diatonic without altering the harmonic function.

TEXTURE PLAN
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
The arrangement opens (bars 1-2) with the SSAA choir carrying the melody
and harmony in clear four-part writing, while the TTBB voices provide a
sustained but slightly simpler harmonic support — the effect is a bright,
upper-register presentation of the chorale. Bars 3-4 repeat the same phrase
with the TTBB choir brought forward through slightly varied inner-voice
counterpoint, creating antiphonal dialogue between the two choirs on the
repeat. Bars 5-7 introduce the B phrase with all eight voices in full
homophony, building to the climactic D5 in Soprano I at bar 6 (the highest
note in the piece) with B1 sitting on D2 for maximum register spread.
Bar 8 presents a short sequential passage as a unified eight-voice block
before the final cadence. Bar 9 recapitulates the closing phrase of A in
full eight-voice texture with a broad final cadence (B1 ending on D2,
S1 on D4), achieving maximum ensemble weight at the close.

Texture types used:
  1. Full 8-voice homophony — bars 5 (phrase B entry), 6 (climax), 8, 9.
  2. Antiphonal exchange (SSAA prominent / TTBB prominent alternation)
     — bars 1-2 vs. bars 3-4.
  3. Call and response between outer voices (S1 melody, B1 bass line)
     with inner voices providing harmonic support — throughout, but most
     explicit in bars 1-4.
  4. Stretto-like entrance emphasis — bar 9 final cadence, where S2 and
     T1 echo the S1 melodic fragment a third below and an octave below
     respectively, arriving on the tonic chord simultaneously.

RULE COMPROMISES
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
1. Bar 7, Tenor I (V:5) high note E5 (written) / E4 concert:
   The T1 written range tops out at G5 (G4 concert); E4 concert is the
   comfortable top. The original tenor line ascends to E4 in this bar
   (source S2V1: E2 E2 C2 = E4 E4 C#4 in the B-section). This is the
   original tenor material and represents the climax of the B phrase.
   No rule was violated (E4 is within the stated range), but it sits at
   the comfortable maximum and should be noted for casting.

2. Bar 6, beat 1, Alto II (V:4): F#3 (F,) in treble clef:
   This is the lowest note in A2's line. A2 comfortable bottom is D3;
   F#3 is above that and within range. No compromise, but noted as
   the lowest approach in that voice.

3. Parallel motion in inner voices at bar 6:
   The stepwise ascent D2-A2-B2-c2-d2 in S1 and the corresponding
   third-below in S2 (B,2-F2-G2-A2-B2) create parallel thirds, which
   is standard and desirable. However, A1 and A2 also ascend (A,2-E2-
   D2-E2-F2 and F,2-C2-B,2-C2-D2). The A1/A2 pair also moves in
   parallel thirds with each other, and largely in similar motion with
   S1/S2. This is a controlled homophonic texture at the climax and is
   intentional; the parallel-fifths/octaves rule between adjacent voice
   pairs is satisfied.

4. Antiphonal texture interpretation note:
   Bars 3-4 use the same harmonic content as bars 1-2 with slight inner-
   voice variation. The antiphonal effect is a performance-practice
   suggestion (TTBB choir prominently, SSAA supporting) rather than a
   structural change to the notation. Conductors may realise this by
   dynamic balancing rather than muting any voices.
