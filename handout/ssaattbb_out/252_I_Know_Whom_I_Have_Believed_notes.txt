ARRANGER NOTES
I Know Whom I Have Believed (Daniel Webster Whittle / James McGranahan, 1892)
SSAATTBB arrangement with lever harp
============================================================

TRANSPOSITION
─────────────
Original key:   Eb major (3 flats)
Chosen key:     Eb major (3 flats) — no transposition required
Semitones:      0
Direction:      none

Eb major is a valid lever-harp key (exactly 3 flats). It corresponds to the
lever harp's native storage tuning with three levers raised:

  Lever positions for Eb major (storage = all levers down = Eb):
    Ab string lever: DOWN (Ab natural — storage position)
    Eb string lever: DOWN (Eb natural — storage position)
    Bb string lever: DOWN (Bb natural — storage position)
  All other strings: DOWN (natural, diatonic to Eb major)

In practical terms the harpist begins with all levers in the down (flat)
position; no lever changes are needed during the piece.

All 8 voices remain comfortably within professional ranges throughout.


HARMONIC SUBSTITUTIONS
──────────────────────
None required. The original hymn is entirely diatonic to Eb major throughout
all four voices. No secondary dominants, raised leading tones, modal mixture,
or chromatic inner-voice notes appear in the source. The harp therefore doubles
the inner voices without conflict at every moment.


DIATONIC VIOLATIONS CAUGHT
───────────────────────────
None. Every note in the original SATB is a member of Eb major (Eb, F, G, Ab,
Bb, C, D). No replacement of accidentals was necessary in any voice.

S1 (Soprano I) carries the original soprano melody without modification;
since that melody is also entirely diatonic, the S1 unrestricted-accidental
privilege was not invoked.


TEXTURE PLAN
────────────
The arrangement opens with a reduced 6-voice texture (S1, S2, A1, T1,
Baritone, Bass I; A2 and T2 rest). This establishes the hymn's character at
a moderate dynamic without the full weight of eight voices. The second verse
brings all eight voices in, building to a confident forte. The refrain
alternates antiphonally: the SSAA choir carries the first half of each
refrain phrase while the TTBB choir echoes beneath, then all eight voices
unite for the final refrain statement (bars 13–16). The final bar achieves
maximum spread — S1 on Eb5 (climax) against B1 holding the pedal tonic Eb3
— before converging to a root-position Eb major chord.

Texture types used:
  1. Reduced 6-voice homophony (verse 1, bars 1–4)
  2. Full 8-voice homophony (verse 2, bars 5–8)
  3. Antiphonal SSAA / TTBB exchange (refrain A, bars 9–12)
  4. Full 8-voice climax with converging inner voices (refrain B, bars 13–16)


UNISON LOG
──────────
No within-pair unisons occur in this arrangement. Voice pairs are kept in
continuous 3rds, 6ths, or occasionally 4ths throughout. Cross-choir unisons
between A2 and T1 (which sit naturally at the register break around D3–F3)
do not occur because the two parts occupy different pitch classes at every
shared beat; both voices are in independent stepwise motion that happens to
remain separated. No unison was used as a default solution to a voice-leading
problem.


RANGE LOG
─────────
S1 (Soprano I):
  Bar 9/13 (refrain): e = Eb5. This is the climax pitch, appearing first in
  refrain A and again at the start and final cadence of refrain B (bar 16,
  e3 = Eb5 dotted half). Well within professional S1 range; singers should
  support fully on each occurrence. Top of comfortable range but not a concern
  for a trained ensemble.

S2 (Soprano II):
  Bar 9 (refrain A): c = C5 held through the descending ornamental passage.
  Comfortable for professional S2.

A1 (Alto I):
  Bars 10–12 (refrain A): F, = F3, the lowest note of A1's professional range.
  These occur on weak inner beats and resolve stepwise upward. No concern for
  a trained contralto, though conductors may consider slight dynamic support.

  Bar 15 (refrain B): F, = F3 on beat 4 of the penultimate bar, resolving to
  G3 at bar 16. At the cadence — appropriate expressive descent.

A2 (Alto II):
  Bars 11–12 (refrain A): D, = D3, the lowest note of A2's professional range.
  These occur on beats 1 and cadential positions. Manageable for a professional
  section; conductor should ensure full support.

  Bar 15 (refrain B): D, = D3 on beat 4, resolving to Eb3 at bar 16. Same
  situation as A1 — cadential descent, not a concern.

T1 (Tenor I, sounding):
  Verse 1 bar 3: B = Bb4 written = Bb3 sounding. Upper-middle register,
  no concern.
  Sounding range throughout: Eb3–Bb3. Well within T1 comfort zone.

T2 (Tenor II, sounding):
  Bar 12 refrain A: G, = G3 written = G2 sounding. This is the very bottom of
  T2's professional range and occurs at the half-cadence end of refrain A (the
  E B2 / C G,2 figure in T1/T2). The low G2 provides a grounding dominant
  sonority at the phrase division. Acceptable at cadence; conductor should
  ensure basses support.

Baritone:
  Bar 12 (refrain A): B,, = Bb2. Lower-middle bass register, no concern.
  Bar 14 (refrain B): E, = Eb3. Upper baritone, comfortable.

Bass I:
  Bars 9, 13: A,, = Ab2. Low but within professional B1 range (min C2).
  Occurs on the downbeat of each refrain phrase as bass pedal. The Ab2 is a
  strong structural tone (iv root) and should be well-supported.
  The original source notated this as A,, (Ab2) throughout the refrain; it
  has been preserved at its original pitch rather than transposed up an octave,
  preserving the sense of a deep bass foundation.


RULE COMPROMISES
────────────────
1. Sustained octaves, S2 / A1 (refrain, passing moments):
   S2 descends C5→Bb4→Ab4→G4→F4→Eb4 across the refrain while A1 occupies
   the same pitch classes an octave below (C4, Bb3, Ab3, G3, F3). Completely
   strict avoidance of all octave doublings between S2 and A1 would require
   A1 to use entirely different pitch classes, which would force dissonant
   harmonic intervals or very wide spacing inconsistent with the style. The
   arrangement therefore accepts brief passing octaves between S2 and A1 on
   weak sub-beats (eighth-note subdivisions), where both voices pass through
   the same pitch class in contrary or oblique motion. These are conventional
   in 8-voice choral writing at this register density and do not produce a
   perceptible parallel-octave effect in performance.

   Specific instances:
   - Refrain A bar 10, beat 3.5 (eighth note): S2=G4, A1=G3, both converging
     from different directions. Contrary motion; treated as passing.
   - Refrain B bar 14, beat 2–4: S2 holds G4, A1 visits Bb3 (not G3), then
     returns — octave is avoided by having A1 use Bb3 rather than G3.
   - Refrain B bar 13 beat 1: S2=C5, A1=G3 — not an octave (3rd+octave). ✓

2. A2 at range minimum (D3), bars 11–12 refrain A and bar 15 refrain B:
   The harmonic demands of the Bb major dominant chord combined with the
   requirement to remain below A1 push A2 to D3 (the 3rd of Bb major, also
   the 7th of Eb). This is the technical minimum of the A2 professional range.
   The conductor should treat these bars as expressive intensity points rather
   than low-register strain; the notes are musically purposeful (Bb-D pedal
   against the descending soprano).

3. T2 at G2 (refrain A bar 12):
   The half-cadence figure T1=Bb3/T2=G2 sounding places T2 at its absolute
   minimum. This occurs at a structural cadence point where the low sonority
   is musically effective. Accepted as a deliberate textural choice rather
   than a range violation.

4. A1 leap of a minor 6th (verse 1, bar 3):
   A1 leaps from G4 down to Bb3 (minor 6th) within the ornamental passage
   G3/2 G/ A/G/ B,/C/. The leap is followed immediately by a step upward to
   C4, satisfying the rule that leaps of a 6th resolve by step in the opposite
   direction. The leap arises naturally from the voice leading at this bar and
   adds expressive contour to the inner voice.
