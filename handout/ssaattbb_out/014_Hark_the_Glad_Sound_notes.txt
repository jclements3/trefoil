ARRANGER NOTES
Hark the Glad Sound — SSAATTBB Arrangement
Tune: Chesterfield (Thomas Haweis, 1792)
Words: Philip Doddridge, 1735

═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
TRANSPOSITION
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
Original key: G major (1 sharp)
Chosen key:   G major (1 sharp) — no transposition required
Semitones shifted: 0

Harp lever positions for G major (Eb storage tuning, raise levers):
  Ab -> Eb: levers RAISED (Eb strings sharpened to E natural)
  Eb -> Bb: levers RAISED (Bb strings sharpened to B natural)
  Bb -> F:  levers RAISED (F strings sharpened to F# — the key signature sharp)
  F  -> C:  levers DOWN (C remains natural)
  C  -> G:  levers DOWN (G remains natural)
  G  -> D:  levers DOWN (D remains natural)

In practice: raise levers on E, B, and F# strings only.
All 8 voices remain comfortably within professional ranges in G major.

═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
HARMONIC SUBSTITUTIONS
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
The original hymn contains two bars where chromatic inner-voice
accidentals imply secondary dominant (V/vi = A major) harmony.
These were diatonized as follows:

Bar 5, beat 3: Original bass had C#2 (^C, in S2V2), implying
  an A major chord (V/vi tonicizing E minor). Replacement: the
  bass on beat 3 moves to E2 (B1 voice), making the chord Am
  (vi of G major — A C E). The T2 voice (written C4, sounds C3)
  is the diatonic C natural, consistent with Am. Harmonic effect:
  a brief vi substitution for V/vi, smoothly approached from D/A
  on beat 1 and resolved back to D major in bar 6.

Bar 6, beat 3: Original S1V2 (alto) had C#4 (^C in the score),
  again implying A major harmony. Replacement: the diatonic
  neighbor C natural (C4) is used in S2. The harmony on beat 3
  of bar 6 becomes D major (D F# A) with the C# omitted from
  all diatonic voices, leaving a simple V chord approaching the
  half-cadence.

═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
DIATONIC VIOLATIONS CAUGHT
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
All non-S1 voices are restricted to the 7 diatonic notes of
G major: G A B C D E F#. The following original chromatic notes
were replaced:

Bar 5, beat 3 (B1/Bass voice):
  Original: C#2 (^C, in S2V2 of source)
  Replaced with: E2 (diatonic 6th scale degree)
  Reason: nearest diatonic neighbor below C#2 is C2, but C2
  would create a Bass-Baritone ordering problem. E2 (a semitone
  above, then up a minor third) is the next available diatonic
  pitch that maintains Bass I above its lower limit and below
  the Baritone.

Bar 6, beat 3 (S2/Soprano II voice):
  Original: C#4 (^C in S1V2 of source)
  Replaced with: C4 (diatonic natural 4th)
  Reason: C natural is the diatonic semitone below C#4 and the
  nearest diatonic neighbor. The chord context shifts from A major
  to D major with a suspended 7th color, which resolves naturally.

Bar 6, beat 3 (A1/Alto I voice):
  Original implied: if following the original alto's C# tendency,
  A1 would touch C#. The voice was recomposed to use A3 (diatonic)
  as a passing tone away from the chromatic area entirely.

Note: The original S2V3 (pedal bass) had D1 sustained through bars
5-7, below B1's playable range. This pedal was absorbed into B1's
line at D2 (bars 7-8), one octave higher.

TEXTURE PLAN
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
The arrangement opens with SSAA alone (bars 1-3), presenting the
first phrase antiphonally as an upper-choir statement. The TTBB
choir enters on bar 4 with full tutti texture for the second phrase,
creating an immediate dynamic surge. Bars 5-7 sustain the full
8-voice texture with increasing harmonic density as the piece
approaches the second section. Bars 8-10 use 8-voice homophony
with all voices in contrary motion, building toward the climax
in bar 11 where S1 reaches E5 simultaneously with B1 on C2 — the
widest registral spread (over 4 octaves). The final three bars
(12-14) include a 4-3 suspension in S2 (D4-C4-B3) and a 7-6
motion in T1 (C4-B3) at the cadence, resolving to full G major
with Bar and B1 providing a grounded bass fifth (C3-G2).

Texture types used:
  1. Antiphonal: bars 1-3 SSAA vs implied TTBB answer
  2. Full 8-voice homophony: bars 4-7 and 8-11
  3. Contrary motion between outer voices (S1 descends while
     B1 ascends in bars 12-13)
  4. Cadential suspension with stretto-like voice leading in
     bar 14 (S2 4-3, T1 7-6, Bar 4-3 simultaneously)

UNISON LOG
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
Unisons occur only within permitted pairs (S1+S2, A1+A2, T1+T2,
Bar+B1) and are used sparingly:

Bar 14, beat 1: S1+S2 — both approach the final cadence on related
  tones (S1=G4, S2=C4). No unison — this is a 5th. ✓

No within-pair unisons were found in the arrangement. Voices were
kept independent throughout. This was possible because the hymn
melody moves constantly and the harmonizations were designed with
contrary motion in mind.

Cross-choir A2/T1 sharing: bars 9-10 have A2 and T1 sounding
near-unison pitches at the register break (A2 around C4-D4,
T1 sounds C4-D4). This is permitted and aids blend.

═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
RULE COMPROMISES
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
1. Bar 5, beat 3 — B1 (Bass I): The original pedal bass C# was
   replaced with E2 rather than the "nearest diatonic neighbor"
   in strict semitone terms (which would be C natural = C2). C2
   would have placed B1 at its absolute low limit on a weak beat
   (beat 3), which is poor practice. E2 is the next diatonic
   pitch that maintains comfortable range and clear voice leading.
   The Am (vi) harmony on beat 3 is perfectly idiomatic.

2. Bar 12, beat 1 — S1: The original melody has E4 C5 B4
   (interval: ascending minor 7th E4->C5). This is a leap of a
   7th, which per the rules requires stepwise resolution in the
   opposite direction. C5 proceeds to B4 (step down), satisfying
   the resolution requirement. The leap is preserved from the
   original soprano and is a characteristic gesture of the tune.
   All other voices move by step at this point.

3. Bar 14 — A1 sustained G3: The final chord has A1 holding G3
   (the root of G major) for 3 beats. While this creates oblique
   motion (not the preferred contrary motion), it provides a
   stable anchor at the final cadence and prevents voice crossing
   with A2 (D3). This is a deliberate choice for textural
   stability at the close.

4. Bar 3 — A1: The phrase ends with A1 descending to A3, which
   is within range but approaches the lower end. This descent
   (E4-C4-A3) mirrors the soprano's descent (B4-C5-A4) at the
   interval of a sixth below, providing good contrary coloring.
   No range concern.
