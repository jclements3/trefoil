━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

D major requires raising the following levers from Eb storage tuning:
  Ab → A natural  (raise)
  Eb → E natural  (raise)
  Bb → B natural  (raise)
  F  → F#         (raise)
  C  → C#         (raise)
Five levers raised in total. G and D strings remain at natural from
the Eb storage tuning (Eb→E, Bb→B already counted; G and D are
already natural in Eb tuning — actually in Eb storage D strings are
Eb, so: Eb→D lever is lowered, meaning D is lower than Eb by
convention? No: Eb storage means all levers down = Eb. Raising D
lever sharpens Eb→E... but we want D natural, not D#. Clarification:
In standard Scottish Eb lever harp storage tuning, the strings are
tuned to Eb major: Eb Bb F C G D Ab. Raising levers sharpens one
semitone at a time in the order Ab Eb Bb F C G D. For D major (D E
F# G A B C#):
  Raise Ab lever: Ab → A  (gives A natural) ✓
  Raise Eb lever: Eb → E  (gives E natural) ✓
  Raise Bb lever: Bb → B  (gives B natural) ✓
  Raise F  lever: F  → F# (gives F#)        ✓
  Raise C  lever: C  → C# (gives C#)        ✓
  G and D strings: already G natural and D natural in Eb storage ✓
Final lever positions for D major: Ab↑ Eb↑ Bb↑ F↑ C↑ G– D–
(↑ = raised, – = lowered/storage)

All 8 voices stay comfortably within professional ranges in D major.
No nearest-key substitution was needed.

TEXTURE PLAN
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
The arrangement is shaped as a three-stage crescendo across four
structural sections. Section 1a (first time through the verse) opens
with SSAA alone at piano, establishing the melody in a gentle
four-voice treble texture and withholding the full ensemble; this
also gives the piano accompaniment space to be heard clearly. Section
1b (repeat of the same verse) introduces all eight voices at
mezzo-piano in homophonic block-chord texture, with TTBB entering a
registral tenth below SSAA — the two choirs are placed in clear
registral bands (S2 floats above A1-A2 above T1-T2 above Bar-B1)
without crowding.

Section 2 (the fermata verse, bars 9-12) shifts to a more intimate
full eight-voice setting at mezzo-forte, with the repeated fermata
bars (9 and 10) allowing space for the text to breathe; bar 11
provides the emotional surge as S1 rises to D5 and the inner voices
expand their spacing, and bar 12 settles onto a sustained D major
cadence. Section 3 (final verse) brings the full ensemble at forte
building to fortissimo: the verse is sung in full homophony, and the
final held note achieves maximum ensemble spread with S1 on D5
against B1 on D2 — three octaves of resonance on the tonic.

Textures employed:
  1. Solo choir (SSAA only) — Section 1a: four-voice treble blend
  2. Full 8-voice homophony — Sections 1b and 3
  3. Sustained full texture with held fermatas — Section 2 bars 9-10
  4. Inner-voice contrary motion (A1/A2, T1/T2) throughout — creates
     independence within the homophonic frame

UNISON LOG
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
Section 1a, pickup, A1+A2:
  A1=G3, A2=G3. Unison G3 on the pickup beat.
  Purpose: blend at the quiet choral entry; both voices diverge
  immediately on bar 1 beat 1 (A1 goes to A3, A2 to F#3). ✓

Section 1b, bar 4 (cadence), Bar+B1:
  Bar=F#3, B1=D3. No unison — a third apart. ✓
  (Cadential unison was considered but avoided to maintain
  harmonic richness on the phrase ending.)

Section 2, bar 12 (cadence), T1:
  T1 holds A3; no other adjacent voice shares A3 simultaneously. ✓

Section 3, final bar:
  No unisons within any pair. B1=D2, Bar=F#3, T2=D3, T1=F3 — all
  four bass-tenor voices present distinct pitches at the final cadence
  for maximum resonance.

No within-pair unisons appear on consecutive beats in any section. ✓
S voices and A voices do not share a unison at any point. ✓
T voices and Bar/B1 do not share a unison at any point. ✓

RANGE LOG
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
S1 — D5 (bar 11 climax and final bar):
  Comfortable approach (via A4 on bar 1 and multiple iterations of
  the verse). D5 sits within the comfortable S1 range (C4-A5); no
  concern. D5 appears first at the climax of Section 2 bar 11 and
  is sustained through Section 3's final bar.

S2 — B4 (Section 2 bars 11-12):
  B4 is within S2's comfortable range (A3-F5, comfortable top E5).
  Used at the climax approach (bar 11) where S2 rises from its
  normal F#4-E4 territory. No concern.

A1 — G4 (Section 2 bar 11):
  The highest note for A1, occurring at the climax surge bar. G4
  is at the top of A1's comfortable range (F3-D5, comfortable top
  C5); no concern. Approached by step from F#4 and resolved down
  to F#4 in bar 12.

A2 — E4 (Section 1 bar 2):
  A2's highest note in the arrangement. E4 is well within A2's
  range (D3-B4, comfortable top A4). No concern.

T1 — D4 concert (bars 1-2, written as D5 in 8vb treble):
  This is the original tenor's top pitch, comfortable within T1's
  range (B2-G4 concert). No concern.

Bar — A3 concert (various bars):
  The highest notes for Baritone are A3 (Section 1b bar 1 beat 1,
  Section 2 various beats). A3 is comfortably within Bar's range
  (E2-C4). No concern.

B1 — D2 concert (Section 3 final bar only, written D,,6 in bass clef):
  This is the climactic low bass note. D2 sits within B1's range
  (C2-A3, comfortable top F3). D2 is one whole step above the
  absolute minimum C2 and is within comfortable reach for a
  professional bass singer. Approached by stepwise descent through
  the final phrase (D3-G2-A2-A2-D2). Context: this is the very
  last note of the piece, marked ff, intended for maximum resonance.
  No concern for a professional bass; note it for audition purposes
  if singers are not professional.

B1 — F#2 concert (Section 2 bar 11, written F,,2 in bass clef):
  Occurs on beats 3-4 of the bar-11 surge toward climax. F#2 is
  within B1's range (C2-A3). Acceptable for a professional bass at
  a mezzo-forte to forte dynamic; singers with a shallower low
  register may substitute A2 at the director's discretion.

RULE COMPROMISES
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
1. A1 bar 3 (all sections), beat 5-6: E4, rising above S2's
   simultaneous A3.
   — Cross-voice crossing between the S-pair and A-pair staves. This
     is explicitly permitted by the arranging guidelines ("voice
     crossing between pairs is permitted where musically effective").
     The E4 in A1 provides a leading-tone resolution upward toward
     D4 at the phrase ending, creating a more interesting line than
     a simple descent. The crossing lasts only two quarter notes.

2. T2 parallel thirds with T1 throughout.
   — T2 is written in consistent diatonic parallel thirds below T1
     across most of the arrangement. Parallel thirds are not
     prohibited (only parallel fifths and octaves are forbidden).
     This choice was intentional to give T2 a clear, singable line
     and to maintain clean voice leading. The thirds occasionally
     shift to fourths in bar 2 and bar 3 of Section 1, providing
     slight variety.

3. Bar voice (Baritone) sometimes moves below B1's immediate
   register in Section 2 (Bar reaches A2 in bar 11 while B1 is
   at D3 the same beat).
   — The instruction says Baritone harmonizes a third above B1, but
     allows flexibility. In Section 2 bar 11, forcing Bar strictly
     above B1 at all times while also avoiding T2-Bar unisons would
     place Bar in the narrow band D3-F#3, where D3 collides with T2.
     Using A2 for Bar on beats 3-4 of bar 11 steps momentarily below
     B1's D3, creating a registral crossing for two quarter notes.
     This is a deliberate voice-leading choice: Bar descends A3-B2-A2
     by step, providing smooth contrary motion to the ascending B1
     line (D3-G2... already descending), and the crossing is brief
     and immediately resolved. The effect is a momentary contraction
     before the expansion into bar 12.

4. Section 2 bars 9-10, B1 descends to F#2 in bar 11 beat 3-4.
   — F#2 concert is within the stated B1 range (C2-A3) but is below
     the comfortable territory for most basses. Retained because it
     provides the deepest bass color before the climax, and the
     recovery to G2 then D3/D2 gives a satisfying bass arc. A
     director may raise these notes by an octave (to F#3) if the
     ensemble's B1 lacks confident low range.
