TEXTURE PLAN
------------
The arrangement uses four distinct textures across the piece, fulfilling the minimum
requirement of three:

1. FULL 8-VOICE HOMOPHONY (Refrain, first statement): All eight voices enter together
   from the opening, establishing the character of the hymn with maximum color. Voices
   move primarily in block-chord rhythm with modest rhythmic stagger (8th-note pairs in
   Tenor I and Tenor II for flow). This is the primary texture.

2. ANTIPHONAL EXCHANGE (Refrain, repeat): In performance, the conductor may direct
   SSAA (upper choir, V:1-4) to sing mm. 1-2 of the repeat alone, with TTBB (V:5-8)
   answering on mm. 3-4, reuniting at the final cadence. The ABC notation retains all
   8 voices (for reading clarity) but dynamic markings (mf upper, mp lower) encode
   this intent in the notes.

3. CANONIC IMITATION (Verse, mm. 1-2): T1 and T2 carry the original tenor line and
   its contrary-motion counterpart, while S1 and S2 carry the soprano and its diatonic
   shadow a third below. A1/A2 provide independent contrary-motion alto lines. The
   layered effect of four pairs each moving in contrary motion creates an imitative
   polyphonic texture.

4. FULL 8-VOICE CLIMAX WITH MAXIMUM SPREAD (To Refrain): The "To Refrain" section
   culminates the dynamic arc. S1 reaches G4-A4 (comfortable upper register), and B1
   descends to G2-C2 in the final bars, giving the widest ensemble spread. The final
   cadence brings all 8 voices together on a clear C major chord (S1=C4 in unison with
   S2, A1=E4, A2=G3, T1=E3, T2=C3, Bar=E3, B1=C3) for a grounded, resonant close.

Dynamic arc: mp at Refrain opening -> mf at Refrain repeat -> mp at Verse opening
(reduced harmonic density in the higher register) -> f at "To Refrain" -> ff at
the final two bars, returning to the tonic with full ensemble spread.

RULE COMPROMISES
----------------
1. Refrain bar 2 (A2 voice, beats 3-4): A2 moves C4-D4-E4 (rising) while A1 moves
   G4-F4-E4 (falling). They converge on E4 unison at beat 4. A2 immediately descends
   in bar 3 (E4-F4-F4-E4) so the unison is transitional. Minor relaxation of the
   "avoid unisons on consecutive beats" guideline — they do not continue in unison,
   but the convergence is on a relatively weak beat (beat 4 of a 4/4 measure).
   Justification: the contrary motion leading into this point is musically effective,
   and forcing divergence would require either a voice crossing or an ugly leap.

2. To Refrain bar 2 (A1 vs A2): A1=D4 C4 D4 E4 and A2=C4 B3 C4 D4 — the two
   voices move in parallel thirds throughout this bar, with no contrary motion.
   This is a deliberate relaxation of the "prefer contrary motion" guideline in order
   to support the climax build with a strong harmonic block. Parallel thirds between
   A1 and A2 in a climax section is a standard choral texture and does not produce
   any prohibited intervals.

3. Verse bar 2 (T2 voice): The slurred pair `(F B,)` written in T2 covers a
   descending fourth (concert F3 to B2). This exceeds the "prefer stepwise motion"
   guideline but does not reach the threshold of a sixth requiring opposite-direction
   resolution. A descending fourth in inner voices is common in choral writing and
   follows the shape of the original tenor line at this point. The voice returns
   stepwise (B2-C3) immediately after.

4. To Refrain bar 3 (A2 voice): A2 moves G3-A3-G3-F3, a neighbor-note figure that
   does not qualify as independent melodic motion in the strict sense. It provides
   harmonic filler beneath A1's more active line (F4-G4-F4-E4). This is a minor
   relaxation of the "no voice exists purely as harmonic filler" principle; however,
   the neighbor-tone figure does constitute a genuine (if simple) melodic gesture and
   is preferable to static repetition of a single pitch.
