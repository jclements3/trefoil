HARMONIC SUBSTITUTIONS
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Bars 2, 6 (beat 4): Original tenor had G# (^G,) implying a secondary
dominant or raised leading tone toward A minor (V/vi = E major chord
requiring G#). This beat sits over the bass E or G, and the soprano
has B4. The original chord is an E major chord functioning as V of vi.
Substitution: E minor (EGB) — the raised third (G#) is dropped to G
natural (diatonic) in all voices except S1. S1 may ornament freely but
in this arrangement S1 does not pass through G# at this beat either,
keeping the color warm rather than bright. The E minor substitution
preserves the vi-chord tonicization feeling without the cross relation.

Bars 3, 7 (beat 4): Same issue — another ^G, in tenor on beat 4
(bar 3). Same substitution applied: E minor for E major.
In the original, both occurrences create a brief V/vi tonicization.
The diatonic version softens this to a modal iv→I cadential approach,
which suits the contemplative character of the text.

Bar 12 (beat 1 through 3): Original alto had ^C (C# = D♭ enharmonic?).
In C major, ^C = C# does not appear in the key; looking at context,
this is in Phrase B measure 4 (the ^C3 in S1V2). This C# functions as
the raised third of an A major chord (V of IV? or a Picardy-flavored
half cadence on A). Substitution: C natural (diatonic). The voice
moves to C4 in S2 and A1, implying an A minor chord (vi) rather than
A major, which fits the Dorian/modal character of the Passion Chorale.

Bar 16 (beat 1): Original bass had ^C, (C#2) in the final phrase,
functioning again as V/vi (or Picardy third approach). Substitution:
C natural (C,=C2 in bass clef) → diatonic. Bass moves through a
C minor sonority briefly before resolving to A minor at the cadence.

Bar 13 pickup: Original S2V2 (bass) had ^F, (F#2) in the final phrase,
implying a D major chord (V/V approach) or part of a ii#5 progression.
Substitution: the bass line is re-voiced diatonically (G2 F2 E2 D2
descending), implying an A minor approach chord rather than D major.
The F natural (diatonic) gives a D minor color (iv of Am = Dm), which
is a standard diatonic substitute for the secondary dominant approach.

TEXTURE PLAN
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

The arrangement opens with reduced texture (Soprano I plus the TTBB
quartet — five voices) for the first statement of Phrase A, establishing
the melody clearly against a sustained four-voice lower foundation at mp.
The SSAA inner voices (S2, A1, A2) enter for the second statement of
Phrase A in full 8-voice homophony at mf, adding harmonic richness while
keeping the texture familiar. Phrase B employs a quasi-antiphonal
approach: the SSAA choir occupies the upper register with the melody,
while TTBB provides a complementary lower texture simultaneously (not
strictly alternating, but registrally separated), giving the sense of two
answering choirs converging at the phrase climax. Phrase C (the final
section) deploys all 8 voices at full strength, with S1 reaching D5 at
the climactic moment and B1 anchored at A2, maximizing ensemble spread.
The final cadence decrescendos to a quiet A minor close (the original
cadential target), with an implied 4-3 suspension in the inner voices.

Texture types employed:
1. Solo melody with accompanying quartet (Phrase A, first time)
2. Full 8-voice homophony (Phrase A, second time; Phrase C)
3. Registrally separated antiphonal exchange (Phrase B — SSAA upper,
   TTBB lower, both active simultaneously)
4. Stretto-like convergence at the final cadence (all voices arriving
   at the Am chord together with suspension resolution)

emphasis and blend at the A minor cadential close of Phrase B. Usage:
occasional unison for phrase-ending emphasis, as permitted.

Strong beat. Both voices move by step in opposite directions after

RULE COMPROMISES
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

1. Parallel octave approach at bar 2→bar 3 (outer pair S1 and B1):
   Both S1 (B4→C5) and B1 (B2→C3 area) move upward by step into bar 3.
   This creates a compound parallel octave between the outer voices.
   Reason: the original Bach chorale itself has this approach built into
   the harmonic rhythm (the progression pushes strongly toward the C
   tonic), and separating it in an 8-voice texture would require
   counterproductive contrary motion in the bass at a tonal arrival
   point. The inner voices provide sufficient contrary and oblique
   motion to mask the outer-pair parallels in performance. This is
   considered a minor compromise given the stylistic context of the
   Passion Chorale, where the outer voice "octave doubling" on the
   tonic arrival is characteristic of the chorale style.

2. Phrase A reduced texture excludes S2, A1, A2 entirely:
   The instruction to "open with reduced texture (4-6 voices)" is
   satisfied with 5 voices (S1+TTBB). The three SSAA inner voices are
   tacet for the full first phrase. This means SSAA singers have a long
   rest at the opening. For performance, conductors may cue S2 in at
   bar 3 of the first statement if the balance feels thin; the
   arrangement is written to work both ways.

3. Tenor I at B2 (floor) for two consecutive beats in bar 15-16:
   Strictly, spending two beats at the range floor on consecutive
   beats approaches a concern. However, bars 15-16 constitute the
   cadence of the final phrase, and the downward resolution (D3→C3→
   B2) creates a satisfying descending line matching the soprano's
   descent. The B2 is the leading-tone approach to the final A2 in
   bar 16 (T1 moves B2→C3 = step up ✓). Given the professional
   context, this is acceptable.

4. Phrase B Bar and B1 share pickup G2 unison (bar 13):
   At the start of Phrase C (pickup to the final section), V:7 (Bar)
   and V:8 (B1) both approach the downbeat from G2 (Bar's G, pickup).
   However, reviewing the written arrangement: Bar has C (C3) as
   pickup and B1 has A, (A2), so these are NOT in unison at the pickup.
   The G, unison occurs on beat 1 of the first full bar of Phrase C
   (both Bar and B1 on G2 at bar 13 beat 1). See Unison Log above.
   This is acceptable as a brief strong-beat root anchor.

5. A1 pickup in Phrase A second time (G,=G3 vs S1=E4):
   A1 opens on G3 rather than matching the original SATB alto's C4,
   because C4 would create a cross-voice unison with S2 (also on C4).
   G3 is the fifth of the C major chord and an equally valid alto
   entry pitch. The slight gap below S2 at the opening phrase gives
   A1 room to ascend through the phrase. The original alto intention
   (a sustained C4) is not entirely captured, but the harmonic
   function is preserved.
