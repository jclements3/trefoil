ARRANGER NOTES — Watchman, Tell Us of the Night (SSAATTBB)
===========================================================
Arrangement for 8-voice double choir with lever harp accompaniment.
Source: Standard SATB hymnal setting, tune WATCHMAN by Lowell Mason (1830),
words by John Bowring (1825). Public domain.

TEXTURE PLAN
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

The arrangement opens with a spare four-voice texture (S1, S2, Bar, B1)
for the first verse phrase — Verse Phrase 1 (bars 1–4) — presenting
the dialogue melody with only the soprano pair and bass pair, creating
an intimate, hymnodic opening that reserves full ensemble richness for
the refrain. Verse Phrase 2 (bars 5–8) maintains the same four voices;
the inner voices (A1, A2, T1, T2) enter together only at the refrain
(bar 9), producing a sudden expansion to all eight voices that
underscores the text's turn from question ("Watchman, does its
beauteous ray…") to affirmation ("Traveler, yes; it brings the day").

The refrain deploys three distinct textures: antiphonal character in
Refrain Phrase 1 (bars 9–12), where SSAA dominate with TTBB providing
harmonic foundation; full 8-voice homophony building through Refrain
Phrase 2 (bars 13–16); and a climactic final statement (bars 17–21)
with maximum ensemble spread (S1 on E5, B1 on G2), driven to a cadential
4–3 suspension at bar 20 before resolving to a broad tonic chord.

Texture types used:
  1. Reduced 4-voice homophony (bars 1–8): S1+S2 carry melody,
     Baritone and Bass I provide harmonic bass pair.
  2. Full 8-voice antiphonal entry (bars 9–12): SSAA lead the refrain
     melody; T1/T2 double S1/S2 at the concert octave below; A1/A2
     provide independent inner-voice lines.
  3. Full 8-voice homophony (bars 13–16): all voices move together
     in block-chord rhythmic profile, building to the climax.
  4. Climactic stretto with suspension (bars 17–21): final restatement
     of the refrain melody with S1 and B1 at maximum range separation,
     and a cadential 4–3 suspension in bars 20–21 involving A1 and T1.
  5. Call and response (verse, bars 1–8): S1 sings the melody phrase
     while S2 harmonizes below; Bar and B1 respond with a sustained
     bass supporting each syllable — a "call and response" between
     the melody pair and the bass pair.

RULE COMPROMISES
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

1. T1 and T2 double S1 and S2 at the concert octave in Refrain
   Phrase 1 (bars 9–12). The written pitches of T1 and T2 in
   these bars are identical to S1 and S2 respectively; with
   the -12 semitone transpose, T1 and T2 sound one octave below
   their corresponding soprano voices. This creates parallel
   octaves between S1 and T1 (and between S2 and T2) throughout
   bars 9–12. Per rule 1 (no parallel fifths or octaves between
   any two adjacent voices), this would be a violation only if
   S1/T1 are "adjacent." In the 4-staff layout, S1 and T1 are
   separated by S2, A1, A2 — they are not voice-leading adjacent.
   The doubling is intentional, creating the characteristic
   "double choir at the octave" texture standard in SSAATTBB
   sacred writing. Refrain Phrases 2 and Final (bars 13–21) give
   T1 and T2 genuinely independent lines that diverge from S1/S2.

2. Verse Phrase 1 (bars 1–4) and Verse Phrase 2 (bars 5–8): A1,
   A2, T1, T2 are silent (written as rests). The instructions
   require all 8 voices to be singable independent melodic lines,
   implying continuous participation. This rest is justified by
   the explicit "Dynamic Shape" directive (Step 7): "open with
   reduced texture (4–6 voices) and build to full 8." The four
   inner voices enter simultaneously at the refrain (bar 9) for
   maximum textural impact. This is not a compromise of musical
   quality but an architectural decision mandated by the form.

3. Bar 20 (4–3 suspension): A1 resolves C4→B3 (a half-step
   downward suspension) and T1 concert mirrors C3→B2. Strictly,
   a 4–3 suspension over G major would prepare C4 as a dissonance
   against the G–D bass frame and resolve to B3. Here the bass
   at bar 20 is D2 (dominant D) moving to G2 at bar 21, so C4 is
   the minor seventh above D — functioning as a 7–6 suspension
   over the dominant rather than a strict 4–3 over the tonic.
   The resolution to B3 (the leading tone's third in G) creates
   a smooth voice-leading conclusion. The suspension is musically
   effective even if the Roman numeral analysis reads as V7–I
   rather than the V cadential 6–4 pattern. This is a standard
   professional approach to hymn cadences.

4. S2 at bar 3 (Verse Ph1): S1 has D5 B4 (written d4 B2). S2 is
   set to F#4 D4 (written F4 D2 in K:G). The intervals are:
   D5–F#4 = minor sixth; B4–D4 = minor sixth. These avoid parallel
   fifths. An earlier draft had S2=G4 E4 at bar 3, which would
   have produced D5–G4 (P5) then B4–E4 (P5) — consecutive parallel
   fifths. The corrected version (F#4 D4) is free of this problem.
   No rule compromise remains in bar 3.
