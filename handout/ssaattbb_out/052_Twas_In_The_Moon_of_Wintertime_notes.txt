ARRANGER NOTES
Twas In The Moon of Wintertime (Huron Carol — Jesous Ahatonia)
SSAATTBB Arrangement — Lever Harp Accompaniment
=========================================================


TRANSPOSITION
-------------
Original key:    D major (2 sharps — F# and C#)
Chosen key:      D major (2 sharps — F# and C#)
Semitones shifted: 0 (no transposition required)
Direction: N/A

D major is well within the valid 3-flat to 4-sharp range for the lever
harp in Eb storage tuning.

Lever positions for D major from Eb storage tuning (all levers down = Eb):
  A-string lever: UP   (Ab → A natural)
  B-string lever: UP   (Bb → B natural)
  C-string lever: UP   (C natural → C#)
  D-string lever: DOWN (D natural — already correct in Eb tuning)
  E-string lever: UP   (Eb → E natural)
  F-string lever: UP   (F natural → F#)
  G-string lever: DOWN (G natural — already correct in Eb tuning)

Five levers raised; two remain down. The Huron Carol melody and all
diatonic inner voices sit entirely on the resulting D major scale,
with no mid-piece lever flips required.

Note on tonal center: Although the key signature is D major, the Huron
Carol is tonally centered on B minor (Aeolian/Dorian character). The
piece opens, closes, and cadences on Bm. All D major diatonic pitches
apply, and the harp's fixed tuning is unaffected by the Bm modality.


HARMONIC SUBSTITUTIONS
-----------------------
No harmonic substitutions were required. The Open Hymnal setting by
John Murphy (2008) is entirely diatonic to D major. The original SATB
uses only the seven diatonic scale degrees (D E F# G A B C#) in all
voices throughout. No secondary dominants, chromatic passing tones,
or modal mixture appear in the original inner voices.

The A7 chord (A-C#-E-G) that appears implicitly in measures 11-12
(S1 on C#5, S2 on A4, inner voices on G-E) is a fully diatonic
dominant seventh in D major — C# and G are both diatonic scale degrees.
No accidental is required for the harp.

S1 (Soprano I) is unrestricted and may sing accidentals freely per the
arrangement rules. In this setting, however, S1 follows the original
soprano melody verbatim, which contains no accidentals beyond the key
signature. There are no chromatic passing tones to manage in S1 either.


DIATONIC VIOLATIONS CAUGHT
----------------------------
None. Every note in the original SATB is diatonic to D major. The
original S2V1 and S2V2 voices (tenor and bass) are identical throughout
mm. 1-8 and very similar in mm. 9-13, and all pitches remain on the
seven diatonic scale degrees. No inner voice accidentals required
replacement.


TEXTURE PLAN
-------------
The arrangement unfolds in three clearly differentiated sections designed
to create an arc from quiet mystery to triumphant conclusion, reflecting
the trajectory of the Huron Carol text (from the stillness of winter
creation to the proclamation of Christ's birth).

SECTION A (mm. 1-8) — ANTIPHONAL: The lower choir (TTBB) enters alone
for the first four bars, with Tenor I carrying the soprano melody
transposed down an octave. The texture is dark and restrained (mp),
evoking the winter setting of the carol. The upper choir (SSAA)
answers in mm. 5-8 with the same melody in its natural register,
brighter and slightly warmer (mf). Alto I provides contrary-motion
counterpoint against the rising soprano, while Alto II moves in the
opposite direction to Alto I. This antiphonal call-and-response between
the two choirs is the primary texture type of the opening.

SECTION B (mm. 9-13) — FULL 8-VOICE HOMOPHONY: All eight voices enter
together for the chorus ("Gloria..."), swelling from mf to forte. The
voices move in a loosely homophonic texture with gentle stepwise motion
in the inner voices (A1 and A2 descend as a pair in contrary motion;
T1 and T2 elaborate the harmony chromatically through their parallel
thirds). The sustained climax of this section is S1 reaching F#5 (m. 9
beat 2), which is the highest note of the chorus.

SECTION C (mm. 14-19) — FULL 8-VOICE, BUILDING TO CLIMAX: The final
verse begins with full 8-voice texture (forte throughout). Inner voices
A1-A2 and T1-T2 take on greater melodic independence, moving in contrary
pairs that reinforce the harmonic rhythm. The climax falls in m. 18:
Soprano I reaches F#5 (the highest note of the whole arrangement) while
Bass I sustains F#3, spanning four octaves. The final bar (m. 19) cadences
on open Bm (B-D-F# distributed across all voices) with S1 on B4, a
satisfying and spacious conclusion.

Texture types used:
1. Antiphonal exchange (SSAA vs TTBB, mm. 1-8): the defining texture
   of the opening.
2. Full 8-voice homophony (mm. 9-13): all voices together for the
   chorus, creating a sudden increase in mass and color.
3. Full 8-voice with contrary-motion pairs (mm. 14-19): each pair
   (A1/A2, T1/T2) moves independently but in coordinated contrary
   motion, adding polyphonic interest to the final verse.
4. Call and response between outer voices (S1 and B1 in mm. 9 and 18):
   S1's melodic leaps up to F#5 are grounded by B1's sustained bass
   while inner voices provide harmonic support.

RULE COMPROMISES
-----------------
1. Climax register ceiling (bar 18):
   The guidelines call for S1 at G5-A5 and B1 at C2-D2 simultaneously
   for maximum ensemble spread. The Huron Carol's highest melody note
   is F#5, not G5-A5. Transposing the melody up to force S1 to G5
   would alter the original soprano line, which conflicts with Step 8
   (S1 follows the original soprano closely). The maximum spread
   achieved is F#5 (S1) to F#3 (B1) — two octaves — which is the
   full natural spread of this source melody.
   Compromise: Range instructions followed as closely as the source
   melody permits. S1 reaches F#5 at the climax (bars 9 and 18),
   which is the absolute melodic ceiling of this carol.

2. Baritone D4 in bar 18:
   The Baritone's professional top is listed as C4. In bar 18 (climax),
   Bar rises to D4 to support the chord texture under S1=F#5 and T1=D4.
   This momentary D4 falls at the textural and dynamic climax and is
   justifiable as a professional push at a peak moment.
   Compromise: Bar M18 beat 3 = D4, one whole step above the listed
   professional ceiling. Noted here and acceptable for a climax moment.

3. Hidden/stationary octaves between T1 and B1 (bars 1-4):
   When T1 carries the soprano melody (F#3-B3-C#4-D4) and B1 stays on
   B2, the motion T1 B3 against B1 B2 produces a stationary-octave
   arrival (T1 leaps up a P4 to B3 while B1 stays on B2). Similarly in
   bar 15, T1 and B1 both land on C#4/C#3 (octave) by contrary stepwise
   motion. These are hidden or stationary octaves rather than parallel
   octaves, and are acceptable in multi-voice choral writing where the
   interior voices provide harmonic density.
   Compromise: No parallel octaves were introduced; the hidden/stationary
   octaves in the outer TTBB pair are within normal choral practice.

4. Opening texture (reduced to TTBB only rather than 4-6 voices):
   The guidelines suggest opening with 4-6 voices and building to 8.
   This arrangement opens with TTBB (4 voices) and answers with SSAA
   (4 voices), reaching full 8 in bar 9. The antiphonal division is
   itself a texture type (Guideline 7), so the structure satisfies both
   the "reduced opening" instruction and the "antiphonal exchange"
   texture requirement simultaneously.

5. T2 non-third intervals in adjusted bars:
   In bar 9 beat 1, T2 was moved from E3 (diatonic 3rd below T1=G3)
   to D3 to avoid a T-voice/B-voice unison with B1=E3. In bar 17,
   T2 was moved from D3-E3-F#3 to A2-E3-G3 to avoid T-B unisons with
   B1=D3 and B1=F#3. These adjustments use non-third intervals (P4,
   P5) between T1 and T2 in the affected bars. The voice leading remains
   smooth and the harmonic content is preserved; only the strict
   "thirds-below" relationship is relaxed at these specific beats.
