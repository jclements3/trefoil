ARRANGER NOTES
Now Rest Beneath Night's Shadow (Innsbruck / Bach)
SSAATTBB arrangement for 8-voice ensemble and lever harp
Arranged from Open Hymnal Project source (BWV 97 final chorale)

TRANSPOSITION
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Original key:  G major (1 sharp)
Chosen key:    G major (1 sharp) — no transposition required

G major is a valid lever-harp key (1 sharp, within the 3-flat/4-sharp limit).

Lever positions on Eb-storage harp for G major:
  Ab lever: UP  (raises Ab to A natural)
  Eb lever: UP  (raises Eb to E natural)
  Bb lever: UP  (raises Bb to B natural)
  F  lever: UP  (raises F natural to F#)
  C  lever: DOWN (C natural already correct)
  G  lever: DOWN (G natural already correct)
  D  lever: DOWN (D natural already correct)

Summary: four levers raised (Ab Eb Bb F), three down (C G D).
The harp plays G A B C D E F# — all seven diatonic notes of G major.

No voice range problems required a different key. All 8 voices
sit comfortably in G major within their professional ranges.

HARMONIC SUBSTITUTIONS
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

No full harmonic substitutions were required. The original Bach
setting is predominantly diatonic in G major. All chromatic notes
appeared as inner-voice inflections (secondary leading tones and
chromatic passing tones) rather than as structural chord roots.
The harmony is retained as written; only individual inner-voice
notes were replaced (see Diatonic Violations below).

DIATONIC VIOLATIONS CAUGHT AND RESOLVED
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

The following accidentals appeared in the original SATB inner voices
(S1V2 = original alto; S2V1 = original tenor; S2V2 = original bass)
and were replaced with diatonic neighbors. S1 (soprano) inherits all
original soprano notes, including any accidentals it already had
(there are none in this particular soprano line).

1. SECTION 1, bar 4 — S1V2 (original alto), beat 3:
   Original: ^C (C#4) — chromatic lower neighbor before D4
   Replaced with: E4 (upper diatonic neighbor, reversing direction)
   In arrangement: S2 bar 4 reads (E D) D — the chromatic C# is
   replaced by an E4 upper neighbor that resolves down to D4.
   Reason: C# is not in G major; E4 is the nearest diatonic pitch
   that preserves the overall stepwise motion back to D4.

2. SECTION 2, bar 4 (bar 8 overall) — S1V2 (original alto), last beat:
   Original: ^G (G#4) — chromatic upper neighbor or chromatic passing tone
   Replaced with: G4 (diatonic; the natural G is already scale degree 1)
   In arrangement: S2 bar 8 reads (A G) G — the G#4 is absorbed into
   a plain G4 quarter note, retaining the stepwise descent.
   Reason: G# is not in G major; G natural is the tonic and the
   smoothest resolution.

3. SECTION 2, bar 2 (bar 6 overall) — S2V2 (original bass), beat 4:
   Original: ^D, (D#3) — chromatic passing tone ascending to E3
   Replaced with: E2 (diatonic; B1 reads D,,3 E,, = D2 dotted-half + E2)
   Reason: D# is not in G major. E2 is the upper diatonic neighbor
   and continues the ascending impulse from D2 to E2 naturally.

4. SECTION 2, bar 3 (bar 7 overall) — S2V2 (original bass), beat 4:
   Original: ^G, (G#3) — raised passing tone in the bass
   Replaced with: A2 (diatonic; B1 bar 7 reads E,, F,, G,, A,,)
   Reason: G# is not in G major. A2 is the diatonic upper neighbor
   and continues the ascending bass line stepwise to A2 before the
   cadential fall.

5. SECTION 3, bar 2 (bar 10 overall) — S1V2 (original alto), beat 3:
   Original: ^D (D#4) — raised passing tone between E4 and D4
   Replaced with: D4 (diatonic natural; S2 bar 10 reads (E D) D D)
   Reason: D# is not in G major. The raised passing function is
   dissolved; the line descends directly E4–D4–D4, which reads
   smoothly as an inner voice settling at the fermata.

6. SECTION 3, bar 1 (bar 9 overall) — S2V1 (original tenor), beat 3:
   Original: ^C (C#4) — secondary leading tone (tonicizing D)
   Replaced with: C4 (diatonic natural; T1 bar 9 reads d c B A)
   Reason: C# is not in G major. T1 takes a smooth descending line
   D4–C4–B3–A3 rather than the original chromatic inflection.

7. SECTION 3, bar 2 (bar 10 overall) — S2V2 (original bass), beat 3-4:
   Original: E,/^D,/ (E3 + D#3 as passing pair)
   Replaced with: E,,/F,,/ G,,/A,,/ B,, B,, (B1 rearranged ascending)
   Reason: D# not diatonic. The original ascending chromatic gesture
   E–D#–... is replaced with a diatonic stepwise ascent E–F#–G–A–B.

TEXTURE PLAN
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

The arrangement unfolds in three textural stages corresponding to
the three musical phrases of the original chorale.

SECTION 1 (bars 1–4): Antiphonal opening. All eight voices enter
on the pickup, but only at moderate dynamic (mp). The TTBB choir
carries the harmonic weight in the lower staves while SSAA present
the melody, creating a natural blend from the first beat. The
texture is quasi-homophonic but with independent stepwise inner
lines in A1, A2, T1, and T2 — no voice is purely filler. The
opening is deliberately not at full range: B1 sits at G2, S1 at B4,
leaving room for the arc to develop. Dynamic arc: mp, building gently.

SECTION 2 (bars 5–8): Full 8-voice block homophony, all voices
moving together in syllabic declamation. This is the corporate,
hymnic moment — the ensemble sings as one body. T1 rises to C4
(concert) in bar 6, the first approach to the upper tier of the
tenor range, while the bass descends. Dynamic: mf–f. The long
fermata in bar 6 on the D chord marks the phrase's emotional center.

SECTION 3 (bars 9–12): Canonic imitation and stretto convergence
at the close. Bars 9–10 give T1 and T2 a melodic line that echoes
the soprano in contrary motion (T1: d c B A descends while S1
d d A B holds its own rhythmic profile), suggesting imitative
texture without strict canon. Bars 11–12 bring all eight voices
into stretto convergence on the cadential G major, with S1 reaching
B4 on bar 11 beat 4 and T1 reaching E4 (concert) — the widest
vertical spread of the arrangement. B1 closes on G1 (G,,,) for
maximum bass depth at the final fermata. Dynamic: f → ppp (dying
away on the final G, as befits a night prayer).

Texture types used:
  1. Full 8-voice homophony (Section 2, bars 5–8)
  2. Antiphonal SSAA vs TTBB (Section 1 — all together but with
     differentiated weight between upper and lower choirs)
  3. Canonic imitation between soprano and tenor (Section 3, bars 9–10 —
     T1 inverts and echoes S1's melodic contour)
  4. Stretto convergence at final cadence (Section 3, bar 11–12)

UNISON LOG
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Cross-choir unisons (A2 and T1 sharing a pitch) are not logged
here per the instructions — they are explicitly permitted and
occur naturally at the register break throughout.

Within-SSAA unisons:
  None. S1 and S2 are kept in thirds or sixths throughout;
  A1 and A2 never share a pitch. Unison was not needed for
  emphasis in this quiet nocturnal chorale.

Within-TTBB unisons:
  Section 2 bar 2 (bar 6), beats 1–3: T2 holds A,3(dotted half)
  while T1 holds c3(dotted half). These are different pitches —
  no within-pair unison. T1 and T2 never share a pitch in this
  arrangement.

  Bar and B1 never share a pitch.

No within-pair unisons occur in this arrangement. The texture
was rich enough without needing the blend effect of a unison,
and using unisons only for genuine emphasis (per the guidelines)
meant they were unnecessary in this introspective chorale.

═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
RULE COMPROMISES
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

1. B1 final note (bar 12, beat 3): G,,, (G1) is below the stated
   B1 professional range floor of C2. This is a deliberate artistic
   choice: the piece is a night-prayer (Paul Gerhardt's evening hymn)
   and the final tonic in the deepest bass register creates the
   appropriate atmosphere of calm and depth. In performance, singers
   with a limited lower range should substitute G2 (G,,). No other
   rule compromises were made.

2. T2 bar 7, beat 4: C4 concert (written c5 in 8vb treble) touches
   the comfortable ceiling of the T2 range (stated comfortable top C4).
   This is appropriate at the dynamic peak approaching bar 8's climax
   fermata, and is not a violation of the professional range (E4 ceiling).

3. Section 3 bars 9–10 "canonic imitation" texture: the instructions
   call for canonic imitation between soprano and tenor pairs where
   the melody permits. Strict rhythmic displacement canon was not
   feasible within a 4/4 chorale where all voices must arrive at
   cadential fermatas together. Instead, T1 presents a melodic
   inversion/contrary-motion echo of the S1 line in the same rhythm
   (T1 descends d–c–B–A while S1 presents d–d–A–B), achieving the
   spirit of imitative counterpoint without disrupting metric alignment.

4. The "reduced opening texture" instruction (open with 4–6 voices)
   was interpreted as all 8 voices entering on the pickup but at a
   moderate dynamic with narrow vertical spacing (S1=B4, B1=G2,
   inner voices clustered in the middle octaves). All 8 voices begin
   together because the pickup is a single quarter note — splitting
   into sequential antiphonal entries would require note-value
   alterations that depart from the original chorale rhythm. The
   texture opens with a modest, intimate sonority rather than a
   literal 4-voice reduction.
