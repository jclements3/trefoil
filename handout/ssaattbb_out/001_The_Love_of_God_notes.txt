ARRANGER NOTES — The Love of God (SSAATTBB)
===========================================
Arrangement for 8-voice double choir with lever harp accompaniment.
Source: Open Hymnal Project SATB, Frederick Martin Lehman, 1917 (public domain).

DIATONIC VIOLATIONS CAUGHT
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

No diatonic violations to report. All original inner voices in the source
SATB were diatonic in Db, and the one-semitone transposition to C preserves
diatonic status throughout. Every note in the arrangement (S1 included)
belongs to the C major scale. S1's freedom to sing accidentals was not
exercised since the melody itself never requires them.


═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
TEXTURE PLAN
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

The arrangement opens with four voices (S1, S2, Bar, B1) for verse phrase 1
(bars 0–4), providing a clear, hymn-like texture that introduces the melody
without overwhelming the ear. Verse phrase 2 (bars 5–9) adds T1 and T2 (six
voices), building warmth and depth beneath the soprano pair. The chorus
(bars 10–29) uses all eight voices with varied textures: antiphonal SSAA
vs. TTBB trading in chorus phrase 1 (SSAA prominent with TTBB providing
harmonic foundation), full homophonic tutti in phrases 2–3, and a climactic
stretto approach in the final phrase where all eight voices drive together
toward the cadential suspension at bar 28. The dynamic arc moves from a
quiet, intimate opening through progressive enrichment to a broad, confident
close, ending with B1 on the tonic C (C3) in a firm low register.

Texture types used:
  1. Reduced-voice homophony (bars 0–4, 4 voices): S1+S2 carry the melody,
     Baritone and Bass provide harmonic foundation.
  2. Layered entrance / building texture (bars 5–9): T1 and T2 added beneath,
     creating a warm 6-voice pillar that prepares the full chorus.
  3. Full 8-voice homophony (bars 10–19): all voices together in block-chord
     style, SSAA above TTBB with full harmonic support.
  4. Antiphonal / canonic octave doubling (bars 10–14): T1 and T2 written as
     octave shadows of S1 and S2, creating a natural antiphonal richness
     between the upper and lower choirs; the identical rhythmic profile
     emphasizes the canonic relationship.
  5. Climactic stretto / suspension (bars 25–29): Inner voices A1 and A2
     hold a 7–6 suspension (D→B in A1, B→G in A2) over the dominant at bar
     28, while S1 descends stepwise from C5 through the chorus figure,
     arriving on C4 at the final bar to settle the register.

═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
RULE COMPROMISES
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

1. T1 and T2 double S1 and S2 at the octave in chorus phrases 1–4 (bars
   10–29). This means T1 and S1 move in parallel octaves throughout the
   chorus. The rules prohibit parallel octaves between adjacent voices; S1
   and T1 are NOT adjacent (separated by S2, A1, A2), so this is permissible
   by strict interpretation of rule 1. The effect is intentional: it creates
   the characteristic "double choir" organ-like richness and supports the
   canonic octave-doubling texture type. In bar 12-13 the inner voices A1/A2
   provide independent contrary-motion lines that prevent the texture from
   sounding merely doubled. If a stricter reading prohibits any octave
   parallels between non-adjacent outer voices, T1 and T2 should be given
   independent lines derived from the original S2V1 (tenor) skeleton.

2. Verse bars 0–4: A1, A2, T1, T2 are silent (written as rests). The
   instructions say "every one of the 8 voices must be a singable independent
   melodic line," which implies all voices sing throughout. The rests were
   used to create the "open with reduced texture (4–6 voices)" required by
   the Dynamic Shape rule (Step 7). This is a deliberate architectural choice
   and is justified by the instruction's explicit directive to "open with
   reduced texture." Verse bars 5–9 add T1/T2 (6 voices), and the full 8
   voices enter with the chorus, creating the required dynamic build.

3. Bar 28 suspension: A1 holds (D2 B,4) — a tied D4 releasing to B3 — and
   A2 holds (B,2 G,4) — tied B3 releasing to G3. The D→B motion in A1 is a
   downward third rather than a strict stepwise suspension resolution. This
   was chosen for voice leading efficiency and chord-tone clarity at the final
   cadence. A strict 7–6 suspension would resolve D4→C4, but that would
   double the S1 arrival note (C4) too closely. The 3rd-fall to B3 gives A1
   a stronger independent ending pitch.

4. In bars 12, 17, 22, 27 (inner chorus bars with the pattern (G2 E) G
   G3/2 A/), A1 has (E2 C) E D3/2 E/ and A2 has (C2 G,) C B,3/2 C/. In bar
   12, A2 has G, (G3) directly below A1's C (C4), forming a 4th — acceptable
   inner-voice spacing. The A2 line momentarily sits in the T1 register
   (concert G3), which is an allowed cross-choir touch at the register break.
