HARMONIC SUBSTITUTIONS
======================================================================
Bar 13, beat 3 — original harmony: implied G7 (G B D F-natural)
  The original alto voice has =F4 (F natural) creating a dominant 7th
  color over the G bass, functioning as a brief tonicization of C major
  (IV) before the Am in bar 14. The original bass has ^G# (G#3) on
  this beat, creating an augmented approach tone G#→A for the Am chord.

  These two chromatic notes (F-natural and G#) cannot be played by the
  lever harp without a mid-piece lever flip. They are also prohibited in
  the diatonic inner voices.

  Substitution: G7 → G major (I)
  All inner voices (S2, A1, A2, T1, T2, Bar, B1) use F# on beat 3 of
  bar 13 and G-natural on the same beat in the bass.
  Effect: the progression becomes I → vi (Am) rather than I7 → vi.
  This is a standard and effective diatonic substitute; the Am resolution
  in bar 14 retains full dramatic impact without the chromatic color.

  Bars affected: bar 13 only.
  Chord pairs: G7 (original) → G major (arrangement).

DIATONIC VIOLATIONS CAUGHT
======================================================================

  Voice: Alto (original S1V2), note: =F4 (F natural, non-diatonic in G major)
  Replacement: F#4 (diatonic neighbor, one semitone up)
  Reason: F natural is not in G major; nearest diatonic neighbor is F# (one
  semitone sharp). Voice leading is preserved: the alto moves G4→F#4 instead
  of G4→F4, then proceeds to E4 in bar 14 (stepwise descent maintained).

Bar 13, beat 3:
  Voice: Bass (original S2V2), note: ^G,3 = G#3 (non-diatonic chromatic passing tone)
  Replacement: G3 (diatonic, G natural)
  Reason: G# is not in G major. The original G# was an augmented passing
  tone (G→G#→A) acting as a secondary leading tone into Am. Replaced with
  G natural; the bass line now sustains G3 across bar 13, giving a pedal
  point effect before the A bass in bar 14. This is effective and diatonic.

No other accidentals appeared in the original inner voices. The original
soprano's F# (written F in K:G) is entirely diatonic throughout.

Note: bar 13 soprano has no accidentals (d d B = D5 D5 B4, all diatonic).
The =F and ^G# were exclusively in the alto and bass.

======================================================================
TEXTURE PLAN
======================================================================
The arrangement opens with Choir I alone (SSAA, bars 1–4) at mp, creating
an intimate four-voice texture that recalls a small chamber ensemble; this
reserves the full eight-voice sonority as a reward for patient listeners.
Choir II (TTBB) enters in bar 5 with a full eight-voice homophonic texture
(mf), bringing warmth and weight to the second half of phrase one. In the
second phrase (bars 9–12) the two choirs engage in an antiphonal exchange:
SSAA takes bars 9 and 11 while TTBB fills bars 10 and 12, creating a
call-and-response conversation across the two choirs. The final four bars
(13–16) build to a stretto-flavored climax in bar 14 where S1 reaches E5
and B1 sits on G2 (G,,), spanning a compound tenth across the full ensemble;
a suspension in bar 15 (A1 holding F#4 over a D chord) leads to a quiet,
open-fifth cadence in bar 16.

Texture types used:
  1. Reduced four-voice opening (SSAA, bars 1–4)
  2. Full eight-voice homophony (bars 5–8)
  3. Antiphonal exchange between SSAA and TTBB (bars 9–12)
  4. Call-and-response with climax spread (bars 13–14)
  5. Suspension at cadence (bar 15, A1 and T1 resolve downward)

  allowed. No rule relaxed.

  Reason: the original G4 would create a prohibited S/A unison with S2
  (which passes through G4 on the same eighth-note beat). The replacement

  chord) is unaffected.

  Reason: B3 exceeds the B1 professional top (A3). The original hymn bass
  voice (S2V2) ascends to B3 in these bars, which lies above B1's range
  ceiling. The Baritone voice covers B3 in both bars, and B1 provides the
  root G3, giving a complete G major sonority in the bass register without
  range strain.
