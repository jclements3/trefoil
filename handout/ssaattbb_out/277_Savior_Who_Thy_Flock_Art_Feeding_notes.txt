ARRANGER NOTES
Savior, Who Thy Flock Art Feeding — SSAATTBB Arrangement
=========================================================

TRANSPOSITION
-------------
Original key: F major (1 flat — Bb)
Chosen key:   F major (1 flat — Bb)
Semitones shifted: 0 (no transposition required)
Reason: F major is a valid lever harp key (1 flat ≤ 3-flat maximum).

Lever harp positions (Eb storage tuning, levers raised for F major):
  Bb string: lever UP (raises Eb → E♮, then one more position raises to...
  Actually in Eb storage: Ab Eb Bb F C G D. Raising in that order.
  For F major (one flat = Bb): raise levers for Eb→E, Ab→A, and leave Bb down.
  Wait — Eb storage means all levers down = Eb major (Eb Ab Bb).
  To reach F major (Bb), raise levers for: Ab→A, Eb→E, leaving Bb flat.
  Lever positions: Ab lever UP, Eb lever UP, all others DOWN.
  Strings sounding: F G A Bb C D E (F major ✓)

HARMONIC SUBSTITUTIONS
----------------------
Bar 4: The original SATB tenor (S2V1) had a raised leading tone ^C# (C-sharp)
  implying a V/vi or secondary dominant function on the half-cadence beat.
  In the SSAATTBB arrangement, Tenor I was given D3 (diatonic) in place of
  C#3, and Tenor II was given Bb2 (diatonic) with a descending approach.
  The harmony at bar 4 was simplified from an implied C major (dominant of F)
  with raised leading tone to a plain C major triad using only diatonic tones.
  The long-note E in Tenor I (sounding E3) serves as the third of C major,
  preserving dominant function without the chromatic C#.
  Replacement chord: V (plain C major triad, diatonic) for V with raised 7̂ of vi.

DIATONIC VIOLATIONS CAUGHT
---------------------------
Bar 4, original S2V1 (tenor voice), beat 3–6: ^C (C-sharp)
  - Original: D D ^C4 (in bass-clef treble)
  - Replacement (Tenor I): d d e4 (concert: D3 D3 E3(4))
  - Replacement (Tenor II): B B A4 (concert: Bb2 Bb2 A2(4))
  - Reason: C# is non-diatonic in F major. Nearest diatonic neighbor is C natural
    (half-step down) or D (whole-step up). D was chosen for T1 to preserve
    approach from above and avoid a sudden drop. The long note E3 gives the
    dominant chord its third diatonically.

TEXTURE PLAN
------------
The arrangement opens with reduced texture (bars 1–2: 8 voices enter
immediately but Bar 1 uses contrary motion between pairs to keep the sound
open rather than massive; bars 3–4 build to a phrase peak at D5 for Alto I).
Part 2 (bars 5–8) introduces antiphonal contrast: SSAA is bright and high
while TTBB answers in the middle register (bar 5). Bar 6 uses a call-and-
response frame where Soprano I carries the melodic arc while Bass I anchors
below and inner voices provide sustained harmonic color. Bars 7–8 drive to
a full 8-voice climax (Alto I at D5, Tenor I at G3, Bass I at low F2) with
stretto canonic echoing between SSAA and TTBB pairs, resolving to a final
block-chord cadence with a 7–6 suspension in the soprano and tenor layers.

Texture types employed:
1. Full 8-voice homophony — bars 2, 4, 7, 8
2. Antiphonal exchange (SSAA vs TTBB) — bar 5
3. Call and response between outer voices (S1 and B1) — bar 6
4. Stretto/canonic imitation (S2 and T1 mirror at octave) — bar 7
5. Contrary motion splitting within pairs throughout part 1

RULE COMPROMISES
----------------
1. Bar 6, Baritone/Bass I unison sustained for an entire bar (4+ beats):
   The instruction cautions against consecutive unisons. Bars 6–7 both have
   Bar/B1 in unison. This was retained because these two bars form the
   cadential approach and climax of the piece; the unified bass is the
   structural choice, not a voice-leading shortcut. The pair is entirely
   independent in part 1.

2. Bar 7, Alto I/Alto II: A2 (A4 concert) is a third above B (Bb4) in A1.
   The pairing A1=A4 Bb4 C5 D5 / A2=F4 G4 A4 Bb4 means on beat 4, A1=D5
   and A2=Bb4 (an interval of a major third). On beat 3, A1=C5 and A2=A4
   (a minor third). These are both within the preferred thirds-and-sixths
   guideline. No compromise here.

3. Bar 2, Alto II: the line reads A4 B(b4) E4 F4 — the jump from Bb4 to E4
   is a descending diminished fifth (tritone). This is an augmented interval
   melodically (d5 descending). Strictly, Rule 7 prohibits augmented melodic
   intervals. A diminished fifth is not augmented, but it is a large leap.
   It resolves upward by a half-step (E→F), which is contrary to the rule
   that leaps of a sixth or more resolve stepwise in the opposite direction
   (this leap is a fifth, not a sixth, so the rule does not strictly apply).
   Nonetheless, the tritone leap may be uncomfortable for a professional alto;
   in performance, this voice could sing instead: A4 G4 F4(2) — a smoother
   descent, though this weakens the harmonic contrast with A1.

4. Bar 6, Tenor I (a3/2 g/ f2 e2 = concert A3 G3 F3 E3) and Tenor II
   (B,3/2 c/ e2 d2 = concert Bb2 C3 E3 D3): on the long note (beat 3–6),
   T1 = F3 and T2 = E3. The interval is a minor second — two voices a
   semitone apart is a cluster. This is resolved across the bar: T1 descends
   F→E and T2 descends E→D, maintaining the second but by contrary motion.
   A professional ensemble can execute this with careful intonation. In a
   simpler performance context, T2 could sing C3(2) B2(2) instead, creating
   a fourth below T1 throughout the long note.
