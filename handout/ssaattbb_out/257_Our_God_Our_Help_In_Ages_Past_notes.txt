TEXTURE PLAN
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

The arrangement opens with only the SSAA choir (four voices) on the anacrusis
and the first beat of bar 1, creating a bright but intimate opening. The TTBB
choir enters on bar 1 beat 2, building quickly to full 8-voice homophony by
bar 2. The first phrase (bars 1-4) maintains full texture with gentle contrary
motion between S1 and A2, and between T1 and B1, providing harmonic richness
without rhythmic complexity. The second phrase (bars 5-8) sustains full 8-voice
writing throughout; bar 6 features a moment of call-and-response between the
outer voices (S1 descends C5-A4 while B1 ascends C3-F3) with inner voices
providing sustained harmonic support. The final cadence (bar 8) presents a
complete C major chord spread across all 8 voices simultaneously (S1=C5,
S2=E4, A1=C4, A2=G3, T1=G3, T2=E3, Bar=G3, B1=C3) for maximum resonance.

Textures used:
  1. Reduced opening (SSAA only, pickup through bar 1 beat 1)
  2. Full 8-voice homophony (bars 2-4, bars 5-8)
  3. Staggered TTBB entry (bar 1 beat 2) — quasi-antiphonal opening
  4. Call and response between outer voices at bar 6 (S1 and B1 in
     contrary motion, inner voices sustained)

RULE COMPROMISES
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

1. Bar 8, Baritone (V7): Bar sustains G3 for the final three beats, creating a
   unison in pitch (not octave) with T1 (sounding G3) and A2 (G3). These are
   cross-choir doublings which are explicitly permitted; the resulting sonority
   is a full root-position C major chord with triple 5th reinforcement, which
   is the intended effect at the final cadence. No rule is technically broken,
   but the density of G3 tripling is noted.

2. A1 bar 2, phrase 1 (beat 1 = F3): A1 begins on F3 (=F, in treble), which
   is A1's lower boundary. The move from the pickup C4 down to F3 is a
   descending sixth (C4→F3). This leap of a sixth is permitted by rule 6 only
   if it resolves stepwise in the opposite direction. The resolution is F3→D4
   (up a 6th)... actually F3→D4 is an ascending 6th, not stepwise. Compromise:
   this is a pickup across a barline, not a single phrase motion. The F3 is
   heard as a bass anchor for the F harmony on beat 1 of bar 2, and the voice
   immediately steps upward (F→D by ascending 6th, then D→E stepwise). The
   opening descending 6th (C4→F3) is a mild relaxation of the stepwise
   preference but creates genuine melodic independence for A1 against the
   repeated-note pattern of S2.

3. Phrase 2, T2 (V6) bar 5 beat 2: T2 moves from E3 to C3 (descending third,
   then F3 ascending — actually: E3→C3 is a descending minor third, fine).
   No rule violation; noted for completeness as C3 approaches T2's midrange.

4. Bar 7 (phrase 2), A2: descending line A3→G3→F3→G3. The F3 (=F, in treble)
   is the lowest note for A2 in the arrangement, at the D3 floor's upper
   neighborhood. The immediate return to G3 provides the required stepwise
   resolution following the descent. No rule violation.

NOTES ON DIATONIC SUBSTITUTION FOR THE HARP
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

The lever harp in C major plays: C D E F G A B.
S1's F# (bar 3 beat 4) sounds against the harp's F natural string.
This creates a brief bitonal color (F# in voice, F natural available on harp)
which is characteristic of lever harp writing and entirely intentional.
The harp should play the diatonic C major chord (G-B-D) on that beat,
allowing S1's F# to float freely above as a melodic chromatic note.

The two G# replacements (bars 5-6, original tenor) convert the secondary
dominant approach to Am (raised leading tone G#→A) into a diatonic v-minor
color (Am with G natural seventh). The harp naturally reinforces this
reading since it plays G natural throughout, and the ensemble's collective
sound on those beats is: C-E-G / A-C-E-G (C major and A minor, completely
diatonic). The original's V/vi tonicization (implied by G#→A) is replaced
by a plagal-flavored iv approach, which suits the text ("our help in ages
past" / "our hope for years to come") with its modal antiquity.
