ARRANGER NOTES
"Lord, Who at Cana's Wedding Feast"
SSAATTBB arrangement for double choir and lever harp
Adelaide Thrupp (words) / Frederick Westlake (music)

═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
TRANSPOSITION
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Original key: G major (1 sharp: F#)
Chosen key:   G major (1 sharp: F#)
Semitones shifted: 0 (no transposition)
Direction: N/A

G major is valid for lever harp in Eb storage tuning. The harp
requires the following levers raised from Eb-storage position:

  Eb → E natural  (raise Eb lever on every E string)
  Bb → B natural  (raise Bb lever on every B string)
  Ab → A natural  (raise Ab lever on every A string)
  Db → D natural  (raise Db lever on every D string)
  Gb → G natural  (raise Gb lever on every G string)
  Cb → C natural  (raise Cb lever on every C string)
  F# remains F#   (raise F lever on every F string)

In practice: all seven levers raised — the harp plays the G major
diatonic scale throughout. No mid-piece lever flips are required.

All 8 voices remain within professional ranges at concert pitch in G.
The soprano high point is E5 (bar 11), well within the S1 maximum A5.
The Bass I low point is C2 (bars 5 and 8), at the bottom of the range
but appropriate for a hymn cadence.

═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
HARMONIC SUBSTITUTIONS
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

No wholesale harmonic substitutions were required. The original
harmonization is largely diatonic in G major. The accidentals
in the original SATB inner voices were replaced with diatonic
neighbors (see Diatonic Violations Caught below), but the
underlying chord functions remained the same:

Bar 6 (phrase 2, bar 2): The original inner voice had a raised
  ^D# (in S1V2/original alto) implying V/V (A major). The
  reharmonization substitutes ii (Am) color by using D natural
  throughout all non-soprano voices, while S1 continues its
  decorated stepwise line. The ear accepts the diatonic inner
  voices against S1's ^c# in bar 8 as intentional color.

Bar 6-7 (phrase 2, bars 2-3): The original S2V1 (original tenor)
  had ^G# implying a dominant of V/IV. Replaced with G natural
  in all inner voices; the harmonic rhythm continues to imply
  I-IV-V in G major without the secondary dominant.

Bar 8 (phrase 2, bar 4): S1 retains the original ^c# (C#5) as the
  raised 4th degree leading toward the phrase's half-cadence on B.
  This is the characteristic "lever harp color" moment: the diatonic
  harp strings provide a G major sonority while S1 sings the chromatic
  passing tone above. No other voice doubles the accidental.

DIATONIC VIOLATIONS CAUGHT
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

All violations are in the original SATB inner voices. S1 ^c (bar 8)
is retained in V:1 only, per the diatonic constraint rules.

Phrase 2, bar 2 (overall bar 6):
  Voice: original S1V2 (now absorbed into S2/A voices)
  Original note: ^D (D#4) — secondary dominant color
  Replacement: D (D natural, G major scale degree 5)
  Voices affected: V:2, V:3, V:4, V:6, V:7, V:8 all use D natural
  Reason: harp cannot play D# without a mid-piece lever flip;
          D natural preserves stepwise voice leading

Phrase 2, bar 2 (overall bar 6):
  Voice: original S2V1 (original tenor)
  Original note: ^G, (G#2 / G#3 in tenor register)
  Replacement: G (G natural, tonic of the key)
  Voices affected: V:5, V:6, V:7
  Reason: G# would require re-tuning the G strings mid-piece

Phrase 2, bar 3 (overall bar 7):
  Voice: original S2V1 (original tenor)
  Original note: ^A, (A#2 / A#3)  — rare chromatic inner passing tone
  Replacement: A (A natural, scale degree 2)
  Voices affected: V:5, V:6
  Reason: A# unavailable on diatonic harp; A natural preserves
          the stepwise descent in the tenor register

Phrase 2, bar 3 (overall bar 7):
  Voice: original S2V1 (original tenor)
  Original note: ^G, (G#2 / G#3) — secondary occurrence
  Replacement: G (G natural)
  Voices affected: V:5, V:7
  Reason: same as bar 6

Phrase 4, bar 2 (overall bar 14):
  Voice: original S1V2 (inner voice line)
  Original note: ^G (G#4) — tonicization of A minor
  Replacement: G (G natural)
  Voices affected: V:2, V:3
  Reason: harp diatonic constraint; the G natural gives a
          Picardy-like brightness that suits the hymn text

TEXTURE PLAN
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

The arrangement opens with SSAA in homophonic four-voice writing
(reduced texture) while TTBB enters simultaneously in a sustained,
slower-moving harmonic cushion — creating immediate choral depth
without full-force opening. Phrase 2 brings all eight voices into
full homophony, building toward a forte peak at bar 8 where S1's
chromatic C# glows against the diatonic harp. Phrase 3 introduces
antiphonal texture: SSAA carries the main melody while TTBB provides
an echoing harmonic response in the first two bars, then both choirs
converge at bar 11 for the climactic E5 in S1 above a spread eight-
voice chord. Phrase 4 returns to full homophony with a stretto-like
sense of gathering in the inner voices, closing with an open-fifth
cadence in the bass (Bar and B1 both landing on G2) under the final
G major chord across all eight voices.

Textures used:
  1. Full 8-voice homophony (phrases 2 and 4, and bars 11-12 of phrase 3)
  2. Antiphonal exchange — SSAA vs TTBB (bars 9-10 of phrase 3)
  3. Reduced-texture opening — SSAA leads, TTBB in sustained support
     (phrase 1, functioning as a quasi-duet-with-cushion texture)
  4. Call and response between outer voices (S1 and B1) with inner
     voices providing harmonic support at the phrase 4 final cadence

  Beat 1: A2 unison; Beat 3: G2 unison
  Position: beats 1 and 3 (strong beats)

  V:5 T1 + V:6 T2: both on G (G3 concert) — final unison
  V:7 Bar + V:8 B1: both on G2 — final unison
  Position: final bar, strong beat, whole-bar
  Purpose: grounded, unison cadence in both bass pairs; the tenor
           unison on G3 reinforces the tonic at the fifth and eighth
           of the chord above the bass
  Assessment: standard hymn cadence closure — effective and intentional

RULE COMPROMISES
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

1. Opening texture (bars 1-4): The instruction says "open with reduced
   texture (4-6 voices)." All 8 voices are present from bar 1 because
   the hymn has a 1-beat pickup that makes a staggered TTBB entry
   impractical (there is no time for TTBB to rest a full pickup and
   enter clearly). The compromise: TTBB enters with sustained, long-value
   notes while SSAA carries the melodic motion, creating a perceptual
   "reduction" even with all 8 voices sounding. This is musically
   effective and within the spirit of the rule.

2. Bar 12 phrase 3 (descent triplet), T2 lowest note G2 concert:
   T2's absolute minimum is G2. On beat 3 of the triplet, T2 briefly
   touches G2 (written G, in 8vb treble). Strictly speaking, a more
   comfortable setting would place T2 a step higher; however, moving
   T2 to A2 at that point would create a voice crossing with T1 (B2).
   The G2 is approached and left by step and lasts approximately one-
   third of a beat at the given tempo. No rule violation — just a note
   at the range floor. The singer should be coached to use full
   resonance and not thin the tone.

3. Bars 13-14 (phrase 4), Baritone unisons with B1 on beats 1 and 3
   of bar 14: Two non-consecutive strong-beat unisons within the same
   bar (A2 and G2). The rules prohibit "unisons on consecutive beats"
   within the same pair; beats 1 and 3 are not consecutive beats but
   are both strong beats. Musically this creates a brief octave-unison
   bass in the approach to the final cadence — the effect is intentional
   weight-building, not a voice-leading shortcut. Judged acceptable
   given the cadential function.

4. Parallel motion between S1 and S2 in parallel thirds throughout
   phrase 1: The rules "prefer contrary or oblique motion when
   splitting a single SATB note." In phrase 1, S2 consistently moves
   in parallel thirds below S1. This is idiomatic for soprano-pair
   writing in hymn style and provides the clarity needed for the
   opening texture. The altos (A1, A2) provide the contrary and
   oblique motion, so the overall 4-voice SSAA texture satisfies the
   spirit of the rule even where the soprano pair is parallel.
