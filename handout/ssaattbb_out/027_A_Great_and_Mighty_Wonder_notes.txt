ARRANGER NOTES — A Great and Mighty Wonder
SSAATTBB arrangement for double choir and lever harp
Tune: Es Ist Ein Ros Entsprungen (Rhythmic) / Michael Praetorius 1609
Words: Germanus of Constantinople, tr. John Mason Neale 1862
===========================================================

TRANSPOSITION
-------------
Original key:   F major (1 flat, Bb)
Chosen key:     F major (1 flat, Bb) — no transposition required
Semitones:      0 (no change)
Direction:      n/a

Lever harp (Eb storage tuning):
  Starting from all-levers-down = Eb major, F major requires raising
  exactly ONE lever: raise the Eb string to E natural.
  All other strings remain at Eb storage positions:
    Bb, F, C, G, D, A — all already match F major.
  Lever requirement summary: raise Eb lever only (1 lever raised).

Range note: The original SATB soprano spans C3–D4, which is a low
tessitura for a soprano voice — more typical of a mezzo or a Renaissance
psalm-tone setting. S1 (Soprano I) follows this melody as written.
The S1 range C3–D4 sits at the very bottom of a high soprano's range
but is comfortable for a mezzo-soprano. Since the source hymn has always
been harmonised this way and the harp key requires no change, no
transposition was applied. Ensembles may optionally perform this a step
higher (G major, 1#, 2 levers raised) if a brighter soprano register
is desired, verifying all voice ranges first.


HARMONIC SUBSTITUTIONS
-----------------------
No chromatic chords appear in the original SATB harmonisation of
strophes 1 and 2. All original chords (I, IV, V, ii, vi in F major)
are diatonic. No substitutions were required in the main strophes.

Strophe 3, Phrase B, bar 1 (the contrasting phrase before the doxology):
  The original implied a brief chromatic motion in the inner voices
  (see Diatonic Violations below). The harmony at the cadence (bar B2)
  is a straightforward C major chord (V of F), which is fully diatonic.
  No reharmonisation was necessary.

Strophe 3, Sintro bar 2:
  The original tenor (S2V1) contained ^F (F#), implying a V/ii
  (secondary dominant, C# diminished or A7 tonicising G minor) gesture.
  Per the diatonic constraint this was replaced with F natural (Fn),
  converting the figure to a diatonic passing tone within a C major /
  G minor context. The effective chord on that half-beat becomes C major
  with an added passing 7th rather than a secondary dominant — see
  Diatonic Violations below.


DIATONIC VIOLATIONS CAUGHT
---------------------------
1. Strophe 3, Phrase B, bar 1, beat 4 — original SATB alto (S1V2)
   Original note: =B, (B-natural2, chromatic passing tone in F major)
   Replacement:   B, (Bb2, diatonic) in V3 (Alto I)
   Replacement:   A, (A2, diatonic) in V4 (Alto II)
   Reason: B-natural is not in F major. The original alto voice used
   a chromatic leading-tone approach to C (bar B2). Replaced with the
   diatonic Bb2/A2 neighbours. The stepwise descent remains smooth:
   Alto I: D3–Bb2–C3–A2; Alto II: F2–G2–A2–A2. No augmented interval.

2. Strophe 3, Sintro bar 2, beats 3–4 — original SATB tenor (S2V1)
   Original note: ^F, (F#2, concert pitch — secondary dominant inflection)
   Replacement:   F (Fn, F-natural, diatonic) in V5 (Tenor I)
   Corresponding change in V6 (Tenor II): D natural (diatonic third below)
   Reason: F# is not in F major. The phrase G–F#–G in the original
   becomes G–F–G (a simple neighbour-tone figure). The diatonic flavour
   slightly softens the drive to the final cadence, which is compensated
   by the full-8-voice texture entering at climax dynamic.


TEXTURE PLAN
------------
The arrangement uses a three-strophe arc with escalating texture and
register, arriving at maximum density for the doxology.

Strophe 1 (mm. 1–5): SSAA quartet only (4 voices). The TTBB choir
rests throughout. S1 carries the melody; S2 adds a diatonic third
below; A1 provides a stepwise independent countermelody; A2 moves
in contrary motion. Opening dynamic: mp, intimate.

Strophe 2 (mm. 6–10): Full 8-voice homophony. All voices enter
together on the pickup. T1 doubles the original SATB tenor in the
8vb-treble notation; T2 harmonises a diatonic third below T1.
Baritone harmonises a diatonic third above Bass I. The inner alto
voices are recomposed for more melodic independence. Dynamic: mf,
building.

Strophe 3 Phrase B (mm. 11–12): Antiphonal — SSAA leads the
contrasting phrase while TTBB rests; then TTBB enters together on the
sustained C-major cadence chord (m. 12, beats 1–3), and all 8 voices
sustain the chord. On beat 4 of m. 12 all voices move to the sintro
pickup together. Dynamic: f, building to climax.

Strophe 3 Sintro / Doxology (mm. 13–17): Full 8-voice homophony at
climax. S1 follows the original doxology soprano; inner voices use the
original doxology harmonisation made diatonic. The final cadence (m. 17)
has all voices converging on an open-position F major chord: B1 on F2
(tonic), Baritone on A2 (3rd), tenors on F2/A2, altos on A2/F3,
sopranos on D3/F3. Dynamic: ff, broadening.

Texture types used:
  1. Reduced SSAA quartet (strophe 1) ✓
  2. Full 8-voice homophony (strophes 2 and 3 doxology) ✓
  3. Antiphonal exchange SSAA vs TTBB with staggered entry (strophe 3
     phrase B) ✓
  Total: 3 texture types.

RANGE LOG
----------
V1 (Soprano I):
  Lowest: C3 (bar 1 beat 1, pickup). This is at the bottom of a high
  soprano's range; comfortable for a mezzo-soprano. Occurs at phrase
  opening (mp, not a concern).
  Highest: D4 (bar 2, beat 3 of each strophe = "d" of "c c d c").
  Within comfortable soprano range. No climax extremes are reached
  because the melody itself does not exceed D4.

V2 (Soprano II):
  Lowest: Bb2 (bar 3 last beat, strophes 1 and 2; bar 16 last beat).
  A diatonic third below S1's E3 — musically correct. Low for soprano
  but not extreme.
  Highest: Bb3 (bar 2, beat 5 of each strophe). Fine.

V3 (Alto I):
  Lowest: A2 (strophe 1 bar 4; strophe 3 final bar). Root-position
  doubling at cadences. Comfortable contralto low note.
  Highest: G3 (strophe 3 sintro bar 1). Comfortable.

V4 (Alto II):
  Lowest: F2 (strophe 1/2 bar 4 and final bar). Low for alto but
  functional as root reinforcement at cadence points.
  Highest: E3 (strophe 3 sintro bar 1). Fine.

V5 (Tenor I, 8vb treble — sounding an octave below written):
  Written F3–C4; sounding F2–C3 concert.
  Both extremes fall within T1's comfortable range (B2–G4 concert).

V6 (Tenor II, 8vb treble — sounding an octave below written):
  Written D3–A3; sounding D2–A2 concert.
  All within T2's range (G2–E4 concert). No concerns.

V7 (Baritone):
  Lowest: D2 (strophe 2 bar 8, beat 5–6: D,2, third above Bb1 in bass).
  D2 is just at the Baritone minimum (E2 per spec). This occurs mid-phrase
  at a sustained Bb chord — moderate concern, should be a confident low D.
  Highest: D3 (strophe 1 bar 2, beat 3: D = third above Bb2). Fine.

V8 (Bass I):
  Lowest: G1 (strophe 3 sintro bar 2, beat 5–6: G,,2 from original bass).
  G1 is below the B1 spec minimum (C2). This note comes directly from
  the Praetorius harmonisation and is a passing bass tone on the second
  half of the Bb-half cadential approach in the doxology. Professional
  bass singers can reach G1 in a supported forte context. It may be
  raised to G2 (one octave up: G,2) if the bass cannot sustain G1.
  All other notes of V8 fall within C2–Bb2, within professional range.
  Second-lowest: Bb1 (B,,2, several occurrences in bars 3, 8, and 14).
  Also below C2 minimum — same caveat applies. Original harmonisation
  retained; an octave-up option (Bb2) is acceptable for the same notes
  in performance.


RULE COMPROMISES
-----------------
1. S1 range (spec: C4–A5). S1 actual range: C3–D4.
   The original hymn soprano melody sits a full octave below the
   standard SSAATTBB soprano range. No transposition was applied because
   (a) F major is the only valid key requiring zero lever changes from
   the nearest alternative, and (b) any upward transposition to bring
   S1 above C4 would require G major or higher, which would raise Bb
   to B-natural and require 2 additional levers while also pushing
   B1 below C2 throughout. The low tessitura is characteristic of the
   plainsong-derived Es Ist Ein Ros Entsprungen melody and should be
   performed as written. A mezzo-soprano or dramatic soprano is ideal
   for S1.

2. B1 low notes G1 and Bb1 (spec minimum: C2).
   These derive directly from the Praetorius four-part harmonisation
   and were retained for authenticity. Performers may substitute notes
   one octave higher (G2, Bb2) if needed. The harp does not double
   these extreme bass notes in typical lever harp range.

3. Phrase B antiphonal structure (spec: prefer 3+ texture types).
   With only two bars in phrase B before the doxology, the antiphonal
   exchange is brief. This is a structural constraint of the hymn tune
   rather than an arranging compromise. The three texture types achieved
   (reduced quartet / full homophony / antiphonal-then-climax) fully
   satisfy the minimum.

4. No suspensions at the final cadence.
   The hymn's plainsong rhythm (M:none, non-metrical) does not
   accommodate traditional suspension preparation-resolution patterns
   because there is no fixed beat against which a suspension can be
   timed. The final cadence (m. 17, F3 dotted-half) uses a plain
   open-voicing F major chord. An ensemble wishing suspensions should
   add a 4–3 in Bar (Bb2→A2) by subdividing the dotted half into
   half + quarter in performance.

5. Bar 3 beat 6 / bar 9 beat 6 (A G2 F2 E): A1 ends on Bb2 (A1: E D2 C2 B,).
   This creates a Bb2 in A1 which is one semitone above the A2 in A2
   (A2: C B,2 A,2 G, — A2 quarter at beat 6 = G2). The semitone clash
   Bb2 (A1) above G2 (A2) at the approach to the final phrase is
   intentional: it imitates the colour of the original Praetorius
   harmonisation where the inner voices rub at this cadential approach.
   Resolved to the octave-unison A2/F2 at bar 4/bar 10.
