HARMONIC SUBSTITUTIONS
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Bar 4, beat 2:
  Original chord: C major (inner alto voice had Enat making a C major
    triad over C-Bb in tenor/bass, implying a tonicization of F).
  Replacement: Cm7 (C Eb G Bb) — Enat→Eb in the alto restores the
    diatonic minor quality. Voice leading is smooth (Eb moves to F).
  Reason: Inner voice accidentals forbidden; Cm7 is the nearest
    diatonic substitute for C major (V/F → ii of Bb, substitute iv7
    of IV = Cm7). The harp's Eb string produces the correct color.

Bar 6, beat 1:
  Original chord: implied secondary dominant with bass F# (chromatic
    approach to G: F#→F→G in original bass).
  Replacement: F major (F A C) with bass F natural — the chromatic
    passing tone F# in the bass is replaced by diatonic F.
  Reason: Lever harp cannot play F# in Bb major without a mid-piece
    lever flip. The diatonic F creates a clean IV→V progression.

Bar 7, original inner voices:
  The original SATB had =E (Enat) in the alto on beat 1 (implies
    Cm major or C dominant color), and ^F (F#) in the tenor on beat 4
    (chromatic ascent G#→Bb in tenor, a secondary leading tone).
  Replacement: Cm minor substitute throughout bar 7 inner voices.
    Eb is used in place of Enat; G is used in place of F# in tenor
    (the tenor line now reads C C F G, stepwise ascent to cadence).
  Reason: Diatonic constraint. The Cm chord functions well as ii in
    Bb major for the approach to the dominant at bar 8.

TEXTURE PLAN
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Phrase 1 (bars 1–4): SSAA only (4 voices) — a cappella quartet texture,
  intimate and transparent, establishing the melody and harmonic
  language. Homophonic throughout with simple contrary-motion splits
  between paired voices (S1+S2, A1+A2).

Phrase 2 (bars 5–8): Full 8 voices enter together on the repeat of the
  opening music — the antiphonal effect of the upper quartet being
  "answered" by the added lower voices creates a classic double-choir
  gesture. Inner voices (A1 A2 T1 T2) have independent quarter-note
  melodic lines that provide harmonic movement beneath the melody.
  Bar 7 introduces the only contrasting melody (G A/B/ d3/2 c/) and
  all 8 voices carry distinct lines simultaneously — the arrangement's
  richest homophonic block.

Phrase 3 (bars 9–12): Climactic phrase. S1 opens with Eb5–Eb5–D5–Bb4
  (the highest pitches in the arrangement); B1 simultaneously holds
  low bass notes (A2, G2 descending to F2–Bb2). The full ensemble
  spread at bar 9 represents the dynamic apex. Bar 11 introduces a
  brief call-and-response sense: the tenor pair echoes the soprano
  pair's melodic contour a third lower. The final cadence (bar 12)
  uses a 4–3 suspension implied in A1 (E2=Eb4 resolving upward to…)
  and converges on a complete Bb chord with Bar on D3, B1 on Bb2.

Texture types used:
  1. Full 8-voice homophony — phrases 2 and 3 throughout
  2. SSAA solo with TTBB tacet — phrase 1 (bars 1–4)
  3. Antiphonal effect (SSAA opening phrase, then full 8 voices for
     the repeat) — bars 1–4 vs 5–8
  4. Call and response between outer voices (S1 and B1) with inner
     voices sustaining harmony — bar 11

RULE COMPROMISES
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

1. Bar 3 / Bar 5, beats 2–3: cross-pair unisons between S2 and A1
   recur on consecutive beats within each of these bars (Eb4→D4 in
   both S2 and A1 simultaneously). Strictly, this creates two
   consecutive cross-pair unisons. Relaxed because: (a) the unisons
   are cross-pair (not within-pair), which the rules permit freely;
   (b) both voices move away by step after each unison; and (c) these
   are inherent to the tight three-note harmonic cell (D–Eb–F) that
   the original inner voices occupy in Bb major. Recomposing to avoid
   them entirely would require non-chord tones or octave displacement
   that creates worse problems.

2. Bar 7 (phrase 2), Baritone (V7): G,2 → D3 leap (ascending perfect
   fifth). This is within the "no restriction on leaps smaller than
   a sixth" rule, but the melodic jump from a low register to a higher
   one is unusual for inner-voice motion. Accepted because: the G2
   provides the fifth of the Cm chord on beat 1, and the D3 is needed
   on beat 2 to maintain Baritone above the rising Bass I (Bb2).
   The subsequent C3 step-down resolves the leap in contrary motion.

3. Bar 8 (phrase 2), Baritone (V7): G,2 held for two beats (G, G,).
   Repeated notes in a slow-moving inner voice are technically
   acceptable but represent a compromise in melodic independence.
   The alternative (larger leaps to reach chord tones above the
   rising Bass I) would violate the leap-resolution rule more severely.

4. Final cadence (bar 12), Bass I (V8): ends on Bb2, not in the
   C2–E2 "low register" specified for the final cadence. Bb major's
   root does not fall within C2–E2; the lowest available Bb for bass
   voice is Bb2 (B,2). Bb1 (B,,1) is below the professional bass
   vocal range minimum (C2). This is an unavoidable consequence of
   the key. Bb2 still provides a grounded, resonant final tonic.

5. Phrase 1 (bars 1–4): T1, T2, Baritone, and Bass I are completely
   tacet. The "open with 4–6 voices" instruction is satisfied (4
   voices = SSAA), but the TTBB voices enter all at once at bar 5
   rather than being introduced one pair at a time. This was chosen
   for the antiphonal effect: the full 8-voice texture at bar 5
   doubles the weight of the repeated melodic phrase, which is more
   effective dramatically than a staggered entry over 12 short bars.
