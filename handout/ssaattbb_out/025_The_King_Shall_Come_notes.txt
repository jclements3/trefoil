TRANSPOSITION
─────────────
Original key:   Ab major (4 flats: Ab Bb Db Eb)
Chosen key:     Bb major (2 flats: Bb Eb)
Semitones shifted: +2 (up a major second)
Direction: upward, to preserve bass register (equal distance — Ab is 5 semitones
  below Eb, or 2 semitones below Bb; nearest valid keys are Eb (down 5) and Bb
  (up 2); up was chosen because the bass line's lowest note at Ab2 transposed to
  Bb2 stays within B1's range, while transposing down to Eb would send some soprano
  notes too low for phrase climaxes).

Lever positions on harp (Eb storage tuning — all levers down = Eb major):
  Levers raised for Bb major: Ab lever and Eb lever.
  Ab lever raised → gives A natural (all A strings).
  Eb lever raised → gives E natural (all E strings).
  Two levers must be raised. The remaining five strings stay at Eb tuning.

Range check after transposition:
  S1 top: D5 (comfortable); S2 top: Bb4 (comfortable).
  A1 bottom: A3 (comfortable); A2 bottom: D3 (comfortable).
  T1 top: F4 concert (above comfortable E4 — occurs only at climax bar 9, within
    absolute range G4); T2 bottom: G3 concert (within range).
  Bar top: C4 (absolute ceiling — occurs bar 8 beat 4, at climax approach; noted
    below in Range Log).
  B1 bottom: F2 (within absolute minimum C2 — occurs bar 9 beat 2 in section 2).
  No nearest-key skip was required; Bb major accommodates all 8 voices comfortably.

The piece is entirely diatonic in Ab major, and after transposition to Bb major all
original chord progressions map cleanly to the diatonic scale with no secondary
dominants or modal mixture.

DIATONIC VIOLATIONS CAUGHT
───────────────────────────
None. All original inner voices (S1V2, S2V1, S2V2) in the source file are entirely
diatonic in Ab major. After transposition to Bb major every original pitch maps to a
diatonic note of Bb major with no accidental conversion required.

S1V1 (now V:1 Soprano I) likewise contains no accidentals in the original —
the melody uses only scale degrees of Ab major throughout. No chromatic passing
tones, raised leading tones, or modal mixture occur anywhere in the source. As a
result the S1 "unrestricted" allowance was not exercised; the arrangement is fully
diatonic in all 8 voices.

TEXTURE PLAN
────────────
The arrangement employs four distinct textures across its short two-phrase form.
Section 1 opens with SSAA alone on the pickup note, presenting a four-voice soprano-
  alto block; TTBB enters on the first downbeat to establish full 8-voice homophony
  that continues through the first phrase cadence (bar 5 on Bb major). The inner
  voices (A1, A2) move in contrary and oblique motion to S1 while the baritone and
  bass provide an independent walking counterpoint below the original bass line.
Section 2 opens antiphonally: TTBB enters alone on the pickup (Bb3/G3 tenor pair,
  F3/D3 bass pair) — a deliberate echo effect — while SSAA rests. SSAA rejoins on
  bar 7 for the second full-texture phrase, building through bar 8 (T1 reaches F4
  concert, S1 climbs to Eb5) to the final cadence. Bar 9 introduces stepwise
  contrary motion between the soprano and alto pairs as all voices converge on the
  G minor close. Bar 10 gives S1 and S2 a unison G4 for blend at the phrase end,
  with B1 anchoring on G2 for maximum spread.
Textures used: (1) reduced 4-voice SSAA opening; (2) full 8-voice homophony (bars
  1–5 and 7–9); (3) antiphonal TTBB pickup entering against SSAA silence (section 2
  pickup); (4) call-and-response between soprano/alto and tenor/bass register groups
  across the two sections.

RANGE LOG
─────────
Bar 9 (section 2 bar 3), Tenor I (V:5): written f (F5) = concert F4.
  F4 exceeds the comfortable top of T1 (E4 per spec) by one semitone.
  It falls at the piece's climax beat (bar 9 beat 1, the highest moment in section 2)
  and is within the absolute range (G4). Tenors should approach it with a forward
  supported tone. If the section is found taxing, the conductor may drop T1 beat 1
  of bar 9 to d (D4 concert, written D5) — still above T2's d at the same beat and
  harmonically a chord tone of the G minor triad.

Bar 8 beat 4 (eighth note "c"), Baritone (V:7): C4 in bass clef.
  C4 is the absolute ceiling of the Baritone range (E2–C4 per spec). It appears on
  a weak half-beat (the eighth-note pair Bb3/C4 in the B,/C group) at the climax
  approach. Professional baritones will manage this cleanly in context. If desired,
  the C4 eighth may be replaced by Bb3 (the preceding note), keeping the bar on a
  static Bb3 pair — acceptable as a trill-free alternative.

Bar 9 beat 2, Bass I (V:8): F,, = F2.
  F2 is above the absolute minimum (C2) and within B1's stated range. It falls on
  a weak beat and functions as a lower neighbor to Bb2. No concern for a
  professional bass.

Section 2 bar 10, S1 and S2: G4 sustained for 3 beats.
  G4 is mid-range for both soprano voices (comfortable zone). The within-pair unison
  is planned for blend and is not a range concern.

RULE COMPROMISES
────────────────
Section 2 bar 7, Alto I (V:3) beat 1: concert Eb4.
  On this beat Soprano II (V:2) is at Bb4. The interval Bb4–Eb4 is a perfect fourth,
  not a unison — no rule violation. However Alto I at Eb4 briefly sits closer to
  Soprano II than to Alto II (C4 below). This momentary compression of the alto
  pair spacing (A1–A2 = minor third Eb4–C4) is within spec (thirds are the stated
  preference) and is intentional: the alto pair opens outward to a major third (D4–
  Bb3) on beat 2.

Section 1, bar 2 beat 1: T1 (V:5) concert F4, Baritone (V:7) concert Bb3 —
  a perfect fifth apart (F4–Bb3). This is not a voice-leading issue in isolation,
  but the subsequent motion (T1 Eb4→D4, Bar A3→G3) both step down, producing a
  brief parallel descent from P5 (F4–Bb3) to tritone (Eb4–A3). Strictly speaking
  the interval changes (P5 → tritone), so no parallel 5ths occur. Noted here for
  transparency.

No parallel fifths or octaves between any two adjacent voices were introduced.
All 8 voice-leading rules from the prompt were observed throughout the arrangement.
The diatonic constraint (Bb major only, all voices V:2–V:8) is satisfied throughout.
S1 contains no accidentals (the melody is entirely diatonic), so the S1-accidental
  non-doubling rule trivially holds.
