TEXTURE PLAN
-------------
The arrangement opens in full 8-voice homophony (bars 1–2),
establishing the chorale character and the key. Bars 3–4
introduce mild antiphonal coloring: the upper voices (SSAA)
carry the melodic weight while the lower voices (TTBB) provide
sustained harmonic foundation under the chromatic moment in bar 4,
isolating the soprano F# as a solo color against diatonic inner
voices. Bars 5–8 build to full 8-voice climax: bar 6 places
S1 at F5 and the lowest voices at G2–D3 for maximum spread;
bar 8 closes with a 4–3 suspension in S1 (B4 resolving to C5
over a G-major V chord) with supporting suspensions in S2 and
Bar (A4–A4, B3–G3), giving the final cadence a professional
polyphonic finish rather than a plain block chord.

Texture types employed:
  1. Full 8-voice homophony (bars 1–2, 5, 7–8)
  2. Antiphonal weighting SSAA vs TTBB (bars 3–4)
  3. Inner-voice contrary motion (A1 vs A2 throughout;
     T1 vs T2 throughout) creating independent melodic lines
  4. Suspension at final cadence (bar 8, beat 2: S1=B4 over V)
  5. Climax register spread (bar 6: S1=F5, B1=D3)

RULE COMPROMISES
-----------------
1. Bar 4, beat 2 — Dm substitution for D major (V/V):
   The original harmony implies a secondary dominant D major
   (V/V). The diatonic constraint prohibits F# in inner voices.
   The substitution to Dm (diatonic ii) slightly weakens the
   harmonic drive toward G major on beat 3. The soprano F# alone
   preserves the full color of the original, and the Dm in inner
   voices provides a satisfying subdominant preparation for the
   dominant G, which is standard in diatonic hymn writing. The
   compromise is minimal and stylistically appropriate.

2. Bar 1 inner voices — parallel thirds in S1/S2 and A1/A2:
   The paired voices move largely in parallel thirds and sixths
   throughout. Parallel thirds are standard practice in double-
   choir SSAATTBB writing and do not violate the prohibition on
   parallel fifths or octaves. This is intentional and idiomatic.

3. Baritone bar 7 beat 4 — C4 at extreme top:
   The rising Baritone line G3–A3–B3–C4 across bars 7–8 pushes
   to the very top of the voice's extreme range on beat 4 of
   bar 7. This is acceptable at a climax approach and was
   preferred over the alternative (dropping the Baritone down
   and creating an awkward gap between Bar and B1 on that beat).
   A professional baritone will have no difficulty with C4.

4. Bar 3 bass B2/G2 — low approach:
   B1 reaches G2 in bar 3 beat 3 and bar 4 beat 3. This is
   within the stated professional range (C2–A3) but is in the
   extreme low register for a bass voice. At tempo 1/4=100 and
   in C major with no accidentals, a trained bass will sustain
   these pitches comfortably. No rule was relaxed; noted for
   awareness.

5. T2 bar 4 beat 4 — G written (G3 sounding):
   T2 on beat 4 of bar 4 uses G3 sounding (written G4), the
   root of the G7 chord. T1 sounding is B3 (written B4). The
   interval T1–T2 on this beat is a major third (B–G), which
   is consonant. No rule violation; noted for completeness.
