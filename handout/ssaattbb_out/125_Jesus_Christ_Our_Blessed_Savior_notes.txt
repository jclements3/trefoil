TRANSPOSITION
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
Original key: F major (1 flat)
Chosen key:   F major (1 flat) — no transposition required

F major is valid for lever harp (≤ 3 flats). From Eb storage tuning,
the following levers are raised:
  Eb → E natural (the E lever, raising Eb to E)
  Ab → A natural (the A lever, raising Ab to A)
  Bb → B natural (the B lever, raising Bb to B)
Three levers up: E, A, B strings raised. This produces F major
(F G A Bb C D E — but with Bb requiring the B lever DOWN relative to
E-storage... clarification: in Eb storage, all strings are flat.
Raising levers in order Ab→Eb→Bb→F→C→G→D:
  Raise Ab: A natural
  Raise Eb: E natural
  Raise Bb: B natural
These three raises give: F G A Bb→B C D Eb→E = F major. Correct.
Lever positions: Ab lever UP, Eb lever UP, Bb lever UP.
Remaining levers (F, C, G, D) remain DOWN (strings at Eb, Bb, etc.).
Wait — with Eb storage and Bb lever UP, Bb becomes B natural. But
F major needs Bb. Therefore the Bb lever must remain DOWN. Let me
restate correctly:

Eb storage tuning: every string is one semitone flat of natural.
  A-strings = Ab, E-strings = Eb, B-strings = Bb,
  F-strings = E (since F lever at Eb = E... no, Eb storage means
  all levers produce the Eb major scale: Eb F G Ab Bb C D).

To play F major (F G A Bb C D E) from Eb major storage:
  Ab → A: raise Ab levers
  Eb → E: raise Eb levers
  Bb stays Bb: leave Bb levers DOWN
Lever positions for F major from Eb storage:
  Ab levers: UP (A natural)
  Eb levers: UP (E natural)
  Bb levers: DOWN (Bb retained)
  F, C, G, D levers: DOWN (at natural pitch — these are already
    at F, C, G, D in Eb storage? No — in Eb storage they are at
    their natural pitch since Eb tuning only flats the Ab, Eb, Bb
    strings specifically.)

Practical summary: two lever sets raised from Eb storage to reach F:
  Raise: Ab → A, Eb → E
  Leave down: Bb (keeping Bb in key signature)
  All other levers (F, C, G, D) remain at natural pitch.

No transposition was needed. Range check passed for all 8 voices
in F major without adjustment.

HARMONIC SUBSTITUTIONS
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
Bar 11: Original harmony — A major chord (A C# E), functioning as
the Picardy third / raised third of the vi chord. The original SATB
tenor has ^C (C#), making this a major-mode cadence on vi (A major).

Substitution: A major → A minor (vi in F major).
Reason: C# is not diatonic in F major and cannot be played by the
lever harp without a mid-piece lever flip. The replacement A minor
(A C E) preserves the root-position vi chord identity, maintains
the same soprano (A4), alto (C4), and bass (A2) pitches, and
produces a modal Aeolian ending appropriate to the Lutheran chorale
style. The characteristic "question mark" quality of vi instead of
VI adds plaintive depth to the close, which is idiomatic for this
text tradition.

No other bars required harmonic substitution; all other chords
in the original are diatonic in F major.

TEXTURE PLAN
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
The arrangement opens with SSAA alone (bars 1–2), presenting the
chorale melody in a transparent four-voice upper-choir texture that
invites the audience in without overwhelming the ear. The lower
voices enter in stages — T1 and T2 added at bar 3 (six voices),
then full eight-voice homophony from bar 4 — building through the
first phrase climax at bar 5 and settling to a broad F-major cadence
at bar 6. Phrase 2 begins antiphonally: TTBB launches the second
phrase (bar 7) while SSAA holds a sustained F-major chord, then all
eight voices reunite for a sweeping descending-scale gesture across
bars 8–10 that drives to the maximum registral spread at bar 10
(S1 at C5, B1 at C2 — a four-octave span). The final cadence
(bar 11) closes on vi (A minor) with all voices arriving in a
resonant block chord, Bar and B1 in unison on A2 for a grounded,
prayer-like ending.

Texture types used:
  1. Reduced texture / gradual entry (bars 1–3): SSAA alone, then
     adding TTBB in stages.
  2. Full 8-voice homophony (bars 4–6): block-chord texture with
     all voices moving together rhythmically.
  3. Antiphonal exchange (bar 7): TTBB choir moves while SSAA
     sustains, then all converge.
  4. Descending sequence / call-and-response between outer voices
     (bars 8–10): S1 and B1 mark the outer boundaries of a wide
     registral sweep; inner voices fill the space.
  5. Block-chord final cadence with unison in lowest pair (bar 11):
     Bar+B1 in unison for resonant grounding.

RULE COMPROMISES
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
1. Bar 4, S2–A1 voices (V:2 and V:3): On beat 4, S2 = B4 and
   A1 = G4. The interval is a minor third, and both voices arrived
   there by ascending motion from the previous beat (S2: F4→B4 = a
   fourth up; A1: D4→G4 = a fourth up). This produces parallel
   fourths on the weak beat 3→4, which is a standard Renaissance
   and Baroque sonority and not covered by the parallel-fifths/
   octaves prohibition. No rule is technically violated; noted here
   for transparency.

2. Bar 11, final cadence on vi (A minor) rather than I (F major):
   The original hymn closes all previous verses on F major. The
   final cadence of this arrangement lands on A minor as a
   consequence of the diatonic constraint (see HARMONIC
   SUBSTITUTIONS above). This is an intentional reharmonization
   rather than a rule compromise per se, but performers should be
   aware that the ending differs from the chorale original. The
   modal quality of vi is appropriate for the penitential texts of
   verses 6–10 (themes of unworthiness, grace, and surrender).

3. Bar 10, V:7 (Baritone): The line E2 D2 E2 F2 uses only a
   stepwise range of a major second (D2 to F2), which limits
   melodic independence in this voice. This was necessary to keep
   Bar above its floor (E2) while avoiding parallel motion with
   B1's large-interval line (C2 G2 D2 B2). The Bar line serves a
   harmonic-support function at the climax rather than a melodic
   one; this is the one bar where Bar does not meet the ideal of
   full melodic independence. All other bars maintain independent
   melodic character in the Baritone.

4. Bar 9, V:3 (Alto I) and V:6 (Tenor II) have identical written
   note patterns (E2 C A,), which sound an octave apart (E4 C4 A3
   vs. E3 C3 A2 in concert pitch). The cross-choir octave doubling
   is intentional — it reinforces the A1/T2 register-break link —
   but the lack of rhythmic or melodic independence between these
   two voices in this bar is a texture simplification. It lasts
   only one bar and is surrounded by independent lines on either
   side.
