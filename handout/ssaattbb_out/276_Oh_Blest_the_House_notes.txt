  dominant (V/V = F major or F dominant 7th). No harmonic substitution

  rather than a true tonicisation, and no reharmonisation of the inner
  voices was required.

Bar 7, beat 1 (alto): Original alto had E♮ (E natural) as a chromatic
  passing tone (part of a sequential descent). Replaced with F4 (nearest

DIATONIC VIOLATIONS CAUGHT
----------------------------
Bar 7, beat 1, Alto I: Original note =E (E♮4). Replaced with F4.
  Reasoning: E♮ lies outside Eb major. The nearest diatonic pitch moving
  upward is F4; moving downward would be Eb4 (=_E), which the original
  already uses on beat 1.5. Using F4 on the dotted beat and _E (Eb4) on
  the eighth creates a stepwise F4→Eb4 descent, maintaining the smoothly
  falling line of the original while remaining wholly diatonic.
  Original: =E3/2 _E/ G E
  Replacement: F3/2 E/ G E

No other inner-voice accidentals were present in the original SATB.
The Bass I (V:8) follows the original bass verbatim; it contains no
accidentals (all notes are diatonic in Eb).


TEXTURE PLAN
-------------
The arrangement opens with four voices only (SSAA, bar 1) presenting
the chorale melody in close homophony, giving a chamber-like quality
that emphasises the intimate domestic text ("Oh, blest the house").
Tenor I, Tenor II, Baritone, and Bass I enter together at bar 2,
expanding to full eight-voice texture for bars 2–4. The second phrase
(bars 5–9) sustains the full ensemble, building intensity through
bar 7's climax (S1 reaching Eb5 on the _e/ eighth note, B1 descending
to G2–Ab2). A 4–3 suspension in Alto I (F4→Eb4) and a 4–3 suspension
in Baritone (Ab3→G3) ornament the final cadence at bar 9.

Texture types used:
  1. Reduced homophony (SSAA, bar 1) — opening texture
  2. Full 8-voice homophony (bars 2–4) — established texture
  3. Sustained inner voices beneath active outer pair (bars 5–6):
     T1/T2 and Bar/B1 hold more sustained note patterns while
     S1 and the alto pair carry melodic interest
  4. Full ensemble at climax (bars 7–8): all 8 voices active with
     wide spacing (S1 at Eb5, B1 at G2)
  5. Cadential suspension texture (bar 9): A1 and Baritone carry
     delayed resolutions (4–3) over held bass and soprano


UNISON LOG
-----------
No within-pair unisons occur in this arrangement. The brevity of the
chorale (9 bars) and the diatonic constraint provided sufficient harmonic
material to maintain independent motion in all pairs throughout. Unisons
were therefore reserved but ultimately unnecessary given the voice
leading solutions chosen.

Cross-choir: A2 and T1 sounding pitches overlap in register throughout
bars 2–9 (A2 in G3–C4 range, T1 sounding G3–D4). This is intentional
per the A2/T1 register-break blending rule and creates a fused inner-
voice core.

RULE COMPROMISES
-----------------
1. Bar 4, T2 (V:6), written A, (sounding Ab2): Ab2 is above the stated
   T2 minimum G2 but is at the lower edge of comfortable singing. A
   professional tenor can sustain Ab2; no relaxation of the range
   boundary was required but this note is noted as the lowest sounding
   pitch in T2 across the arrangement.

2. Bar 6, S2 (V:2), beat 1: Bb3 (written B,). This is the lowest note
   in S2 across the arrangement (range minimum A3, this is Bb3 = just
   above minimum). S2 then leaps up a major 6th to G4 on beat 2. Per
   rule 6, this leap is permissible provided it resolves stepwise in
   the opposite direction; S2 continues G4→Eb4→Eb4 (downward steps),
   satisfying the resolution requirement.

3. Parallel thirds throughout the tenor pair (T1/T2) are pervasive and
   deliberate. While not a rule violation (only parallel 5ths and 8ths
   are prohibited), the near-constant parallel-thirds texture between
   T1 and T2 reduces the melodic independence of T2. This was accepted
   as a consequence of keeping T2 strictly below T1 and within range
   while maintaining diatonic-only motion. T2 has its own stepwise
   contour within the parallel framework.

4. Bar 9 suspension in Alto I (F4→Eb4): Alto I's final bar departs from
   the original whole-note cadence by splitting into two half notes. This
   is an intentional enhancement per the arranging instructions ("suspen-
   sions at all cadence points are strongly preferred over plain block
   chords"). The departure from the original setting is deliberate.
