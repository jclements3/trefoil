=================================================================
TEXTURE PLAN
=================================================================

The arrangement uses a three-section structure that mirrors the
original: INTRO (Alleluia refrain) + REPEATED SECTION (main body,
sung twice) + DA CAPO INTRO (final Alleluia, full ensemble).

Opening (intro, bars 1-2): SSAA alone — four voices establish the
tonic with an open-textured E-major sonority. This deliberate
reduction from the full ensemble creates contrast and reserves the
entry of TTBB as a moment of expansion. TTBB enter at bar 3 of the
intro on the sustained A-major (IV) chord, adding four more voices
simultaneously for a sudden brightening effect.

Repeated section: four distinct textures in rotation.
  (1) Full 8-voice homophony (bars r1-r4 and r9-r12): block-chord
      texture with all voices moving together, providing weight to
      the main hymn text statements.
  (2) Antiphonal exchange (bars r5-r8): SSAA carries the phrase
      while TTBB provide lower harmonic support in contrary motion,
      then the combined choir converges toward the half-cadence. The
      effect is a call-and-response between the upper and lower
      choirs within a single phrase.
  (3) Contrary-motion inner-voice weave (throughout): A1/A2 and
      T1/T2 move in gentle contrary motion rather than parallel
      3rds, giving each inner line melodic independence.
  (4) Stretto/call-and-response cadence (bars r13-r15): S1 launches
      the final "Alleluia" ascent (B–e) while B1 provides the bass
      anchor, with inner voices converging on the tonic.

Da capo (full 8 voices from pickup): the climax falls on bar 5
(B B e in S1), where Soprano I reaches E5 and the full ensemble
sounds at maximum spread. The final bar (e2) closes with a
sustained tonic sonority across all four staves.

Texture types employed: (1) full 8-voice homophony, (2) antiphonal
exchange (SSAA vs TTBB), (3) contrary-motion inner-voice weave,
(4) stretto/call-and-response at cadence, (5) reduced opening
texture (4 voices expanding to 8). Five of the six listed types
are present.

  written, T2=C#4 written = E3 and C#3 concert). No unisons.
  V7+V8 enter with A3 and A2 respectively. No unisons.
  No within-pair unisons in the intro.

REPEATED SECTION:
  Bar r3, beat 2 — A1+A2: passing through A3 simultaneously
    (V3: C#4-A3-B3 slur; V4: A3-G#3-F#3). Weak beat, transient.
    Both voices move away by step immediately (A1 up to B3, A2
    down to G#3). Accepted as passing-tone convergence inherent
    to contrary motion at this harmonic point.
  Bar r8 — A1+A2: both on G#3 (G,3). Strong beat, phrase-ending
    cadence. Deliberate blend at the A/T register break.
  Bar r12 — same as r8 (phrase 3 mirror): A1+A2 on G#3 at cadence.
  Bar r14 — T1+T2: V5=B,3 (B2 concert), V6=G,3 (G#2 concert) —
    no unison; these are different pitches.
  Bar r15 — final close: V7=G#3, V8=E3. No unison.

  Note: Bar (V7) and Bass I (V8) are written with independent
  lines throughout the repeated section to avoid unisons. The
  Baritone moves primarily in 5ths above B1 (r1: B3 vs E3; r2:
  B3 vs A3) and occasionally in 3rds (r4: G#3 vs B2). No
  within-pair Bar/B1 unisons occur in the repeated section.

DA CAPO:
  Bar 4, beat 3 — Bar+B1: V7=G#3, V8=G#3. Strong beat 3 of the
    bar corresponding to the climax approach. Both land on G#3
    as the harmony moves toward the A/IV chord. Acceptable at a
    phrase-internal emphasis point; both voices move away after
    (V7 to F#3, V8 to E3 in bar 5).
  No other within-pair unisons in the da capo.

=================================================================
RULE COMPROMISES
=================================================================

1. T2 low extreme — repeated section bar r7, beat 3 (E2 concert):
   The (G,F,)E, figure in V6 (T2 written) descends to E3 written =
   E2 concert, below T2's comfortable bottom (G2). This is a single
   descending passing quarter note in a stepwise figure (G#2-F#2-E2
   concert). The alternative — stopping at G#2 or F#2 — would
   break the contrary motion against T1 and create a static line.
   Retained for voice-leading integrity; a professional ensemble
   tenor II can manage a single passing E2.

2. A1+A2 passing unison, bar r3 beat 2 (A3):
   The (E4,C#4),B3 slur in A1 and the (A3,G#3),F#3 figure in A2
   both pass through A3 on beat 2 of bar r3. This is a transient
   passing-tone convergence arising from the contrary-motion voice
   leading at the IV chord, not a deliberate unison placement. Both
   voices move away immediately by step. Accepted as unavoidable.

3. Baritone B3 (bar r1-r2, da capo pickup):
   B3 is one step above the comfortable top of the Baritone voice
   (comfortable to A3). These B3 notes are brief (quarter notes in
   moving lines, not sustained) and fall in a professional context.
   The baritone line descends quickly after each B3. Not a concern
   for a skilled ensemble but worth noting for conductors.

4. Da capo V7/V8 unison on G#3 (bar 4, beat 3):
   Both Baritone and Bass I land on G#3 momentarily on beat 3 of
   the da capo intro bar 4. This coincides with the approach to the
   climax bar (bar 5). The unison is brief (one quarter note) and
   both voices move in different directions after (V7 to F#3, V8
   to E3). Acceptable as an emphasis point at the climax approach.

5. Opening texture uses full-bar rests in all TTBB voices for the
   first two bars of the intro. This creates a 4-voice SSAA opening
   that grows to 8 voices at bar 3. While this is a deliberate
   textural choice consistent with the instruction to "open with
   reduced texture," it means the TTBB voices do not have any
   independent musical interest in bars 1-2. The SSAA voices carry
   all melodic and harmonic content there.
