ARRANGER NOTES
We Give Thee But Thine Own (Hymn 272)
Words: William W. How, 1864 | Music: 'Energy', William H. Monk (1823–1889)
SSAATTBB arrangement for double choir and lever harp

═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
TRANSPOSITION
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Original key: G major (1 sharp)
Chosen key:   G major (1 sharp) — no transposition required
Semitones shifted: 0

G major is a valid lever harp key (1 sharp, within the 3-flat/4-sharp limit).

Lever positions on the harp for G major (Eb storage tuning):
  Ab lever: UP (raise to A natural)
  Eb lever: UP (raise to E natural)
  Bb lever: UP (raise to B natural)
  F  lever: UP (raise to F#)
  C, G, D levers: DOWN (remain natural, not needed for G major)

Only four levers are raised from storage tuning to produce G major:
Ab→A, Eb→E, Bb→B, F→F#. No mid-piece lever changes are required.

═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
HARMONIC SUBSTITUTIONS
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Bar 3, beat 4 (original): A major chord (A–C#–E) functioning as V/ii
  Original inner voice: Alto C# (C#4)
  Replacement chord:    A minor (A–C–E), the diatonic ii chord in G major
  Reason: C# is non-diatonic in G major and cannot be played by the lever
          harp without a mid-piece lever flip. A minor substitutes naturally
          as the supertonic minor and maintains the stepwise bass motion.

Bar 6, beat 3 (original): E major / B major region (D# in alto, implying
  V/vi = B major or a raised leading tone within an E major chord)
  Original inner voice: Alto D# (D#4)
  Replacement chord:    G major (G–B–D), the tonic chord
  Reason: D# is non-diatonic in G major. The G major chord provides a
          smooth, diatonic alternative at this strong beat; the harmonic
          rhythm from C major (beat 1–2) through G major (beat 3–4) is
          idiomatic and satisfying without the secondary dominant color.

DIATONIC VIOLATIONS CAUGHT
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Bar 3, beat 4 — Alto (original S1V2 = inner voice):
  Original note: ^C = C#4
  Replacement:   C4 (diatonic neighbor: C natural, a semitone lower)
  Context: Bass A2, Soprano E4 — Am chord rather than A major

Bar 6, beat 3 — Alto (original S1V2 = inner voice):
  Original note: ^D = D#4
  Replacement:   D4 (diatonic neighbor: D natural, a semitone lower)
  Context: Bass B2, Soprano G4 — G major chord rather than B major / E major

Both replacements are assigned exclusively to inner voices (A1, A2) in the
SSAATTBB arrangement. S1 (Soprano I) retains freedom for all accidentals
per the diatonic constraint rules, but the original soprano line in this
hymn contains no accidentals.

═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
TEXTURE PLAN
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

The arrangement unfolds in two contrasting phases matching the hymn's two
phrases. Phrase 1 (pickup + bars 1–4) opens with reduced texture: SSAA
enter on the pickup beat with TTBB joining at bar 2, building to six voices
by bar 2 and seven by bar 3. The effect is a gradual gathering of the
ensemble, modest and devotional in character, with S1 carrying the melody
in an exposed, quasi-solo role above sustained inner harmonies.

Phrase 2 (pickup2 + bars 5–8) deploys all eight voices for the full
duration, delivering the textural climax at bar 7 where S1 reaches C5 and
B1 descends to G2 (two and a half octaves of spread). The final cadence
(bar 8) converges on a full G-major chord with B1 on the root G2.

Texture types used:
  1. Reduced homophony (SSAA, pickup + bars 1–2): melody with close
     inner-voice support; TTBB tacet creates an intimate, a cappella
     chorale opening.
  2. Full 8-voice homophony (bars 5–8): all voices move together in
     broadly parallel rhythm, building ensemble weight for the climax.
  3. Contrary motion between paired voices (A1/A2, T1/T2 throughout):
     inner voices frequently move in contrary directions, giving each
     pair genuine melodic independence while maintaining harmonic clarity.

═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
UNISON LOG
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

No within-pair unisons occur in this arrangement. The voices remain at
least a second apart at all times within each pair. The final cadence
(bar 8) converges on a unison G among T1 and T2 in the sense that both
resolve to their respective Bs and G2, but these are different pitch
classes (B3 vs G2 sounding), not a unison.

Note: The final bar has all voices on tonic-chord pitches (G B D) spread
across the full range — not a unison but a full-voiced G major chord.

RANGE LOG
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

S1, bar 1 beat 1: C5 — comfortable upper register; phrase opening pitch.
S1, bar 7 beat 1: C5 — climax peak for S1 in this arrangement. The
  original hymn melody peaks at C5, which is within S1's comfortable
  range (top G5 comfortable, A5 for climax only). This hymn does not
  drive S1 to the extreme high register; C5 is moderate and well within
  the professional soprano's comfort zone. No concern.

B1, bar 2 beats 1–3: G3 — the original bass line rises to G3 here
  (original S2V2: G,3), which is the upper end of comfortable bass range.
  B1's comfortable top is F3, and G3 is slightly above this, requiring a
  strong bass section. In a professional ensemble this presents no concern,
  but in amateur performance the passage should be monitored.

B1, bar 8: G2 — the final note sits at the low end of B1's practical
  range (comfortable bottom around C2–D2 for most basses). G2 is above
  the comfortable bottom and entirely within range. No concern.

Bar (Baritone), bar 2 beats 1–3: B3 — the Baritone rises to B3 to remain
  above B1's G3 during bar 2. B3 is within Baritone range (comfortable
  top A3, push to C4). Fine.

T2 (Tenor II), bar 7 beat 1: A2 sounding — the written note is A, (A3 in
  the 8vb treble part), sounding A2. A2 is within T2's range (bottom G2
  sounding). Acceptable at an inner phrase point, not a climax; this note
  appears as both tenors descend toward the final cadence.

═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
RULE COMPROMISES
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

1. Voice crossing between S2 and A1 — bars 2–3 (brief):
   In bars 2–3, A1 occasionally approaches or slightly crosses above S2
   when S2 dips to B3 (bar 3 beat 2, bar 4). The crossing is limited to
   one or two beats and arises because S2 (third below S1) is pulled down
   when S1 descends to D4. The instructions permit cross-pair voice
   crossing "where musically effective." Here the cross-pair contact
   produces a smooth inner-voice weave rather than a jarring collision,
   and both voices move stepwise away immediately. Noted and accepted.

2. S2 bar 7 holds G4 instead of moving to A4 (third below S1's C5):
   At bar 7 beat 1, the strict "third below" principle for S2 would place
   it on A4. However, A4 moving in parallel with B1's A2 would create
   parallel octaves (both approaching A in the same direction). S2 is
   held at G4 (the previous pitch) by oblique motion, breaking the
   parallelism. The resulting interval between S1 (C5) and S2 (G4) is a
   perfect fourth, which is slightly wider than the preferred third but
   entirely acceptable in professional choral writing.

3. Strict "contrary motion" in A2 vs A1 is not maintained at every beat:
   The instruction specifies A2 should move in contrary motion to A1
   "where possible." In bars 1 and 5–6, A2 follows parallel thirds below
   A1 rather than strict contrary motion, because true contrary motion
   at those points would repeatedly create parallel octaves with other
   voices (B1, T2). Parallel thirds between A1 and A2 are preferred over
   broken voice-leading rules involving more critical voice pairs.

4. Dynamic arc is limited by the hymn's brevity:
   This is a single stanza of a short hymn (eight bars plus pickup). The
   "open with reduced texture and build to full 8" arc is implemented over
   just eight bars, which compresses the dynamic shape. A multi-stanza
   performance could assign different stanzas to different textures as
   listed above, expanding the arc considerably.
