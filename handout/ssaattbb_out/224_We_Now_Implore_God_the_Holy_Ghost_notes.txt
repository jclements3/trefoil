TRANSPOSITION
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════

Original key:  F major (1 flat)
Chosen key:    F major (1 flat) — no transposition required
Semitones shifted: 0

F major is fully valid for lever harp in Eb storage tuning. Only one
lever flip is needed (Bb string): raise the Bb lever to produce B-natural
if ever required. All seven diatonic pitches of F major (F G A Bb C D E)
are available with the following lever positions:

  Eb lever  UP   → E natural
  Bb lever  UP   → B natural  (raise for F major)
  F lever   DOWN → F natural  (already in Eb tuning)
  C lever   DOWN → C natural
  G lever   DOWN → G natural
  D lever   DOWN → D natural
  A lever   DOWN → A natural

Lever settings for F major:  raise Eb→E and Bb→Bb (i.e. raise both
the E-string and B-string levers). The remaining five strings stay in
their Eb-tuning positions (Ab→A, Db→D, Gb→G, Cb→C, Fb→F are all
already raised correctly for F major when counting from Eb storage
tuning). In practice: raise the levers for strings B and E; all others
as stored.

HARMONIC SUBSTITUTIONS
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════

Bar 12, beat 3–4:
  Original chord: D major (implied by tenor C# over soprano A, alto E,
                  bass A2 — functioning as V/V, i.e. a tonicization
                  of the dominant C major).
  Replacement chord: F major (F-A-C) with bass motion A2–C3.
  Reason: The raised leading tone C# is non-diatonic in F major and
  would require a mid-piece lever flip incompatible with continuous
  harp accompaniment. The substitution preserves the I→IV→I harmonic
  rhythm of the phrase and avoids the secondary dominant. The soprano
  (S1, unrestricted) retains its original diatonic notes G–A here; no
  chromatic note was present in S1 at this bar.

Bar 16, beat 3–4 (alto =B natural in original):
  Original harmony: implied modal inflection — the original alto moved
                    to B natural (Picardy/modal mixture against the
                    otherwise F-major context).
  Replacement: Alto I and Alto II use Bb (diatonic) throughout bar 16.
  The harmonic function is retained as a passing motion within the
  subdominant area (Bb major chord), avoiding the non-diatonic B natural.
  Reason: B natural is unavailable on the harp without a lever flip.

TEXTURE PLAN
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════

The arrangement opens with all eight voices entering simultaneously but
with the upper pair (SSAA) carrying the melody in clearer relief against
a quieter TTBB. The first two phrases (bars 1–9) establish full 8-voice
homophony, with inner voices A1/A2 moving in stepwise ascending and
descending lines to provide melodic independence beneath the soprano pair.

The third phrase (bars 10–14) introduces antiphonal separation: the SSAA
choir presents the dotted-rhythm figure at bar 10 with A1/A2 in contrary
motion (A1 ascending C–D–E–F, A2 descending A–G–F–E), while TTBB shadow
them in rhythmic unison but with different harmonic weight, creating a
call-and-response effect between the two choirs.

The final section (bars 15–19) builds through a rising chromatic texture
(diatonic passing tones in A1/A2 and T1/T2 create a quasi-canonic
ascending line: A1 ascends F–G–A–Bb while T1 maintains the chorale
melody). Bar 17 provides a hushed cadence on C major (all voices in
close position), and bars 18–19 deliver the final cadence with stretto-
like acceleration in the inner voices (A1/T1 ascending through their
phrase peak) before resolution to a sustained F major chord.

Textures used:
  1. Full 8-voice homophony (bars 1–9, bars 15, bar 19)
  2. Antiphonal exchange SSAA vs TTBB (bars 10–14)
  3. Canonic interior movement — A1 ascending against T1 pedal (bar 10)
  4. Call and response between outer voices S1/B1 with inner harmonic
     support (bars 15–17 — S1 carries the melody, B1 anchors the bass,
     inner voices sustain)
  5. Stretto approach at final cadence (bars 18–19 — all voices converge
     in stepwise motion to the final F)

consistently voiced in thirds (or sixths for variety in bars 6–8
between S1/S2 where the thirds invert). This is appropriate for the
solemn chorale character of the hymn. The A2/T1 cross-choir register
meeting occurs at bars 9, 14, 17 where A2=A3 or D4 while T1 sounds
A3 or F3 — these cross-register touchpoints are intentional for blend.

RULE COMPROMISES
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════

Bar 1, voice leading S2 and A1:
  S2 moves E4–E4–D4–D4 (sustained third below S1). A1 moves C4–D4–E4–F4
  (ascending). At beat 2 to beat 3, S2=E4 and A1=D4 (S2>A1 ✓, no
  crossing), then S2=D4 and A1=E4 at beat 4 — A1 rises above S2.
  This constitutes a cross-pair crossing between S2 and A1, which is
  permitted under Rule 3 (voice crossing between pairs is allowed where
  musically effective). The ascending A1 line creates contrary motion
  to S2's sustained descent and is musically justified.

Bar 10, parallel thirds in T1/T2:
  T1 sustains C4 throughout bar 10 (c dotted-quarter, c eighth, c c);
  T2 sustains A3 throughout (A dotted-quarter, A eighth, A A). This
  produces continuous parallel thirds. Rule 6 (stepwise motion preferred)
  and Rule 4 (prefer contrary or oblique motion when splitting) are
  relaxed here because the original tenor itself was a sustained C4
  pedal on all four beats. Splitting into parallel thirds was the most
  natural solution for the inner tenor pair without disrupting the
  established harmonic support.

Bar 17 (all four pairs in parallel thirds, whole bar):
  The entire bar is voiced in parallel thirds within each pair
  (S1/S2 = C4/A3, A1/A2 = E4/C4, T1/T2 = E3/C3, Bar/B1 = E3/C3).
  Rule 1 (no parallel fifths or octaves) is satisfied — all movements
  are in thirds. However, the vertical stacking produces a sustained
  C major chord with relatively little contrary motion for one full bar.
  This is musically deliberate: bar 17 is the moment of deepest repose
  before the final cadential drive, and the static parallel texture
  underscores the hushed character. This compromise was accepted in
  service of the overall dynamic arc.

Note on Bar/B1 in bar 17: Both Bar (E,) and B1 (C,) in bass clef
  produce E3 and C3 respectively. This means Bar and B1 share the same
  pitch-class distribution as T1 and T2 at concert pitch in bar 17
  (T1=E3, T2=C3). The cross-choir T2/Bar unison on C3 is permitted (not
  counted as an intra-choir unison); the T1/Bar meeting at E3 is also a
  cross-choir contact and is similarly exempt. These doublings reinforce
  the resonance of the C major chord at this structural pivot.
