ARRANGER NOTES
"A Lamb Goes Uncomplaining Forth"
SSAATTBB arrangement from SATB original
Music: Wolfgang Dachstein, 1525 / Evan. Lutheran Hymn-Book 1931

═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
TRANSPOSITION
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Original key: F major (1 flat: Bb)
Chosen key: F major (no transposition required)
Semitones shifted: 0
Direction: none

F major is a valid lever-harp key (1 flat only; maximum allowed is 3 flats).
Lever positions required: only the Bb lever engaged (one string per octave
raised from Eb-storage tuning to Bb). No other levers needed.

All 8 voices fall comfortably within professional ranges in F major. No
alternative key was considered.

═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
HARMONIC SUBSTITUTIONS
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

No full harmonic substitutions were required. The original hymn is almost
entirely diatonic in F major. The chromatic notes found (see Diatonic
Violations section) were isolated accidentals in individual voices, each
resolved by a diatonic neighbor replacement rather than requiring a chord-
level reharmonization.

Bars 23–24 (Phrase D): The original harmony implies a brief tonicization of
G minor via the Eb (flatted seventh of F, modal mixture) in the original
alto voice (S1V2). S1 (soprano) has no Eb — it sings G–c | Bb–A throughout
this phrase. All inner voices (A1/A2/T1/T2/Bar/B1) replace Eb with D
(nearest diatonic neighbor). The resulting harmony is a straightforward Dm
or F chord on those beats, preserving the darkened affect without requiring
an accidental in the harp accompaniment.

Bar 27 (phrase D, last bar): Original tenor has ^F# implying a V/ii or V/v
tonicization. Replaced with F-natural in T1/T2; the harmony functions as a
plain tonic pedal over G2 in the bass (a suspended Gm chord), which is
idiomatic diatonic writing.

TEXTURE PLAN
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

The arrangement opens antiphonally: bars 1–2 present SSAA alone (4 voices),
with TTBB joining at bar 3 to complete 8 voices. Phrase A is heard twice—
the first time with relatively plain inner-voice motion, the second time with
freer counterpoint in A1/A2 and T1/T2 to build intensity. Phrase B (bars
13–17) uses a call-and-response texture with outer-voice prominence (S1 and
B1 carry the melodic interest while inner voices weave passing counterpoint).
Phrase C (bars 18–22) introduces canonic imitation between voice pairs, with
a one-bar antiphonal rest in SSAA at bar 19 (TTBB sustain through). Phrase D
(bars 23–27) is the climactic peak: full 8-voice homophony, S1 reaching C5
and the Eb modal color; the texture is the broadest here. Phrase E (bars
28–32) adds stretto-like entries approaching the final cadence, which lands
on a full 8-voice suspension resolution with B1 closing on F2.

Texture types employed:
  1. Antiphonal exchange (SSAA vs TTBB) — bars 1–2, bar 19
  2. Full 8-voice homophony — phrase D and cadence
  3. Call and response between outer voices (S1 and B1) — phrase B
  4. Canonic imitation between voice pairs — phrase C
  5. Stretto entries at final cadence — phrase E approach

RULE COMPROMISES
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

1. Bar 6 / Bar 12 (Baritone, phrase A cadences):
   The Baritone line closes on A2 (A,,) — this is the minor sixth below
   the B1's F2 (F,,). The rule prefers thirds and sixths; a sixth is
   specifically named as an acceptable alternative for variety. No
   compromise required; this is intentional.

2. Bars 23–24, B1 vs original bass:
   Original bass had Eb3 (chromatic). B1 replaces this with D3
   (diatonic neighbor). The resulting B1 line (D3–C3 | G2–D3) differs
   from the original but stays faithful to the bass motion pattern.
   This is a required diatonic substitution per the arranging rules,
   not a compromise.

3. Phrase D, T2 (bars 23–27):
   T2 was composed as a free lower line implying the same diatonic
   harmony as T1 but in contrary or parallel thirds. In bar 25, T1
   sustains Bb3 (sounding) and T2 sustains G3 (sounding) — a parallel
   third through the held bar, which provides a stable harmonic pad over
   the sustained G2 bass note. No unison occurs between T1 and T2 here.

4. Bar 13 (phrase B, T1):
   T1 writes `A2 c c` — sounding A3, C4, C4. The repeated C4 on beats
   3–4 is a sustained pitch. T2 writes `F2 G A` — sounding F3, G3, A3.
   No voice leading rule is broken; this is noted for transparency.

5. Final bar, Baritone (bar 32):
   The baritone closes on A2 while B1 closes on F2 — a major third
   interval, appropriate for a final open voicing. The final note A2
   (A,,) is within Bar's range. The instructions call for the final
   cadence to have B1 on the root in low register (C2–E2 range). B1
   closes on F2 (F,,) — this is the tonic F of F major, which qualifies
   as root position (F=tonic). F2 is within the specified C2–E2 guidance
   range... strictly E2 is the ceiling of that guidance. F2 is one
   semitone above E2. This is a one-semitone relaxation of the low-
   register suggestion; F2 is the actual tonic of the key and is the
   natural bass note for a final F-major cadence. B2 or lower would
   not be tonic.
   [RULE RELAXED: "B1 at C2-E2" guidance relaxed to F2, which is tonic F.]
