ARRANGER NOTES
Jesus Christ Who Came to Save (OpenHymnal #086)
Bach setting expanded to SSAATTBB
================================================================


TRANSPOSITION
-------------
Original key: C major (0 accidentals).
Chosen key: C major (0 accidentals). No transposition required.
C major is diatonic-compatible with lever harp in Eb storage tuning
(7 levers raised: Ab→Eb→Bb→F→C→G→D, i.e. all 7 levers up = C major).
Lever positions for C major: ALL levers raised (all strings sharpened to natural pitch).
No transposition was necessary; the original key is within the valid 0-flat/0-sharp
window and all 8 voices fit within their professional ranges without adjustment.


HARMONIC SUBSTITUTIONS
-----------------------
No full harmonic substitutions were required. The original bass line and
harmonic rhythm were retained intact. Chromatic inner-voice notes were
replaced at the note level (see Diatonic Violations below) without altering
the underlying harmonic function, because in each case the diatonic neighbor
produced an acceptable chord member or passing tone within the prevailing harmony.

Section 1, Bar 1 beat 4 (bass voice original: A,,–C,–E,–^F,):
  The original S2V2 bass note ^F, (F#2) forms a passing tone in the bass
  approaching the G, in bar 2. Replaced with F, (F natural, diatonic) which
  functions as the seventh of a G7 passing chord (C–E–F–A = Am/C with added 6th,
  or IV6 passing to V). The diatonic F, preserves smooth stepwise bass motion
  A,,–C,–E,–F,–G,.


DIATONIC VIOLATIONS CAUGHT
---------------------------
Every accidental in the original SATB inner voices was replaced with its
nearest diatonic neighbor in C major (C D E F G A B). The S1 soprano melody
retains all original accidentals freely.

Section 1:
  Bar 1,  beat 4:  V8 (Bass I), original ^F, (F#2) → F, (F2)
           Reason: ascending passing tone in bass; F natural continues smooth motion.

  Bar 2,  beat 2:  Original alto (S1V2) ^F (F#4) → F (F4) in V2 (Soprano II)
           Reason: S2 must be diatonic; F natural works as chord tone (Dm or V passing).

  Bar 2,  beat 4:  Original alto (S1V2) ^D (D#4) → D (D4) in V2 (Soprano II)
           Reason: D natural fits the prevailing G–B–D harmony; chromatic D# omitted.

  Bar 4,  beat 2:  S1V1 original ^G (G#4) in S1 — retained in V1 only (S1 privilege).
           V2, V3, V4 all move diatonically through G or E; no doubling of G#.

Section 2:
  Bar 6 (section 2, beat 2):  Original alto ^C (C#4) → C (C4) in V2 (Soprano II)
           Reason: C natural functions as root of C major; C# was an ornamental
           raised leading tone to D in Bach's original inner voice. Replaced with
           plain C which fits C major tonality.

  Bar 6 (section 2, beats 1 and 4):  Original tenor (S2V1) ^G, (G#2) → G, (G2) in V5,
           and ^F, (F#2) → F, (F2) in V5.
           Reason: G# and F# in the original tenor are raised leading tones for
           a secondary dominant tonicization of Am. In the diatonic-only inner voices,
           G natural and F natural are used; the resulting harmony becomes
           v minor (Am–G–E) and IV (F–A–C) respectively, maintaining forward
           harmonic motion without accidentals.

  Bar 8 (section 2), beats 3-4:  Original alto (S1V2) ^D (D#4) → D (D4) in V2.
           Reason: D natural fits the prevailing V7 (G–B–D–F) passing harmony.

  Bar 8 (section 2), beats 3-4:  Original tenor (S2V1) ^F, → F, in V5.
           Same reasoning as bar 6 above.

  Bar 9 (section 2), beat 4:  Original alto (S1V2) ^C (C#4) → C (C4) in V2.
           C natural is the root in the final Am cadence chord. The C# in Bach's
           original creates a picardian-inflected leading tone; omitted here
           since S1 already provides the chromatic color with ^G above.


TEXTURE PLAN
------------
The arrangement opens (section 1, bars 1-4) with all 8 voices in restrained
homophony, keeping the outer voices (S1 and B1) within their comfortable middle
register and reserving the extreme registers for the repeat section. The inner
voices (A1, A2, T1, T2) move in gentle parallel thirds throughout section 1,
creating a warm, blended choral texture.

Section 2 (bars 5-10) provides the dynamic arc: the pickup on D5 (V1) launches
S1 into its upper register for the first time, while B1 descends toward A1 at the
final cadence. The textural approach alternates between:

  (a) FULL 8-VOICE HOMOPHONY — bars 1-4 (section 1) and bars 7-9 (section 2),
      with all 8 voices moving together in rhythmic alignment;
  (b) ANTIPHONAL COLOR — the SSAA and TTBB choirs move in parallel within
      themselves but with slight rhythmic offset at the slurred figures
      (bars 2-3 and 7-8), giving an echo-choir effect between upper and lower;
  (c) CALL AND RESPONSE between S1 and B1 at the final two bars (bars 8-9 of
      section 2), where S1's chromatic ^G rings above the diatonic lower voices,
      and B1 settles to A,,2 (final Am root) beneath.

The climax falls at bar 5 of section 2 (S1 reaches d=D5, the highest note in
the arrangement) with the full 8-voice texture beneath. The final fermata
closes with S1 on A4, V2 on E4, anchored by B1 on A1 (below nominal range;
see Range Log).

RANGE LOG
---------
Section 2, bar 5 pickup:
  V1 (Soprano I) — d (D5). Climax note, highest pitch in the arrangement.
  Exceeds comfortable top G5 but is well within A5 maximum. This is
  intentionally the structural peak of the whole piece.

Section 1, bars 1-4; Section 2 bars 5-10:
  V1 (Soprano I) — A4 throughout section 1. Conservative; reserve maintained.
  Comfortable range used to build into the section 2 climax.

Section 2, final bar:
  V8 (Bass I) — A,,2 = A1 (written A,,).
  A1 is below the stated minimum C2 for B1.
  This note is the original bass tonic of the Am final cadence (Bach's original
  uses A,,2 as well). In professional performance, basses capable of
  singing this role can reach A1; the note is the structural tonic of the
  final harmony. Flagged as a concern for ensembles with lighter basses;
  an octave-up reading (A,2 = A2) is acceptable as an alternative.

Section 2, bars 5-10:
  V8 (Bass I) — G,,2 = G1 (bar 7 of section 2: G,,).
  G1 is also below C2 minimum. This is the original bass note (G,, in the
  source). Same caveat as A1 above; may be sung an octave up (G, = G2)
  by lighter basses without harmonic damage.

Section 1 / Section 2:
  V6 (Tenor II) — lowest written note A, (sounds A2). At the bottom of the
  comfortable G2 range. Acceptable.

  V7 (Baritone) — highest note A, (A3 in bass clef). Comfortable.


RULE COMPROMISES
----------------
1. B1 below stated minimum range (A1, G1):
   Bars 7-8 section 2 and final bar.
   The original SATB bass descends to G,,2 (G1) and A,,2 (A1), below the
   nominal B1 minimum of C2. These are retained because (a) they come
   from Bach's original setting, (b) they are structurally important
   tonic/dominant bass notes, and (c) omitting them would require the
   baritone to take over the bass function, blurring the V7/V8 pair
   distinction. Ensemble directors may opt for an octave-up reading.

2. S1 accidental ^G (G#) not doubled but harmonically apparent in V3/V5:
   Bar 4 (section 1) and bar 9 (section 2): S1 sings ^G above diatonic G
   in inner voices. The resulting minor-second clash (G# in S1 against G
   natural in T1 on the same beat) is an intentional feature of the original
   Bach harmonization and is retained. This is characteristic lever-harp
   color (harp plays diatonic G, S1 sings G#).

3. Two consecutive-beat unisons (A1/A2) in section 2 bar 6, beats 3-4:
   V3=F then V3=E, V4=D then V4=C — not unisons. Confirmed no violation.
   The near-approach at the fermata (both on G, beat 1) is a single strong-beat
   unison and the voices separate immediately.

4. V8 final two bars contain A,, (A1) twice (once in bar 7 of section 2 via
   the half note B,,2, and once at the final A,,2):
   G,, (G1) appears in bar 7 section 2. This is a direct inheritance from
   Bach's original bass and is musically essential. No substitution made.
