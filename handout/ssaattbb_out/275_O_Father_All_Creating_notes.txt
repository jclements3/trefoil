ARRANGER NOTES — O Father, All Creating (Aurelia)
SSAATTBB arrangement for 8-voice ensemble and lever harp
=========================================================

TRANSPOSITION
-------------
Original key: D major (2 sharps: F#, C#)
Chosen key:   D major (2 sharps) — no transposition required

D major is fully valid for lever harp in Eb storage tuning:
  Eb storage: Eb Ab Bb Db Gb Cb Fb (all levers down)
  Raise F lever  → F natural  (needed for D major)
  Raise C lever  → C natural  (needed for D major)
  Raise G lever  → G natural  (needed for D major)
  Raise D lever  → D natural  (needed for D major)
  Raise A lever  → A natural  (needed for D major)
  Raise E lever  → E natural  (needed for D major)
  Raise B lever  → B natural  (needed for D major)
  All 7 levers raised = C major natural notes + F# and C# from key sig.

  Actual lever positions for D major from Eb storage:
  - Eb remains lowered (sounds Eb; not used in D major — becomes enharmonic D#, avoid)
  - Raise Ab → A natural
  - Raise Bb → B natural
  - Raise Eb (above) is the only problematic string — in practice the harpist
    tunes all strings to D major standard tuning by raising:
    Ab→A, Bb→B, Eb→E, then the remaining sharps (F#, C#) are set by lever.

  In standard Eb storage: levers for D major = raise Ab, Eb, Bb on flats side;
  F# and C# levers engaged on sharps side. All 7 diatonic notes available.
  Harp range covers the full diatonic D major scale throughout the piece.

HARMONIC SUBSTITUTIONS
-----------------------
No full harmonic substitutions were necessary. The original hymn (Aurelia in D major)
uses straightforward diatonic harmony in the soprano, alto, and bass. The tenor voice
contained chromatic passing tones (secondary dominant coloring) which were addressed
under "Diatonic Violations Caught" below.

The original progression stays within I, ii, IV, V, vi throughout all four phrases,
with a half cadence (V = A major) at bar 9 and an interior cadence on IV (G major)
at bar 13. Both cadences use chords whose notes are fully diatonic in D major.

DIATONIC VIOLATIONS CAUGHT
---------------------------
All violations occur in the original S2V1 (tenor) and S2V2 (bass) voices.

Bar 6, original tenor (S2V1), beats 1-2: ^A, (A#2 concert)
  → replaced with A (A2, diatonic natural A)
  Reason: A# is not in D major. The original used this as a raised leading tone
  tonicizing E minor (V/vi). Diatonic A natural preserves the approach to B minor
  without secondary dominant color. The T1 voice (C3) provides the harmonic third.

Bar 7, original tenor (S2V1), beats 1-2: ^A, (A#2 concert)
  → replaced with A (A2, diatonic)
  Reason: same as bar 6; continuation of same tonicization passage.

Bar 12, original tenor (S2V1), beat 2: ^A, (A#2 concert)
  → replaced with B, (B2, diatonic)
  Reason: A# in the original runs B-A#-B in an ornamental figure around B minor.
  Since A# is out of D major diatonic set and A natural would create a weak
  tritone clash, the nearest upper diatonic neighbor B2 was used instead.
  This changes the ornament from a lower chromatic neighbor to a repeated B,
  making the tenor line B-B-B-B (sustained), which is less ornamental but
  preserves the B minor color cleanly.

Bar 12, original bass (S2V2), beat 4: ^D, (D#2 concert)
  → replaced with E, (E2, diatonic)
  Reason: D# is not diatonic in D major. The original ^D, appears as a chromatic
  passing tone between D and E in a rising bass line. The diatonic neighbor E
  (E2) continues the stepwise motion naturally: F#2-E2-D2-E2, which provides
  a slight lower-neighbor resolution that maintains bass interest without
  chromatic color.

Bar 3/4/15, original bass (S2V2): A,,, G,,, F,, (A1, G1, F#1 concert)
  → raised one octave to A,, G,, F,, (A2, G2, F#2 concert) for B1 range
  Reason: original bass descends to the sub-bass register (below C2) which is
  outside B1 vocal range (minimum C2 concert). All low bass notes were raised
  one octave while preserving the harmonic function.

Bar 8, original bass (S2V2): B,, C, D, B,, (B1, C#2, D2, B1 concert)
  → replaced with B, C, D, B, (B2, C#2 [as C, in bass], D2, B2)
  Reason: B,, = B1 concert is below B1 minimum C2. Raised B,, to B, (B2).

Bar 15, original bass: G,, G,, G,, G,, (G1 throughout)
  → replaced with G, G, G, G, (G2 throughout)
  Reason: same range adaptation.

Bar 16, original bass: E,, E,, A,, A,, (E1, A1 concert)
  → replaced with E, E, A, A, (E2, A2 concert)
  Reason: same range adaptation.

TEXTURE PLAN
------------
The arrangement opens with 6 voices (bars 1-2, mp) — S1 S2 A1 A2 Bar B1 — with
tenors tacet, reserving textural growth. The tenors enter unobtrusively at bar 3
on the downbeat, filling inner harmony and completing the 8-voice ensemble by bar 5.

Phrase 2 (bars 6-9) introduces antiphonal contrast: the upper choir (SSAA) carries
the soaring ascending line (A4-D5) while the lower voices (TTBB) provide harmonic
underpinning, creating a call-and-response texture.

Phrase 3 (bars 10-13) features a structural canon: S1 and T1 share the same melodic
line one octave apart in parallel, with T6 providing inversion-like contrary motion.
The texture builds to the phrase climax on D5 (bar 11, beat 4) before descending.

Phrase 4 (bars 14-17) recapitulates the opening melody at full 8-voice homophony,
ff, with the widest ensemble spread (S1=F#4, B1=D2). Bar 16 implies a 4-3 suspension
figure in the tenor pair (G-F#) resolving to the final D major chord.

Textures used: (1) reduced 6-voice opening, (2) antiphonal SSAA vs TTBB,
(3) canonic imitation S1+T1 at the octave, (4) full 8-voice block homophony.

RULE COMPROMISES
----------------
1. Voice leading, bar 4, T1-T2 parallel motion:
   T1 sustains on B, (B2 concert) for beats 2-4 while T2 moves G,→G,→A,→A,.
   The T1 sustain creates parallel motion with T2 for two beats. Accepted because
   T1 has already reached its minimum (B2) and cannot go lower without leaving
   range, and a sustained note provides a pedal-point effect under the IV-V
   harmonic motion. The parallel motion is not between outer voices.

2. Bass range adaptation (systematic):
   The original SATB bass descends to A1-G1-F#1 (bars 3-4, 15) and B1 (bars 7-8).
   These are all below B1's minimum C2. The adaptation raises these notes one octave,
   which changes the bass register from sub-bass (organ-style) to normal bass-baritone
   range. The harmonic function is entirely preserved; only the octave placement changes.
   This is a necessary practical adaptation for a vocal bass, not a voice leading compromise.

3. Canonic canon (bar 10-11, T1 mirrors S1):
   The T1 voice follows S1 at the octave below (in unison of written pitch in
   the 8vb treble staff). This creates exact parallel octaves between S1 and T1
   for the duration of bars 10-11. This parallel is intentional and idiomatic —
   it is the structural feature of the canonic imitation texture, and octave
   doubling between outer voices of different choirs (S1 and T1) is standard
   in double-choir writing. The inner voices A1/A2 and T2/Bar fill independent
   harmonic motion that decorates around the canon voices.

4. Bar 12, V5 (T1), beat 2: original ornament ^A, (A#) simplified to B,:
   Rather than using an A natural (which would create A-B-A-B oscillation at T1
   minimum), the note was raised to B, (repeated pitch). This slightly reduces
   the melodic interest of T1 in bar 12, but prevents a range violation and avoids
   an augmented melodic interval (A natural to B natural = major second, diatonic;
   however, A natural was the chromatic violation note, so replacing with B, is
   the cleanest diatonic solution). No voice leading rule is strictly broken;
   this is a melodic simplification in service of the diatonic constraint.

5. Bar 16, S2 line:
   S2 sings A,-D-B,-B, (A3-D4-B3-B3) — this involves a rising fourth (A3→D4)
   on beats 1-2, which is within the permitted range (leaps of a sixth or more
   require step resolution; a fourth does not). The line is somewhat disjunct
   but remains singable. The alternative (keeping S2 strictly in parallel thirds
   below S1) would have produced voice crossings with A1 on several beats.
