===========================================================================
TRANSPOSITION
===========================================================================

Original key:     Ab major (4 flats) — INVALID for lever harp
Chosen key:       A major (3 sharps)
Semitones shifted: +1 (up one semitone)
Direction:        UP, per the tie-breaking rule (Ab→G and Ab→A are both
                  1 semitone distant; the rule mandates transposing up to
                  preserve bass register)

Harp lever positions required for A major (from Eb storage tuning):
  Ab lever → raised (gives A)    — lever 1
  Eb lever → raised (gives E)    — lever 2
  Bb lever → raised (gives B)    — lever 3
  All other levers remain down: F, C, G, D strings stay as F, C, G, D,
  which in A major are diatonic: F# (lever up on F strings), C# (lever up
  on C strings), G# (lever up on G strings), D (lever down = D natural).
  Wait — in Eb storage tuning all levers DOWN = Eb major (Eb Bb F C G D Ab).
  Raising ALL levers: E B F# C# G# D# A = E major. Raising only Ab,Eb,Bb
  levers gives: A B F C G D A — that is A major with F natural not F#.
  Correct A major lever positions (from Eb storage):
    Ab strings → raise (A)
    Eb strings → raise (E)
    Bb strings → raise (B)
    F strings  → raise (F#)
    C strings  → raise (C#)
    G strings  → raise (G#)
    D strings  → down (D natural)
  All 7 levers raised except D. This is the maximum lever raise short of
  full (which would give E major). A major is a valid harp key under the
  stated constraint (3 sharps ≤ 4 sharps maximum).

Range check after transposition (all voices comfortable):
  S1 top note: E5 (e in bar 12) — within comfortable range
  B1 low note: E2 (E,, in bar 16) — within B1 range (C2–A3)

HARMONIC SUBSTITUTIONS
===========================================================================

Bar 2 / Bar 6 (same progression repeated):
  Original chord: Dominant 7th with raised 5th / diminished passing harmony
                  implied by =E natural in soprano (E-natural over an Eb
                  major context = chromatic passing tone creating V7/iv or
                  a German augmented sixth)
  Treatment:      S1 retains the chromatic =F (transposed) as a coloristic
                  passing tone. All other voices hold diatonic chord tones
                  of A major (I–V motion). No reharmonization needed because
                  the chromatic note belongs exclusively to S1, which is
                  unrestricted.
  Reason:         The original harmony is essentially I with a chromatic
                  upper neighbor in the soprano. Moving inner voices to
                  diatonic neighbors (E4→E4, D4→D4 etc.) preserves the
                  functional harmony while the harp plays the diatonic I
                  chord beneath.

Bar 12:
  Original: Alto had =A natural (A-natural in Ab = chromatic #1 scale deg.)
            After transposition, this becomes Bb (=B natural in A major) —
            non-diatonic in A major.
  Substitution: A2 and A1 voices use A natural (scale degree 1, diatonic)
                instead of Bb. This maintains the melodic shape while
                preserving diatonic purity. The original chromatic note was
                a passing chromatic in the alto; removing it strengthens
                the dominant preparation.
  Reason: Bb has no diatonic function in A major and would require a mid-
          piece lever flip on the B string, which is not possible.

Bar 11 (original bass had _G = Gb in Ab / G natural in A):
  Original: Bass voice had Gb (Fb context) creating a bVII passing chord.
  Substitution: In A major, G natural = =G (non-diatonic). The bass line
                moves through C, B,, A,, using G# (diatonic) instead. The
                bVII color is replaced by a vi passing chord (F#m), which
                preserves the descending bass tendency.
  Reason: G natural is not in the A major harp tuning.

Bar 15 (tenor had =E, = E-natural in Ab / F natural in A):
  Original tenor: The raised leading tone to the subdominant (secondary
                  dominant area) created a V/IV color.
  Substitution: T1 and T2 use E natural (diatonic in A) rather than F
                natural, moving through I→IV with stepwise inner voice
                motion. The V/IV is replaced by a plain IV chord approach.
  Reason: F natural is non-diatonic in A major.

===========================================================================
TEXTURE PLAN
===========================================================================

The arrangement opens with the SSAA choir alone for bars 1-4 (Phrase 1),
creating an intimate four-voice texture that draws the listener in before
the full ensemble arrives. The TTBB choir enters with a soft tutti in bars
5-8 (Phrase 2), producing the first experience of all eight voices in
homophonic block style. Phrase 3a (bars 9-10) exploits antiphonal exchange:
SSAA sings bar 9 alone while TTBB answers at bar 10, creating a call-and-
response dialogue that energizes the rising sequential material. Phrase 3b
(bars 11-12) reunites all eight voices in full homophony as the sequence
climbs toward the climax. Phrase 4 (bars 13-16) is the dramatic peak:
S1 reaches E5 while B1 descends to E2, spanning nearly four octaves; the
inner voices provide sustained harmonic pillars while the outer voices carry
the melodic argument in a call-and-response gesture between soprano and bass.
The final cadence (bars 17-18) opens into a held chord on F# major (the VI
of A), an intentionally open, ecclesiastical close appropriate for a single-
verse excerpt.

Texture types used:
  1. SSAA four-voice reduced opening (bars 1-4)
  2. Full 8-voice homophony / tutti (bars 5-8, 11-12, 13-16)
  3. Antiphonal SSAA vs TTBB exchange (bars 9-10)
  4. Call and response between outer voices S1/B1 (bars 13-14) with inner
     voices providing sustained harmonic support
  5. Sustained held chord with stretto approach to final (bars 17-18)

===========================================================================
RULE COMPROMISES
===========================================================================

1. Bar 7 (Phrase 2), V:6 (T2) stepwise motion after large interval:
   The passage `A, B, A, E | B, G, F E | F3/2 G,/ A, G, | ...` contains
   a motion from `E` (E4 written = E3 sounding) up to `F3/2` (F#4 written
   = F#3 sounding) at the start of bar 7. This is a stepwise half-step rise,
   not a large leap. The preceding motion from `G,` (G2 sounding) up to `F E`
   involves a change of register: G2→F#3 is an upward 7th. This large leap
   is unavoidable because the T2 voice must avoid going below G2 (its range
   minimum), and the harmony at that point requires a rising motion to
   support the IV chord. The note after the leap (G,) moves back downward
   by step, partially satisfying the "resolve by step in opposite direction"
   requirement.
   Bar: 7, Voice: T2 (V:6), Rule: "Leaps of a sixth or more must resolve
   stepwise in the opposite direction" — the resolution is incomplete (only
   one step down rather than immediate stepwise descent).
   Reason: range constraint forces the register change at this point.

2. Bars 5-8, V:7 and V:8 (Baritone and Bass) parallel motion:
   In bars 7-8, both bass voices move in close parallel: `C,3/2 B,,/ A,, G,,`
   and then `E, E, A,,3/2 x/` / `A,, B,, A,,3/2 x/`. The voices are a
   5th apart at bar 7 beat 1 (C#3 vs A2), then both descend in similar
   motion. This is close to parallel 5ths. Careful examination shows they
   are not strictly parallel (Bar descends C#-B-A-G#; B1 descends C#-B-A)
   but the similar motion is a mild compromise.
   Bar: 7-8, Voices: Bar and B1, Rule: no parallel 5ths.
   Reason: both voices are following the original bass line closely; the
   doubling at the octave/5th is idiomatic for the bass register.

3. Opening texture (Phrase 1, bars 1-4):
   The instruction states "open with reduced texture (4-6 voices) and build
   to full 8." Bars 1-4 use SSAA only (4 voices). The TTBB choir rests
   entirely through bars 1-4. This means no tenor or bass voices are present
   for the full first phrase, which some might consider a conservative
   reduction but is within the stated guideline (4 voices is the minimum
   of the 4-6 range).
   No rule is broken; this is by design.

4. Bar 15, V:4 (A2): `F,3/2 F/ F A,`
   The note `F,` = F#3 in treble clef. A2 range is D3–B4. F#3 is within
   range but approaches the lower boundary. The dotted rhythm emphasizes
   this note on the strong beat. It occurs at the rhetorical peak of the
   introduction section (!sintro!), where the harmonic intensity warrants
   reaching toward the lower register.
   Assessment: no rule broken; noted for rehearsal awareness.
