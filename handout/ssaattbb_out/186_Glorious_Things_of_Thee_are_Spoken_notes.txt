TRANSPOSITION
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
Original key: F major (1 flat — Bb)
Chosen key:   F major (no transposition)
Semitones shifted: 0
Direction: none

F major is valid for lever harp with Eb storage tuning. Only one
lever must be raised: the Bb lever (one string per octave) converts
each Eb to its natural Bb, giving the F major diatonic set F G A Bb C D E.

Lever positions for F major:
  Bb levers UP (raised from Eb) — all other levers DOWN (Eb, Ab, Db, Gb, Cb, Fb stay flat)
  Effective pitches: F G A Bb C D E — 7-note diatonic F major scale.

No range problems were encountered in the original key, so no
nearest-key substitution was needed.

HARMONIC SUBSTITUTIONS
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
The original contains several B-natural passing tones in the inner
voices (original bass and tenor). These create momentary secondary-
dominant color (implying G major, the V of ii) against the prevailing
F major harmony. Since all voices except S1 must remain diatonic,
these were replaced with Bb (the diatonic scale degree 4) and the
chord color was softened to a IV or I-6/4 implication.

Bar 4  (phrase 1, bar 4): original S2V2 (bass) had =B,, (B-natural 2).
  Original chord color: G major passing chord (V/ii) → Bb2 (diatonic IV).
  Replacement: Bb2 in B1. Voice leading Bb→C preserved (step up).

Bar 5  (phrase 2, bar 1): original S2V1 (tenor) had =B, (B-natural 3).
  Same substitution: Bb3 in T1. V/ii color → diatonic IV area.

Bar 8  (phrase 2, bar 4): original S2V2 (bass) had =B,, (B-natural 2).
  Same as bar 4. Bb2 in B1.

Bar 10 (phrase 3, bar 2): original S2V1 (tenor) had =B, (B-natural 3)
  embedded in a fast passing figure. Replaced with Bb3 in T1. The
  diatonic Bb3→C4 stepwise motion preserves the ascending drive.

Bar 15 (phrase 4, bar 3): original S1V2 (alto) had _E (Eb4) as a
  chromatic passing tone descending F4→Eb4→D4. In the diatonic
  arrangement (A1 voice), this becomes F4→E4→D4 (diatonic passing
  tone), which is equally smooth and retains the stepwise descent
  to the D4 at the phrase's inner cadence. Harmonic substitution:
  the momentary bVII color (Bb/Eb) softens to a plain IV→I motion.

S1 retains the original =B natural in bar 12 (phrase 3, bar 4):
  "=B B/c/ c2" — the raised leading tone in S1 is freely permitted
  and is never doubled by any other voice. This chromatic inflection
  gives the phrase-3 cadence its characteristic harmonic tension
  before the phrase-4 climax.

TEXTURE PLAN
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
The arrangement opens with all 8 voices present but in a relatively
close dynamic range, with T2 and Bar in moderate registers to avoid
full spread at the outset. Phrase 2 (bars 5–8) moves to antiphonal
writing: SSAA carry the melodic material with the original alto line,
while TTBB echo rhythmically, creating a dialogue between choirs.
Phrase 3 (bars 9–12) shifts to a call-and-response texture where S1
and B1 carry the melody in octaves (widely spaced outer voices) while
the six inner voices sustain slow-moving harmonic support, producing
the characteristic "halo" of lever harp writing. Phrase 4 (bars 13–16)
builds to full 8-voice homophony at the climax: S1 reaches D5 in bar 15,
B1 descends to F2 on the final note, and T1 mirrors the S1 melodic line
in its own octave (canonic at the octave). The final cadence in bar 16
uses a 4–3 suspension in the inner voices (A1–A2 and T1–T2 both resolve
downward by step) before settling on an open F major chord.

Textures used:
  1. Full 8-voice homophony — phrases 1 and 4
  2. Antiphonal exchange (SSAA vs TTBB) — phrase 2
  3. Call and response between outer voices (S1 and B1) with inner
     harmonic support — phrase 3
  4. Canonic imitation at the octave between S1 and T1 — phrase 4

RULE COMPROMISES
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
1. Bar 6 (phrase 2, bar 2): T1/T2 share brief passing unisons on F3 and
   G3 within the same bar. The rule permits weak-beat unisons "only if both
   voices move away by step immediately after." Both voices do step away;
   however, two unisons in one bar is slightly above the recommended
   frequency. Reason: the bass voice (B1) occupies E3 and F3 in this bar,
   leaving very limited pitch space for T2 below T1 without violating the
   T-voice/B-voice no-unison rule. The passing unisons are the least-bad
   solution and occur on weak beat subdivisions.

2. Bar 13 (phrase 4, bar 1): B1 rises to A3 (from original bass line
   A,3/2 A,/ B, F,), pushing Bar to C4 (middle C) for three beats.
   C4 is the stated top of the Baritone range. This is justified by the
   climax context (phrase 4) and corresponds to the high-register opening
   of the Haydn theme's final statement, where the bass is naturally high.

3. Phrase 3 (bars 9–10): B1 holds C3 for eight consecutive beats (original
   bass pedal point). The baritone and tenor voices had limited pitch
   variety above this pedal without creating T-B unisons; the solution
   was to keep Bar in G3–F3 oscillation and T2 in A3–Bb3 range, which
   creates slight parallelism between Bar and B1 at certain beats. This
   was accepted to honor the original harmonic stasis of the pedal point.

4. V:2 (S2) phrase 4 bar 16 final cadence: S2 ends on E4 rather than the
   expected F4 (root) to avoid a unison with A1's C4→C4 landing. E4
   creates a 7th above B1's F2 and a major 7th below S1's F4 half note.
   This gives the final chord an added-major-7th color (F C E A C F from
   bottom to top: B1=F2, Bar=C3, T2=F3, T1=F3, A2=C4, A1=C4, S2=E4,
   S1=F4). The E4 in S2 resolves the phrase's inner melodic motion
   D4→E4 (a half-step lead into the tonic area). If a plain F major triad
   is preferred, S2 may sing F4 on the final half note and A1 may be
   adjusted to A3.
