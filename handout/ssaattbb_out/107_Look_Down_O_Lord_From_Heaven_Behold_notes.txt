HARMONIC SUBSTITUTIONS
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
The original SATB contained three types of chromatic harmony:

1. F# (^F) — appears in original S1V2 (alto) and S2V1 (tenor) throughout.
   Function: raised leading tone of G minor, acting as V/ii (secondary
   dominant of G minor within F major), or occasionally as a coloristic
   raised 6th in the key of G minor.
   Substitution: all inner voices replace F# with F natural (diatonic 1st
   degree of F major), which turns the implied secondary dominant into
   a iv chord or ii chord substitute. The melodic color of the raised
   leading tone is reserved exclusively for S1, where the original soprano
   line already carries it naturally.

2. C# (^C) — appears in original S1V2 (alto) and later voices.
   Function: raised 7th of D minor (V/vi), tonicizing the submediant.
   Substitution: all inner voices use C natural, converting the implied
   V/vi into a plain vi (Dm) chord. This keeps the harmony diatonic while
   preserving the D minor color. S1 retains ^C where it appears in the
   original soprano.

3. Eb (_E) — appears in S2V1 (tenor) and S2V2 (bass) at phrase endings.
   Function: modal borrowing from F minor (flattened 7th of the relative
   Bb context, or Dorian inflection), creating a poignant minor-mode
   cadential color.
   Substitution: all inner voices replace Eb with E natural (the diatonic
   leading tone of F major). This converts the borrowed minor-mode color
   into a straightforward leading-tone figure, reinforcing the F major
   cadence. No Eb is possible on the harp without a mid-piece lever flip;
   the E natural is therefore both harmonically sound and practically
   necessary.

TEXTURE PLAN
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
The arrangement opens in an antiphonal texture (mm. 1–4): the SSAA choir
states each phrase while the TTBB choir echoes at the same pitch level one
beat offset, then the two choirs converge at the fermata cadence points for
a moment of blended homophony. In the repeated verse (mm. 5–8) the full
8-voice homophony launches on the downbeat, giving a sense of intensification
and gathering of the congregation's voices. The middle section (mm. 9–12)
shifts to a call-and-response texture between the outer voices (S1 on top,
B1 in the bass) while the inner voices A1 A2 T1 T2 provide a flowing
diatonic countermelody beneath — giving the effect of a melodic duet over
a harmonic cushion. The final section (mm. 13–15) introduces a stretto
compression: S1+S2 enter with the climactic phrase, followed immediately by
A1+A2 and T1+T2 in close imitation, all converging into a full 8-voice
spread at the final fermata cadence, with S1 at the top and B1 anchoring
the root in the low register.

Textures used: (1) antiphonal SSAA vs TTBB, (2) full 8-voice homophony,
(3) call and response between outer voices with inner harmonic support,
(4) stretto entries at final cadence.

UNISON LOG
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
All unisons are at phrase endings or fermata points (strong beats), used
for blend and emphasis, never as default voice-leading solutions.

m.1 (pickup A): V:1 S1 and V:5 T1 both enter on A — cross-choir unison
  (S1 and T1 octave unison on the pickup note), intentional for a clean
  ensemble entry. This is a cross-choir unison and does not count against
  either choir's within-pair budget.

m.2 fermata beat 3 (A): S1 and S2 approach the fermata; S1=A, S2=F
  — no unison here; S1 holds the soprano A while S2 resolves to F a third
  below (preferred spacing at cadence).

m.4 fermata G: all voices converge on the G-major chord. Within SSAA:
  S1=G, S2=E, A1=C, A2=G, — A2 and S1 share G as an octave (cross-pair,
  not unison within a pair). Within TTBB: T1=G, T2=E, Bar=E, B1=G,,
  — Bar and T2 share E (cross-pair octave). No within-pair unisons.

m.8 fermata (repeat): same as m.4.

m.13 fermata beat 3 (G in S1): S1=G, S2=E, A1=B, A2=E — no unisons.

m.15 final fermata A: S1=A (fermata), S2=F, A1=C, A2=A, — A2 and S1 share
  A as octave (cross-pair); no within-pair unison.

Summary: No within-pair unisons are used in this arrangement. All
coincidences of pitch are cross-pair or cross-choir octave doublings at
cadence points, which are unrestricted per the rules.

RULE COMPROMISES
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
1. Parallel motion in inner voices, mm. 1–4 antiphonal section:
   Because all inner voices (S2, A1, A2, T1, T2, Bar) are constrained
   to strict diatonic motion and must harmonize a melody that moves in
   largely parallel stepwise motion, some parallel thirds and sixths
   between adjacent inner voice pairs are unavoidable. Parallel thirds
   (e.g. A1 and A2 moving F→E→D together) are explicitly acceptable
   by convention in SATB-derived writing and are not counted as violations.
   No parallel fifths or octaves occur between any two adjacent voices.

2. Voice overlap at register boundary (A2/T1), m.9–12 middle section:
   Per the rules, A2 and T1 may freely share pitches at the register break.
   In the middle section, A2 (bottom of soprano staff) and T1 (top of
   tenor staff written in 8vb treble) share the same written C pitch at
   several points. This is explicitly permitted and is used deliberately
   to create ensemble blend at the choir boundary.

3. Stepwise resolution of leaps: V:8 B1 makes a downward leap of a fourth
   (D,→A,,) in the bass line at m.2 and similar points, following the
   original SATB bass. Bass fourths are conventional and not subject to
   the "sixth or more must resolve by step" rule; no compromise is needed.
   All leaps of a sixth or larger were avoided in B1.

4. The opening texture assigns identical lines to S1=T1 (octave) and
   S2=T2 (octave) to establish the antiphonal effect clearly. This creates
   octave doublings between cross-choir voices, which are explicitly
   permitted. The internal independence within each choir (SSAA having
   S1≠S2, A1≠A2; TTBB having T1≠T2, Bar≠B1) is maintained throughout.
