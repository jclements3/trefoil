ARRANGER NOTES
250 — How Shall We Answer Terror's Cry?
Tune: MELITA (Dykes, 1861)
SSAATTBB Arrangement for Double Choir and Lever Harp

TRANSPOSITION
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
Original key:  C major (0 accidentals)
Chosen key:    C major (0 accidentals)
Semitones shifted: 0 (no transposition)
Direction: N/A

C major is fully within the valid range for lever harp
in Eb storage tuning (3 flats or 4 sharps maximum).
C major requires all 7 levers raised from Eb storage:
  Ab → A  (lever raised)
  Eb → E  (lever raised)
  Bb → B  (lever raised)
  F  → F  (lever already at F natural; raise from Fb — storage Eb→E already covers this)

More precisely, from Eb storage tuning (Eb Bb F C G D A all
levers down = Eb Ab Db Gb Cb Fb Bbb = Eb major enharmonic),
raising all seven levers in sequence produces C major:
  Lever 1 (Ab→A): 6 flats → 5 flats
  Lever 2 (Eb→E): 5 flats → 4 flats
  Lever 3 (Bb→B): 4 flats → 3 flats
  Lever 4 (F→F#): 3 flats → 2 flats — OVERSHOOT; stop here at F lever up.

Correction: Eb storage gives strings tuned to Eb, Bb, F, C, G, D, Ab.
To reach C major from Eb major (3 flats: Bb Eb Ab), raise:
  Ab → A  (lever 1 raised)
  Eb → E  (lever 2 raised)
  Bb → B  (lever 3 raised)
All other strings (F, C, G, D) are already natural. C major achieved with
3 levers raised (Ab, Eb, Bb strings). No additional lever flips mid-piece
are possible; S1 accidentals (F# in bars 4, 6, 7, 8, 10) are sung by
the voice only and explicitly not doubled by any other voice or the harp.

HARMONIC SUBSTITUTIONS
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
Bar 6, beats 1–4:
  Original bass: C#2 – C2 – D2 – D# 2 (chromatic ascending passing bass)
  Replacement:   C2 – C2 – D2 – E2    (diatonic ascending stepwise bass)
  Reason: The chromatic bass line implies a diminished or augmented
  sequential motion outside C major diatonic. The diatonic substitute
  C–C–D–E preserves the ascending direction and prepares the dominant
  region (D–E approaching the G bass in bar 7) without requiring
  any non-diatonic string on the harp.
  Bar (Baritone) voices correspondingly ascend E2–E2–F2–G2 (diatonic thirds
  above the new bass), creating a smooth parallel ascending line.

Bar 8, beats 1–4:
  Original harmony implied: E minor with D#4 leading tone (secondary
  dominant of A minor, or augmented dominant of E minor)
  Replacement: E minor, purely diatonic (E–G–B), with no raised leading tone
  in the inner voices.
  Reason: The raised 7th (D#) in S1V2 is removed from S2 (replaced by D
  natural), and the tenor/baritone voices outline a plain E minor triad
  (B2–G2 in T1/T2, E2 pedal in B1). The S1 accidental ^F (F#) in this bar
  functions as a chromatic color tone in the melody and is intentionally
  unsupported by any other voice, creating the characteristic lever harp
  color described in the arranging brief.

Bar 9, beats 1–2:
  Original S1V2: Bb3 (flat-7 of C, implying G7 dominant 7th)
  Replacement in S2: C4 (diatonic, functioning as the root of C major)
  Reason: Bb is outside C major diatonic. The S2 voice moves C–C–D–D
  (beats 1–4), outlining a C major → F major motion that preserves the
  harmonic direction without the non-diatonic Bb.

Bar 11, bass region:
  Original S2V2: C2 – F#1 – G1 – G1
  F#1 and G1 (written ^F,, G,, G,,) lie below B1's minimum range (C2).
  Replacement: C2 – C2 – G2 – G2 (C,3/2 C,/ G, G,)
  Reason: Range correction; the replacement emphasises the C–G root and
  fifth, strengthening the climactic C major arrival.

TEXTURE PLAN
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
The arrangement opens with SSAA alone on the pickup and bar 1 (mezzo-piano,
4-voice homophony), allowing the upper choir to establish the tune before
the lower choir enters at bar 1 beat 1 with TTBB, creating the first full
8-voice texture. Bars 3–4 use full 8-voice homophony as the melody reaches
its first phrase peak (c = C5 in S1). Bars 5–6 employ a quasi-antiphonal
texture: the upper voices (SSAA) carry the melodic line while the lower
voices (TTBB/Bar/B1) hold a supporting pedal-like motion with longer note
values in Bar and B1. Bars 7–8 restore full 8 voices with increased inner-voice
independence (A1 and A2 in contrary stepwise motion, T1 and T2 similarly).
Bars 9–10 feature a call-and-response element between the outer voices S1
and B1, with inner voices providing harmonic support in steady quarter-note
motion. Bar 11 is the climactic bar, with S1 reaching A4, B1 spanning from
C2 to G2, and all 8 voices in full homophony — maximum ensemble spread. The
final bar (12) resolves with a 4-3 suspension in the tenors (T1: F3→E3, T2:
D3→C3 sounding concert), stepwise contrary motion in the altos, and a
conclusive I cadence with B1 arriving on C2 and Bar ascending to C3.

Texture types used:
  1. Full 8-voice homophony (bars 3, 8, 11, 12)
  2. Reduced 4-voice texture with upper choir alone (pickup + bar 1)
  3. Quasi-antiphonal SSAA vs. TTBB texture (bars 5–6)
  4. Call and response between outer voices S1/B1 (bars 9–10)
  5. Suspension at final cadence (bar 12, tenors and altos)

RULE COMPROMISES
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
1. Pickup bar, S1+A2 unison on C4:
   Rule: S voices and A voices may not share a unison with each other.
   Bar: Pickup; S1=C4, A2=C4.
   Reason: The pickup is a single-beat anacrusis. Having all four upper
   voices enter together on a C major chord (S1=C4, S2=A3, A1=E4, A2=C4)
   gives a clean, resonant opening. The S1+A2 unison on C4 is one octave
   below S1's primary register and lasts only one beat, with both voices
   diverging immediately into bar 1. Accepted as the lesser compromise
   compared to leaving A2 on an incomplete chord or starting A2 on G3
   (which would create a very wide, thin spacing on the opening pickup).

2. Bar 5, Bar voice (Baritone) stepwise line:
   Rule: Bar should be a third above B1.
   Bar: Beats 3–4, Bar=B2–C3 (B, C in bass clef), B1=C2–C2 (C, C,).
   At beat 3, Bar=B2, B1=C2 — Bar is above B1 (B2>C2) ✓, but the
   interval is a major 7th rather than a diatonic third (E2 would be
   the strict third). The reason: a strict third on beat 3 would produce
   E2 (E,), but the jump from D3 (beat 2 Bar) to E2 (beat 3 Bar) is a
   descending major 7th, violating the leap-of-sixth-or-more rule.
   Accepting the stepwise Bar line D3–D3–B2–C3 as the lesser compromise.

3. Bar 11, B1 rhythmic pattern:
   Rule: B1 should closely follow original SATB bass.
   Original S2V2 bar 11: C, ^F,, G,, G,,
   Replacement: C,3/2 C,/ G, G,
   Reason: ^F,, (F#1) and G,, (G1) are both below B1's minimum range (C2).
   The replacement uses C2 (doubled dotted-half then eighth) and G2 for
   beats 3–4, sacrificing the original bass motion in favor of a playable,
   range-appropriate line. This is the only bar where the original bass
   character is substantially altered rather than just made diatonic.

4. Bar 3, T1 leap:
   T1 (8vb treble written): A, F D E (sounds A2 F3 D3 E3)
   The written A, to F interval is a rising major sixth (A3→F4 written,
   A2→F3 concert). This exceeds the "prefer stepwise motion" guideline.
   Reason: The chord at bar 3 beats 1–2 is A minor → D minor passing,
   requiring the tenor to move from the A2 pedal point (matching the bass)
   up to F3 to fill the F chord tone for the D minor/F major color. The leap
   resolves stepwise (F3→D3→E3) in the immediately following notes. Accepted
   as the best available option given the diatonic constraint and voice
   crossing avoidance requirements.
