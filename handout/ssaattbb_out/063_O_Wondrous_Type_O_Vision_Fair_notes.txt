HARMONIC SUBSTITUTIONS
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
No full harmonic reharmonizations were required. The original
harmony is entirely diatonic in E major (vi–IV–I–V motion
throughout the piece's medieval modal style). The ^B (B#)
chromatic notes in the original alto voice are treated as inner
voice diatonic violations (see below) rather than reflecting
a true secondary dominant, since the surrounding harmonic context
(C#m in bar 7 and bar 14-15) supports a simple C# resolution
without reharmonization.

DIATONIC VIOLATIONS CAUGHT
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
The original SATB contains ^B (B#) in the original alto (S1V2)
at three points. B# is chromatic and not diatonic in E major.
All instances are replaced with C# (the nearest diatonic
neighbor, a half-step away), which is the natural resolution
of a raised leading tone in the context of C#m (vi):

  Bar 7,  beat 3:  original alto ^B, (B#3) → C (C#4)
    Reason: In the original, the alto raises B to B# as an
    inner-voice leading tone to C#. The diatonic C# serves
    the same harmonic purpose without an accidental.
    In the SSAATTBB, A1 at bar 7 beat 3 moves C#4→C#4 (stable)
    and A2 moves A3→G#3 (descending), preserving the voice
    leading without the chromatic note.

  Bar 14, beat 2:  original alto (^B,) (B#3) → C (C#4)
    Reason: Same as bar 7. The ^B, appears in the original
    as a chromatic passing leading tone. Replaced with C#4,
    which fits the surrounding B major (V) harmony as the
    major 2nd above the root. A1 at bar 14 carries B3→B3→B3
    (stable), preserving the sustained character of this bar.

  Bar 15, beat 3:  original alto ^B, (B#3) → C (C#4)
    Reason: Same figure as bar 7 and 14. At the penultimate
    bar, the original alto uses the leading tone chromaticism
    one final time. The SSAATTBB replaces it with C#4 in the
    recomposed A1 (E4→E4→D#4), which provides a more decisive
    stepwise descent toward the final C#m cadence.

TEXTURE PLAN
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
The arrangement uses full 8-voice homophony throughout, with
the inner voices (A1, A2, T1, T2) providing genuine melodic
independence through contrary motion and stepwise voice leading
rather than simple doubling. The texture opens with all 8 voices
on the first downbeat, which suits the hymn's strophic processional
character (the "Agincourt" tune traditionally supports a robust,
unified sound). The dynamic arc is shaped through register:
S1 remains in the C#5 region for the first half, with the
clearest climactic articulation at bars 13–15 where S1 ascends
back to G#4–F#4–E4 over a fuller bass as the intro section
returns (!sintro!/!eintro! markers). The final cadence on
C#m (vi) is modal and open, reflecting the medieval origin
of the tune; the final chord has five distinct pitch classes
spread across two octaves for maximum resonance.

Texture types employed:
  (1) Full 8-voice homophony — primary texture throughout
  (2) Contrary motion between voice pairs — A1 moves in
      opposition to A2, and T1 moves in opposition to T2,
      providing linear interest within the block-chord texture
  (3) Inner-voice suspensions implied at the cadence (bar 16):
      A1 holds E4 (a 3rd of the C#m chord) while B1 resolves
      down to C#3, creating a sense of gentle suspension
      resolution at the final cadence

RULE COMPROMISES
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
1. Baritone frequently sounds above Tenor II (sounding pitch):
   Bars 1-2, 4-15 at various beats, Baritone sounding pitch
   equals or exceeds T2 sounding pitch.
   Reason: The original bass (B1) ascends unusually high for
   this tune (A3, G#3 frequently), and the Baritone as a
   "third above B1" correspondingly pushes into the B3-C#4
   range. Since cross-pair voice crossings are explicitly
   permitted by the arranging rules ("Voice crossing between
   pairs is permitted where musically effective"), this is
   accepted. The modal, processional character of the 'Deo
   Gracias' tune produces a compressed register stack, and
   the resulting sonority is characteristic of 15th-century
   vocal practice (full-texture, close-position scoring).

2. No climax at S1=G5-A5 and B1=C2-D2:
   The original soprano melody peaks at C#5 and never rises
   above this pitch. The original bass reaches no lower than
   G#2. Extending either extreme would require significant
   melodic departure from the original. The "climax" directive
   is instead interpreted as the moment of fullest texture and
   harmonic arrival (bar 13-16, sintro/eintro section), where
   all 8 voices converge on the final C#m chord with maximum
   ensemble resonance.

3. Texture variety limited to two main types:
   The short, strophic nature of this hymn tune (pickup +
   16 bars of 3/4) leaves limited room for antiphonal exchange
   or canonic imitation without departing substantially from
   the original. Full homophony with linear inner-voice
   independence is the primary texture. A second texture type
   (contrary motion between voice pairs) provides melodic
   interest. A formal antiphonal section or canonic treatment
   would require extending the arrangement beyond the given
   notes, which was not done in order to remain faithful to
   the original hymn.

PERFORMANCE NOTES
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
- The piece ends on C#m (vi), not E major (I). This is authentic
  to the medieval modal character of the 'Agincourt' tune and
  should be understood as a Phrygian/Dorian inflection on E
  rather than an incomplete cadence. Singers should treat the
  final C#m as a point of repose, not of tension.

- The !sintro!/!eintro! markers indicate the interior verse
  repeat. All 8 voices observe these repeat marks together.

- The harp doubles the diatonic inner voices naturally; S1's
  accidentals (none present in this arrangement, since the
  original soprano is fully diatonic in E major) would provide
  the characteristic lever-harp color if accidentals existed.
  In this piece, the harp and all voices share the same
  diatonic set — an unusually unified sonority.
