ARRANGER NOTES — Break Forth, O Beauteous Heavenly Light (SSAATTBB)
===================================================================
Arrangement for 8-voice double choir with lever harp accompaniment.
Source: Bach chorale BWV 248/12, from Christmas Oratorio (1734). Public domain.
Original SATB by J.S. Bach; melody 'Ermuntre dich' by Johann Schop (1641).
Words: Johann Rist (1641), translated by John Troutbeck (1885).

TEXTURE PLAN
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

The arrangement opens with six voices (S1, S2, A1, A2, Baritone, Bass I)
for Phrase 1 (bars 1-3), providing a transparent, hymn-like texture that
presents the A-phrase melody before the full ensemble arrives. Tenor I and
Tenor II are tacet during this phrase, creating an intentional register gap
between the alto pair and the bass pair that gives the opening a lean,
chorale-like character. The TTBB choir joins on the Phrase 2 pickup (before
bar 4), completing the double choir for the first time; Phrases 2-4 use all
eight voices. The dynamic arc moves from quiet and focused (6 voices, bars
1-3) through an antiphonal tutti (Phrase 2, bars 4-6), a homophonic B-section
with independent inner voice motion (Phrase 3, bars 7-9), to a broad climax
and suspension-laden final cadence (Phrase 4, bars 10-12).

Texture types used:

  1. Reduced-voice homophony (bars 1-3): S1+S2 carry the melody in parallel
     thirds; A1+A2 provide inner harmonic support; Bar and B1 anchor the bass.
     The absence of T1/T2 opens the middle register and reserves the full
     ensemble sound for later phrases.

  2. Antiphonal double-choir (bars 4-6): SSAA choir carries the A-phrase
     melody in their natural upper register while TTBB mirrors the same
     harmonic content a register below. The two choirs share the same rhythm
     and text simultaneously, but the separation of registers creates an
     antiphonal richness characteristic of double-choir writing.

  3. Full 8-voice homophony with independent inner voices (bars 7-9): All
     eight voices move in the same harmonic rhythm, but A1, A2, T1, and T2
     are given genuinely independent melodic lines (stepwise contrary and
     oblique motion) rather than block-chord doubling. The half-cadence at
     bar 9 is underscored by 4-3 suspension figures in A1/A2 (D#4→C#4 and
     B3→A3 respectively) over the sustained B major dominant.

  4. Climactic full texture with suspension at final cadence (bars 10-12):
     Phrase 4 reuses the B-section material at full 8-voice intensity. S1
     reaches C#5 at bar 10 (the arrangement's peak) while B1 sustains E2,
     producing the maximum ensemble spread. Bar 11 introduces 4-3 suspension
     figures simultaneously in A1 (A3→G#3), A2 (F#3→D#3), T1 (F#3→D#3
     written), and T2 (D#3→B2 written), creating a stretto-like accumulation
     of suspensions before the final E major whole note at bar 12.

RULE COMPROMISES
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

1. T1 and T2 (Phrases 2-4) largely shadow the A1/A2 harmonic material at
   the octave below, particularly in bars 4-6 where T1=A1 and T2=A2 a
   register apart. Bars 7-11 give T1 and T2 more independence (they follow
   the B-section inner voice material from Bach's tenor voice rather than
   doubling A1/A2 exactly). Rule 1 prohibits parallel octaves between any
   two adjacent voices; T1 and T2 are not adjacent to A1 and A2 (separated
   by the staff boundary), so this is permissible. The double-choir antiphonal
   effect in bars 4-6 is intentional.

2. Opening texture (bars 1-3): T1 and T2 are silent. The instruction that
   "every one of the 8 voices must be a singable independent melodic line"
   implies continuous participation. The rests implement the required
   "open with reduced texture (4-6 voices)" from Step 7. This architectural
   choice is justified by the explicit dynamic-shape rule, which overrides
   the constant-participation guidance for the opening phrase only.

3. Bar 9 (half-cadence fermata): A2 moves by a descending minor second
   (B3→A3) over the dominant B chord. Strictly this is a 4-3 suspension
   resolving correctly, but A3 is the 7th of the B major chord (not a
   standard chord tone in pure triadic writing). The diatonic constraint
   permits A natural as a scale degree of E major, and A resolves naturally
   to G# in bar 10. This was retained for the suspension color; replacing
   A3 with G#3 would eliminate the expressive dissonance at the cadence.

4. Baritone and Bass I share a unison (B2) on the weak beat 3 of bars 2,
   5, and 11 (beats that lead to dominant-resolution motion). These are
   consecutive in the sense that they recur across similar phrases, but
   they never occur on consecutive beats within a single bar. The rules
   permit within-pair unisons occasionally; these three occurrences across
   12 bars are contextually motivated by voice leading convergence at the
   V chord and fall within acceptable usage.

5. Pickup bars for T1 and T2 use B (B4 written = B3 concert) and G (G4
   written = G3 concert) respectively, which are not strongly independent
   melodic gestures — they serve purely as chord-tone entries before the
   first full bar of each phrase. This is the most natural entry for the
   tenor voices and prioritizes ensemble security over melodic distinction
   at the pickup moment.
