TRANSPOSITION
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
Original key:  F major (1 flat)
Chosen key:    F major (1 flat) — no transposition required
Semitones:     0
Direction:     n/a

F major is valid for lever harp in Eb storage tuning. Required lever
positions: Bb lever UP (raised), all others down. The remaining six
strings (Eb Bb F C G D) stay at Eb-storage pitch; only Bb is raised to
produce Bb, giving F major (F G A Bb C D E).

No range problems were encountered with the original key. All 8 voices
fall comfortably within professional ranges in F major.

HARMONIC SUBSTITUTIONS
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
Bar 18 (section B, bar 2):
  Original S1 melody: c d eb — the Eb is a flattened 7th over a dominant
  chord (C major triad), producing a momentary Bb7 / mixolydian color.
  S1 sings the Eb freely (melody voice, accidentals permitted). All other

  diatonic terms is not possible — C major requires E natural which is
  diatonic). The inner voices hold diatonic chord tones of F major (F,
  C, D, G) on beat 3, so the harp's diatonic strings do not conflict with
  S1's Eb; the Eb sounds as an expressive leading-edge passing tone in the
  melody against the harp's diatonic texture.

Bar 22 (section B, bar 6 — repeat of bar 18):
  Same treatment as bar 18. S1 Eb is melodic color; all other voices
  remain diatonic.

DIATONIC VIOLATIONS CAUGHT
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
1. Original S2V2 (Bass), section B bar 5 (overall bar 21):
   Note: =B,, (B natural, concert)
   In F major, B natural is not diatonic (the 4th degree is Bb).
   Original function: passing tone in a descent F–D–B(nat)–G.
   Replacement: Bb,, (remain on Bb, diatonic), approaching the following
   G,2 by step (Bb → A → G implied over two beats).
   The Bb strengthens the subdominant color at that point in the phrase
   rather than tonicizing G minor with a raised leading tone. In the
   arrangement V:8 (Bass I) uses A,4 F,2 at bar 21, which is entirely
   diatonic and follows the original bass movement to the mediant.

2. Original S1V2 (Alto), final section bar 4 (overall bar 28):
   Note: ^C (C-sharp, concert)
   In F major, C# is not diatonic.
   Original context: an inner-voice chromatic neighbor or passing tone
   approaching D in what functions as a secondary dominant inflection
   (A7 or D major tonicization).
   Replacement: The arrangement assigns V:2 (Soprano II) D2 C4 at bar 28,
   using the diatonic C natural. The brief A-major tonicization is
   dissolved; the harmony reverts to a diatonic ii chord (Gm) on beat 1
   moving to C (V) across bar 28–29, which is entirely diatonic and
   preserves the cadential approach to the final A section.

3. Original S2V2 (Bass), final bar 32:
   Chord [F,,6 F,6] — both Fs are diatonic (the tonic root). No
   replacement needed. Retained in V:8 as [F,,6 F,6].

TEXTURE PLAN
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
The arrangement opens (bars 1–8, first A section) with measured full
8-voice homophony in a moderate registral spread — voices pair closely
to establish blend without exposing extreme registers. The B section
(bars 17–24) introduces antiphonal exchange: the SSAA choir carries the
melodic sequence (c4 c2A, c4 cde) while the TTBB choir provides a
sustained harmonic platform beneath, trading roles mid-phrase for the
descending answer (d2c B2 A2). The final A section (bars 25–32) returns
to full homophony with growing registral expansion, reaching the widest
spread at bar 32 where S1 holds F5 against B1's [F,,6 F,6] — a full
four-octave spread. A 4–3 suspension in the inner voices at bar 31
(A,–G, in Alto II, G,–F, in Tenor II) creates a gentle suspension before
the tonic resolution.

Texture types used:
  1. Full 8-voice homophony — sections A (first and repeat) and final A
  2. Antiphonal exchange (SSAA vs. TTBB) — section B
  3. Call and response between outer voices (S1 melody, B1 bass) with
     inner voices providing harmonic support — bars 17–20
  4. Suspensions at cadence — bar 31 leading into bar 32

RULE COMPROMISES
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
1. Bar 7–8, 15–16, 31–32 — V:7 and V:8 in sustained unison:
   Bar and B1 hold C,4 C,2 and F,6 in unison at every phrase ending
   (bars 7–8, 15–16, 31–32). This violates the "avoid unisons on
   consecutive beats within the same pair" guidance. Reason: the
   original hymn cadences strongly to the bass root; unison bass at
   phrase endings is an idiomatic choral practice and provides the
   grounding weight that the homophonic texture requires. The voices
   diverge by step (or to octave) immediately after each cadence.

2. Bars 28–30 — V:4 (Alto II) low register:
   As noted in Range Log above, A2 dips below its comfortable D3 floor
   at bars 28–29. This was retained to preserve smooth voice leading
   in the final A section's bass descent. The passage lasts only two
   bars and sits in an inner voice beneath three other moving parts.

3. Section B (bars 17–24) — reduced melodic independence in T2 and Bar:
   In the antiphonal texture of section B, T2 (V:6) and Bar (V:7) move
   largely in parallel thirds with T1 (V:5) and B1 (V:8) respectively.
   This is a deliberate textural choice to provide a solid harmonic
   foundation for the SSAA choir's melodic sequence above. The paired
   parallel thirds are a classic device in choral writing and are
   acceptable here given the brevity of the section (8 bars) and the
   melodic independence restored in the surrounding A sections.
