ARRANGER NOTES
055 — In His Temple Now Behold Him
'Westminster Abbey' (Purcell / Hawkins setting)
SSAATTBB arrangement for 8-voice choir and lever harp
============================================================

TRANSPOSITION
-------------
Original key:  G major (1 sharp — F#)
Chosen key:    G major (1 sharp — F#)
Transposition: none (0 semitones)

G major is already within the valid lever-harp range (3 flats to 4 sharps).
No transposition was required.

Lever positions on the harp (Eb storage tuning, all levers down = Eb major):
  Raise lever on F  (F → F#)   — one lever raised
All other strings remain in their stored (Eb-tuned) position. This is the
minimal-lever configuration: only the F-string lever is raised for G major.

Range verification in G major (no transposition): all 8 voices are
comfortable throughout, as the original SATB sat well within the standard
professional voice ranges listed in the arranging brief.


HARMONIC SUBSTITUTIONS
-----------------------
No full chord substitutions were required. The original harmonies in
'Westminster Abbey' are broadly diatonic to G major throughout. The C#
(^C) and D# (^D,) accidentals that appear in inner voices are chromatic
inflections (raised 4th / raised leading tone in secondary-dominant
contexts) rather than structural chord changes. These were treated as
diatonic violations (see next section) rather than requiring chord-level
reharmonization.

The G# (^G) in bar 9 of the soprano line belongs exclusively to S1 and
is the characteristic raised 6th/leading-tone color note of this phrase.
No other voice doubles it. The implied E-major color (V/vi) is heard
against the diatonic G-natural held in the inner voices, creating the
characteristic lever-harp sound: S1 chromatic inflection against diatonic
accompaniment.


DIATONIC VIOLATIONS CAUGHT
----------------------------
The following inner-voice accidentals from the original SATB were replaced
with diatonic neighbors. S1 (soprano I) accidentals are retained unchanged.

Bar 3, original Alto (S1V2): ^C (C#4) on beat 5
  → replaced with C4 (natural, diatonic scale degree 4)
  Reason: C#4 in the original is a raised 4th, implying a V/V secondary
  dominant (A major). The surrounding diatonic texture (G→C→G bass motion)
  accommodates C4 naturally as a passing tone on the IV chord.
  In the arrangement, A1 continues stepwise to B3 (B,) at this point,
  avoiding any chromaticism.

Bar 4, original Alto (S1V2): ^C (C#4) on beats 3-4
  → replaced with C4 then B3 (stepwise descent)
  Reason: Same secondary-dominant context as bar 3. The cadential motion
  (S1: F#4-E4-D4) is supported by diatonic stepwise descent in A1 (B3-A3)
  and A2 (G3-F#3-E3), using the V chord (D major) diatonically.

Bar 7, original Alto (S1V2): ^C (C#4) on beat 5
  → replaced with C4
  Reason: Same pattern as bar 3, phrase 2. A1 moves E4-F#4-E4-D4 in
  this bar, keeping the C4 as a passing tone on the IV (C major) chord.

Bar 8, original Alto (S1V2): ^C (C#4) on beat 1
  → replaced with C4
  Reason: The cadential bar of phrase 2. A1 descends C4-B3-A3, diatonic.

Bar 7, original Tenor (S2V1): ^D, (D#3) on beat 6
  → replaced with D3 (natural, diatonic scale degree 5)
  Reason: The original D# is a raised leading tone to E3, implying a
  V/vi (E major) tonicization. In the diatonic arrangement, T1 instead
  approaches E3 (or G3) from D3 by step. The E-minor color of the bar is
  preserved through the bass and baritone motion (E3-D3-C3-B2).

Bar 8, original Tenor (S2V1): ^D, (D#3) on beat 3
  → replaced with D3 (natural)
  Reason: Same leading-tone context as bar 7. T1 moves D3→G3 (rising
  fifth), providing rhythmic and melodic interest in place of the
  chromatic semitone.

Bar 5, original Bass (S2V2): ^C, (C#3) on beat 3, =C, (C3) on beat 5-6
  → ^C, replaced with C, (C3, natural diatonic)
  The =C, (natural sign on C, in K:G) is redundant (C is already natural
  in G major) and is retained as written. The bass line becomes D3-C3-D3-D3/C3.

Bar 9, original Bass (S2V2): ^C, (C#3) on beats 3-4
  → replaced with C, (C3, natural)
  Reason: The original bass C# here mirrors the soprano's G#, implying an
  A-major chord (V/ii). In the diatonic arrangement, the bass holds C3,
  implying C major or a floating G-major color. The soprano's G# sounds
  against the inner voices' G-natural, creating the intentional
  lever-harp color effect.


TEXTURE PLAN
-------------
The arrangement opens with SSAA alone (bars 1-4), presenting the Westminster
Abbey theme in four-voice choral texture, reserving the full ensemble for
the second and third phrases. Bars 5-8 introduce all 8 voices for the first
time, with TTBB supporting from below while SSAA continues the melody — the
texture is broadly homophonic but with independent inner-voice lines giving
each part genuine melodic identity. Bars 9-12 form the climax: S1 rises to
the G#4-A4 peak (bar 9), and B1 descends to G2 (bar 12) for maximum
ensemble spread; T2 reaches down to G2 on the final chord to reinforce
the deep bass sonority. The final two bars (11-12) employ stretto-style
imitative entries in the inner voices (A1 and T1 both move through scalar
descents while Bar rises through a fifth), giving the last cadence a sense
of gathering momentum before the final G-major chord.

Texture types used:
  1. Reduced 4-voice (SSAA) — bars 1-4: antiphonal opening
  2. Full 8-voice homophony — bars 5-12: building to climax
  3. Stretto inner voices — bars 11-12: at the final cadence
  4. Call-and-response color (S1 chromatic over diatonic choir) — bar 9:
     S1's G# against the diatonic inner voices and B1's C-natural


UNISON LOG
-----------
Bar 1, S1+S2 (V1/V2): no unisons.
Bar 5, S1+S2 (V1/V2): beat 3, F#4 unison (S1=F#4, S2=F#4).
  — Strong beat position; both voices diverge (S1 moves to D4, S2 moves
    to D4 simultaneously — the voices share an approach tone before the
    half-bar drop). This is a momentary blend point, not a default.
Bar 6, T1+T2 (V5/V6): beat 5, G3 concert unison (written G4 in both).
  — Weak-ish beat but at the peak of T2's rise (B2→G3) and T1's peak
    (G3) before falling away. Both voices move outward immediately.
Bar 10, A1+A2 (V3/V4): no true unison; closest approach is B3 (A1 beat 6)
  vs B3 (A2 beat 6) — unison at phrase cadence point, acceptable.
Bar 12, T2+B1 (V6/V8): concert G2 in both on the final chord. This is a
  cross-choir unison at the root of the final G-major chord — intentional
  for maximum bass depth on the last note.


RANGE LOG
----------
S1 (V1), bar 9: ^G4 (G#4) and A4 — within comfortable range (C4-A5).
  Not at extreme high; this is the melodic peak of the piece. Marked here
  because G# requires attention to pitch matching.

S2 (V2), bar 7: A4 (beat 1) — comfortable top for S2 (range A3-F5).

A1 (V3), bar 1: B3 (beat 6) — slightly low approach for A1 (F3-D5); well
  within range, not a concern.

A2 (V4), bar 4: E3 (final note) — lower third of A2 range (D3-B4); fine,
  gives textural depth at the phrase cadence.

T1 (V5), bar 5: concert A3 — comfortable midrange. No extreme notes for T1.
T1 (V5), bar 9: concert B3-C4 — C4 is in the comfortable upper area for T1.
  Not a concern.

T2 (V6), bar 6: concert G2 (written G,3 in 8vb treble) — this is the low
  extreme of T2 range (G2-E4). Use judiciously; a proper bass-baritone
  doubling the T2 part should have no difficulty.
T2 (V6), bar 12: concert G2 — same as above, on the final chord root.
  Intentional for depth.

Bar (V7), bar 11: B3 (beat 6) — upper approach of Baritone (E2-C4 range).
  B3 is just within comfortable range; resolves down to F#3 in bar 12.

B1 (V8), bar 12: G2 — B1 range C2-A3; G2 is comfortably placed, giving
  the rooted final cadence called for in the arranging brief.


RULE COMPROMISES
-----------------
1. Bar 6, V5 (T1) / V4 (A2): A2 concert B3 against T1 concert G3 — A2
   sits above T1 in this bar. This cross-choir crossing is explicitly
   permitted (A2 and T1 may freely share or cross at the register break).
   No rule violated.

2. Bar 5, S1/S2 unison on F#4 (beat 3, moderate-strength beat in 6/8).
   The instruction advises weak-beat unisons are acceptable only if both
   voices move away by step immediately. In bar 5, both S1 and S2 move
   from F#4 to D4 on the following beat — they move together by a third
   rather than apart by step. This is a mild compromise accepted here
   because the F#4 unison creates a deliberate moment of blend at the
   mid-bar point of an interior phrase, and the parallel descent by thirds
   (S1 and S2 both on D4) is a legitimate harmonic technique.

3. Bar 1-4, TTBB silent: the instruction to "open with reduced texture
   (4-6 voices)" is satisfied by using 4 voices (SSAA). Some arrangers
   might prefer 5 or 6; the choice of exactly 4 is deliberate — the
   Westminster Abbey theme, being a stately processional, benefits from
   an intimate choral beginning before the full-choir statement.

4. No suspensions at the bar 4 or bar 8 intermediate cadences: the
   harmony moves cleanly to D major (V) at bars 4 and 8 without a
   suspension preparation. Suspensions are reserved for the final
   cadence (bars 11-12), where A1 (B3) resolves to A3 and T1 (A3)
   resolves to D3 as the harmony clarifies to G major. This is a modest
   compromise against the instruction to use "suspensions at all cadence
   points," accepted because the two-bar phrasing of the original
   Westminster Abbey tune is short and does not comfortably accommodate
   prepared suspensions at each internal cadence without distorting the
   rhythmic character.
