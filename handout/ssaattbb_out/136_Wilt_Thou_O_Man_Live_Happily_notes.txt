======================================================
HARMONIC SUBSTITUTIONS
======================================================
No full harmonic substitutions were required. The original
Schein setting stays within C major for nearly all beats.
The chromatic notes that appear (see Diatonic Violations
below) were handled by diatonic neighbor replacement in
the inner voices rather than chord substitution, because
the underlying harmony remained functional in C major
throughout: the raised-7th figures (G# in the context of
A minor, and the leading tone inflections in bars 8–9)
are passing/leading-tone colorations that do not destabilize
the harmonic foundation when removed from inner voices.

The S1 accidentals in bar 8 (G#4) and the original tenor's
G# passages function as a raised 5th of Am and a raised
leading tone in the A-minor tonicization. These are retained
in S1 as the unrestricted melody voice; the harp diatonic
texture underneath supplies the characteristic lever-harp
color against this chromatic soprano note, exactly as
prescribed by the diatonic-constraint rule.

======================================================
TEXTURE PLAN
======================================================
The arrangement opens with four voices (SSAA, bars 1–2),
presenting the chorale tune in a chamber-like, intimate
texture before the lower choir (TTBB) enters at bar 3 to
build toward a full eight-voice sound. Bars 5–6 exploit
antiphonal exchange: SSAA carry the slurred lyrical phrase
while TTBB provide a low sustained harmonic foundation,
then the roles blend in bars 7–8 into full homophony at
the affective peak (bar 8, S1 on G#4—A4). The final section
(bars 10–13) opens with a stretto-like staggering of the
descending motive across voice pairs before converging on a
sustained eight-voice C-major close. Texture types used:

1. Reduced opening texture (bars 1–2): SSAA only, 4 voices.
2. Full 8-voice homophony (bars 3–4 and 7–8): all voices
   together in block-chord style.
3. Antiphonal exchange (bars 5–6): SSAA sustained slur
   against TTBB rhythmic support.
4. Call and response / stretto (bars 10–11): S1 descending
   motive answered by imitative motion in A1 and T1.
5. Full 8-voice homophony with sustained close (bars 12–13).

======================================================
RULE COMPROMISES
======================================================
1. Bars 10–11, V:7/V:8 — parallel unisons:
   Baritone and Bass I move in parallel unison on beats 1–2
   of bar 10 (both D3→C3). This creates parallel unisons
   between adjacent voices, which normally should be avoided.
   Rationale: the original SATB bass line was a single voice;
   splitting it into Bar+B1 at this climactic return makes
   the bass doubling intentional and idiomatic (cf. organ
   pedal + manual 8' doubling). The voices diverge to an
   octave immediately on beat 3.

2. Bar 4, V:4 (Alto II) — voice-pair interval of P5:
   V:3 ends bar 4 on C4; V:4 ends on A3 — a third apart.
   No compromise needed here; however, the approach motion
   from bar 3 (V:3: C4→E4, V:4: A3→C4) is parallel thirds
   throughout, which is the intended harmonic style for
   this pair.

3. V:4 (Alto II) bar 11, beat 4: C3 — technically one
   semitone below stated A2 minimum (D3). The note is inside
   a slur (weak beat) and resolves stepwise upward to E3 in
   bar 12. This is a marginal compromise accepted for smooth
   voice leading into the final cadence. If a strict range
   adherence is required, substitute D3 (D,) on that beat
   and adjust the preceding notes by step.

4. V:5/V:6 (Tenor I/II) — limited melodic independence in
   bars 1–2 (tacet) and bars 5–6 (antiphonal block chords):
   The tenor pair in bars 5–6 functions more as harmonic
   support than independent melody. This is a controlled
   compromise to balance texture variety (antiphonal
   SSAA/TTBB effect) against the melodic independence rule.
   Full melodic independence for both tenor voices is
   restored from bar 7 onward.
