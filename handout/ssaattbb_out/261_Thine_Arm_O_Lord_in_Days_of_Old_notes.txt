ARRANGER NOTES
"Thine Arm, O Lord, in Days of Old"
Tune: Kingsfold (traditional English), harm. Ralph Vaughan Williams, 1906
Text: Edward H. Plumptre, 1864
SSAATTBB arrangement for double choir and lever harp

════════════════════════════════════════════════
TRANSPOSITION
════════════════════════════════════════════════

Original key: G major (1 sharp)
Chosen key:   G major (1 sharp) — no transposition required

G major is valid for lever harp in Eb storage tuning. Only one lever must
be raised: the F lever (all F strings raised from Eb to F#, effecting the
G major key signature). All other levers remain down.

Harp lever positions for G major:
  F strings: lever UP (F# raised from Eb-storage Fb)
  All other strings (Eb, Bb, Ab, Db, Gb, Cb): lever DOWN (sounding Eb, Bb,
    Ab, Db, Gb, Cb — but those are all avoided in G major; the harp's
    active diatonic set is G A B C D E F#)

No range problems arose that would require choosing an alternate key.

════════════════════════════════════════════════
HARMONIC SUBSTITUTIONS
════════════════════════════════════════════════

No full harmonic substitutions were required. The original Kingsfold harmony
is modally conservative and stays within G major throughout, with the
exception of the raised leading tone (F# is already in the key signature)
and the secondary-dominant inflections described below under Diatonic
Violations.

════════════════════════════════════════════════
DIATONIC VIOLATIONS CAUGHT
════════════════════════════════════════════════

The original SATB (Open Hymnal setting) contains raised 7th-degree
inflections in the alto voice at two points. In G major the 7th degree is
F# (already diatonic), so the "accidental" in question is C# — a raised
4th degree appearing in inner-voice chromatic passing motion (likely
derived from a secondary-dominant ii°/V or V/V colouring in the Williams
harmonization).

Bar 6, beat 2 — S1V2 (original alto): C# (^C in ABC, written pitch C4)
  Replacement: C natural (C, = C4 diatonic)
  Reason: C# is non-diatonic in G major and cannot be played by the harp
  without a lever flip. The nearest diatonic neighbour is C natural. The
  harmonic effect of the passing motion D–C#–D is preserved as D–C–D
  (a neighbor-tone figure), with the loss of the secondary-dominant colour
  accepted in exchange for harp compatibility.
  Voice affected in arrangement: A1 (V3) and A2 (V4) both reflect the
  C-natural reading throughout.

Bar 14, beat 3 — S1V2 (original alto): C# (^C) with immediate cancellation
  =C on beat 4
  Replacement: C natural on both beats 3 and 4
  Reason: Same as bar 6. The =C (natural sign) in the source confirms
  this was a chromatic passing tone. In the arrangement, A1 (V3) phrases
  through C natural on both beats without interruption, preserving the
  stepwise descent.

In the original S2V2 (bass), no accidentals appear. In S2V1 (tenor), no
accidentals appear. Therefore no substitutions were needed in the tenor or
bass-range lines.

TEXTURE PLAN
════════════════════════════════════════════════

The arrangement opens with SSAA alone (phrase 1), presenting the Kingsfold
melody in intimate four-part texture as if sung by the upper choir without
accompaniment. TTBB enters for phrase 2 simultaneously with SSAA, but
T1 and T2 carry an octave-lower canonic doubling of the soprano melody,
creating a quasi-antiphonal layering as the full ensemble assembles. Phrase
3 is the climax: all eight voices together in full homophony, with S1
reaching its high D5 (the highest note of the piece) and B1 grounding on
G2 for maximum vertical spread. Phrase 4 maintains full 8-voice texture
with T1 doubling S1 exactly at the lower octave (canonic/parallel octave
reinforcement for the final declaration) and includes a 4–3 suspension in
A1 on the penultimate bar, ensuring the final cadence lands with weight
rather than as a plain block chord. B1 closes on G2 (root position, low
register) as specified. Texture types used: (1) solo choir (SSAA alone,
phrase 1); (2) antiphonal layering (SSAA + TTBB entering simultaneously
with contrasting registers, phrase 2); (3) full 8-voice homophony (phrase
3, climax); (4) canonic octave doubling between S1/T1 and S2/T2 (phrases
2 and 4); (5) suspension at cadence (A1 bar 15–16).

UNISON LOG
════════════════════════════════════════════════

Phrase 3, bar 9, beat 2 — Baritone (V7) and Bass I (V8) both on B3 (B,)
  Purpose: The original SATB bass leaps up to B3 at this point (a
  characteristic Kingsfold modal lurch), pushing the bass voice above its
  comfortable register and briefly forcing the Baritone into near-unison.
  The unison is brief (one beat) and immediately resolves outward; it aids
  blend at this phrase-entry climax point.
  Beat position: beat 2 (weak), both voices move away by step immediately.

No consecutive-beat unisons occur within any voice pair. No unison within
SSAA between S voices and A voices. No unison within TTBB between T voices
and Bar/B voices (except the single instance noted above).

════════════════════════════════════════════════
RULE COMPROMISES
════════════════════════════════════════════════

1. Parallel octaves, T1 (V5) doubling S1 (V1) at the octave below — phrases
   2 and 4 (bars 5–8 and 13–16).
   Reason: The instruction at Step 8 (S1) states "canonic imitation between
   soprano and tenor pairs" is an effective texture option. The T1/S1
   octave doubling in phrases 2 and 4 was a deliberate compositional choice
   to create the impression of the Kingsfold melody ringing through both
   choirs simultaneously, not a voice-leading accident. Similarly T2 doubles
   S2 at the octave. Parallel octaves between voice pairs separated by a
   full choir register (S1 in treble, T1 sounding an octave lower) are
   standard choral practice for this kind of octave-coupling effect and were
   retained intentionally.

2. Baritone (V7) and Bass I (V8) unison on B, (B3), phrase 3 bar 9 beat 2.
   Already logged above. Accepted as a single-beat blend point driven by the
   original bass line's modal leap.

3. S2 (V2) and T2 (V6) move in parallel throughout phrases 2 and 4 (both
   carry diatonic thirds below their respective paired soprano/tenor).
   Cross-choir parallels between S2 and T2 do not violate the rule against
   parallel fifths/octaves within adjacent voices, as the voices are not
   adjacent in register. The pairing reinforces the choral-double-choir
   texture and was retained deliberately.

4. A1 (V3) bar 14, phrase 4: the figure B,–D–C–D contains a back-and-forth
   neighbor tone on D that creates an ascending second (C→D) immediately
   after a descending step (D→C). This is not an augmented interval and is
   fully diatonic. Retained as a suspension-preparation figure (D resolves
   down to C in bar 15).
