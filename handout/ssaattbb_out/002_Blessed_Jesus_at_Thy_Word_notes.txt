  Reason: Alto had ^C (C#) as the 3rd of an A major or E major

  (IV), a smooth diatonic substitute. Voice leading is preserved:
  the alto line D D C D becomes D E C D (step motion, no augmented
  interval introduced).

Bar 12, beat 2 (section B):
  Original chord implied: A major (V/ii, requiring C# in bass as
  raised 3rd)
  Replacement: A minor (ii in G major, diatonic)
  Reason: Bass had ^C, (C#) passing from B2 to D3. C natural
  (C3) substituted, producing A minor (ii) instead of A major
  (V/ii). The melodic contour B→C→D is preserved exactly, with
  only the quality of the passing harmony changed from major to
  minor. All other voices are unaffected.

TEXTURE PLAN
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
The arrangement opens antiphonally: bars 1–5 present the first A
phrase with SSAA choir only (4 voices), creating an intimate,
chamber-like texture. On the exact repeat (bars 6–10), the TTBB
choir enters in full, establishing the complete 8-voice homophonic
texture with a doubling warmth that was absent before. The B
section (bars 11–15) sustains full 8-voice writing and builds
through canonic contrary motion in the inner voices toward the
climax at bar 14 beat 1 (S1 on C5, B1 on G2, maximum ensemble
spread). A written-out 4-3 suspension in A1 (C4 held over the
penultimate D chord) provides the cadential dissonance asked for
at the final close.

Textures employed:
  1. Antiphonal (SSAA vs. TTBB): bars 1–5 (SSAA) vs. 6–10 (full)
  2. Full 8-voice homophony: bars 6–15
  3. Contrary motion between A1/A2 and T1/T2 (quasi-canonic): bars 11–13
  4. Suspension at cadence (A1, 4-3): bar 14 beats 3–4

RULE COMPROMISES
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
1. A2 vs. T1 register (bars 6–13): The A2 voice occasionally
   approaches the same register as T1 (both near B3–G3), requiring
   careful management of the A2 line to stay above T1. In bars 6–8
   this produces cross-choir unisons on several weak beats (logged
   above), all of which are explicitly permitted by the arranging
   rules. No actual voice crossing between A2 and T1 occurs.

2. A1 bar 14, beat 3–4 (C4 suspension over D chord): The note C4
   functions as a 7th above the D major chord (D F# A C). This is
   a written-out suspension (C resolves down to B3 conceptually, but
   is held as a half note to the bar line and resolves implicitly
   at bar 15 where the voice moves to D4). The dissonance is mild
   (major 7th in inner voice), well-prepared by step (D→C), and
   all surrounding voices are consonant. This is the principal
   cadential suspension called for in Step 7.

3. S1 climax note (bar 14 b1, C5): The piece does not reach the
   G5–A5 climax range specified for S1. The source melody peaks at
   C5 (the `c` in the original ABC). Forcing S1 to G5 would require
   recomposing the original melody, which is outside the scope of
   expanding a given hymn tune. C5 is treated as the effective
   climax within the constraints of this specific tune.

4. Bar voice bar 9, beat 2 (B2): The Baritone descends to B2 on
   a weak beat approaching the half-note F#3. While B2 is within
   the Bass I range, it sits at the lower edge of the Baritone
   comfortable zone. This arose from the strict "third above B1"
   harmonization when B1 moves through G2. The voice is singable
   and the descent is by step (C3→B2), but conductors may wish to
   adjust the dynamic to mp at this point.

5. Minimal dynamic contrast between bars 6–15: Once the full
   8-voice texture enters at bar 6, there is no further reduction in
   voice count. The contrast is achieved through harmonic rhythm and
   inner-voice activity rather than subtractive texture. This
   reflects the short length of the hymn (15 bars) and the desire
   to keep all voices engaged after their entry.
