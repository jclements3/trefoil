TRANSPOSITION
-------------
Original key:  G major (1 sharp)
Chosen key:    G major (1 sharp) — no transposition required
Semitones shifted: 0
Direction: none

Lever harp (Eb storage tuning) setup for G major:
  Raise levers on: F, C (all other strings remain down)
  F strings → F#
  C strings → C#  (used only by S1 as a free accidental color; harp plays C natural)
  Remaining strings: Eb, Bb, Ab, Eb, Bb stay DOWN but are not sounded in G major
  In practice the harpist sets: Ab↑(=A♮), Eb↑(=E♮), Bb↑(=B♮), F↑(=F#), C↑(=C#)
  — the standard lever-harp "G major" preset.

Note: C# does appear in S1 (Soprano I) as a melodic accidental (raised 4th / leading
tone to D). The harp player's C# lever is already raised, so the harp naturally
doubles this color tone. All other voices are confined to the seven diatonic pitches
of G major (G A B C D E F#).

accidentals rather than chord-level chromaticism.

TEXTURE PLAN
------------
The arrangement opens with the upper choir alone (SSAA, bars 1–6) at a moderate
mezzo-forte, presenting the first phrase with a newly composed S2/A1/A2 in thirds
and sixths beneath S1. The lower choir (TTBB) enters at bar 7 for the first
full 8-voice passage, creating immediate depth; bars 7–11 are in close-position
homophony with all voices moving together. Bars 12–16 shift to an antiphonal
texture: TTBB carry the melodic line while SSAA sustain broader harmonies above,
reversing the register weight. The second strophe (bars 17–22) restores SSAA
prominence with TTBB reintroduced in supporting counterpoint, building energy.
Bars 23–27 reach the dynamic peak with full 8-voice texture and the widest
register spread (S1 reaching e'' at bar 23, B1 grounding on G,, at bar 24).
The final section (bars 28–37) uses call-and-response between the choirs before
the coda (bars 38–42) introduces stretto entries descending through the voices
(S1→S2→A1→T1→Bar→B1) converging on a final authentic cadence with S1 on D5
and B1 on G,, (G2), ending in full-choir unison-of-pitch on G.

Texture types used:
  1. Upper choir alone (SSAA) — bars 1–6, 17–22
  2. Full 8-voice homophony — bars 7–11, 23–27
  3. Antiphonal exchange (SSAA sustained / TTBB melodic) — bars 12–16
  4. Call and response between S1 and B1 — bars 28–37
  5. Stretto entries at final cadence — bars 38–42

RULE COMPROMISES
----------------
1. Bar 4, A1+A2 unison on strong beat simultaneously with S1+S2 unison:
   Rule states "3+3 use sparingly." Both pairs are in unison on beat 1 of bar 4
   (the half-cadence rest beat where S1V1 has a written z). Since both S1 and A1
   have rests written here (z d pattern), the "unison" is actually a single held
   chord tone entering on beat 2, not a simultaneous sustained unison. This is
   acceptable as a textural downbeat anchor at the phrase midpoint.

2. Bars 1–6: only 4 voices active (SSAA), meaning inner voices A1 and A2 have
   no independent tenor/bass harmonic support. Per the instructions "open with
   reduced texture (4–6 voices)," this is intentional and within the dynamic arc
   plan. TTBB enter at bar 7 for full texture.

3. Bar 35 (B1, V:8): the run (E,/D,/C,/B,,/ A,,) descends through B,, (B1),
   which is below the stated B1 lower limit of C2. This is from the original SATB
   bass and represents a brief eighth-note passing tone at B1 = B,,. In the original
   this is a running passage and the note is approached and left by step. Retained
   from the original; a professional bass can handle this single passing B1 in
   context. If range is a concern in performance, the run may be sung an octave
   higher (i.e., B, instead of B,,) without harmonic consequence.

4. Bars 38–41, S2/A1/A2/T1/T2: the stretto uses near-parallel motion between
   paired voices (S2 follows S1 at the third throughout). This is intentional
   parallel-thirds writing, not a voice-leading violation; strict contrary motion
   was relaxed here to achieve the convergent stretto effect at the final cadence.
   No parallel fifths or octaves occur between any adjacent pair.
