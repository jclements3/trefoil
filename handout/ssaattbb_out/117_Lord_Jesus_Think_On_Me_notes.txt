ARRANGER NOTES
Lord Jesus, Think On Me — SSAATTBB Arrangement
'Southwell' (William Daman's Psalter, 1579)
Arranged for 8-voice ensemble with lever harp.
======================================================

TRANSPOSITION
--------------
Original key:  Ab major (4 flats — INVALID for lever harp)
Chosen key:    A major (3 sharps — valid)
Semitones:     +1 (up one semitone)
Direction:     UP — chosen because Ab is equidistant from G (1#, down 1) and
               A (3#, up 1); the instruction requires transposing UP when
               distance is equal, to preserve bass register.

Range check after transposition:
  S1 top note: E5 (bar 5) — well within range (comfortable top G5)
  B1 low note: F#2 (bar 8) — within bass range (C2–A3)
  No voice exceeded professional limits. A major confirmed.

Lever harp setup for A major (from Eb storage tuning):
  Raise levers on: Ab→A, Bb→B, Db→D  (i.e., levers for A, B, and D strings)
  Eb lever also raised (Eb→E) — four levers total.
  Harp strings sounding: A B C# D E F# G#
  The =F (F natural) sung by S1 only sounds against the harp's F# string —
  this cross-relation is intentional lever-harp color (see Diatonic Violations).

NOTE ON TRANSPOSITION NOTATION:
  In K:A, the key signature provides F#, C#, G#. All notes written without
  accidentals (F, C, G in the score) already carry their sharps from the
  key signature. The note mapping from K:Ab is exact and diatonic throughout
  all 8 voices.


HARMONIC SUBSTITUTIONS
-----------------------
No full harmonic substitutions were required. The original 'Southwell' is
a largely diatonic harmonisation in Ab major. Transposition to A major
preserves every chord and harmonic rhythm intact. No secondary dominants,
tonicisations, or chromatic chords appear in the original except for the
E-natural (raised 5th of Ab) in the original S1V2 (SATB alto) voice,
handled under Diatonic Violations below.

The final cadence (bar 8) is a IV chord (D major: D–F#–A) over B1=F#2
in the bass, giving a first-inversion subdominant at the phrase end.
This is idiomatic to the 'Southwell' source, which always closes hymn
phrases ready for repeat; it is not a substitution but a faithful
reproduction of the original harmonic intent.


DIATONIC VIOLATIONS CAUGHT
----------------------------
Original voice affected: S1V2 (SATB inner soprano / original SATB alto line)
This voice contained =E (E natural, i.e. the raised 5th of Ab major) at:

  Bar 1-2, beat 4 of bar 1: original =E4 (E natural in Ab) →
    Transposed: would become =F4 (F natural in A major — chromatic).
    Replacement: F#4 (diatonic F, the 6th degree of A major).
    Original context: inner voice passing from F to =E to F again
    (an ornamental chromatic neighbour). Replaced by stepwise diatonic
    motion C#4–D4–E4–D4 (A1 voice) and D3–D3–D3–C#3 (A2 voice),
    preserving the stepwise character without the chromatic inflection.

  Bar 6, beat 4: original =E4 in the alto → same =F replacement situation.
    Replacement: F#4 (diatonic). A1 voice at bar 6 beat 4 uses D4 (diatonic),
    moving naturally to C4 (C#4) at bar 7 beat 1.

  Bar 7, beats 3-4: original =E4 twice → same treatment.
    Replacement: A1 uses D4–C#4 (diatonic stepwise descent).

Summary: every instance of =E (E natural) in the original SATB alto/S1V2
voice was replaced by the nearest diatonic note (F# or E, whichever gave
better voice leading). The chromatic neighbour function is absorbed into
the diatonic stepwise lines of A1 and A2.

IMPORTANT — S1 NOTE: The =F (F natural) does NOT appear in S1 in this
arrangement. The original SATB soprano had no =E; all soprano notes were
already diatonic in Ab. S1 is therefore fully diatonic in A major and
requires no accidentals. The lever harp plays in unison with S1 everywhere.


TEXTURE PLAN
-------------
This is a short strophic hymn (8 bars plus pickup), so the textural arc
is concentrated within a single verse-length phrase, intended to repeat
with growing dynamics across multiple stanzas.

Texture type 1 — FULL 8-VOICE HOMOPHONY (bars 1–4, first phrase):
  All 8 voices enter simultaneously at the pickup and proceed in block-
  chord rhythm through bars 1–4. This establishes the hymn tune clearly
  and gives the congregation (or audience) immediate orientation. Inner
  voices A1, A2, T1, T2 have gentle contrary-motion lines against the
  outer voices but maintain the hymn's chordal pulse.

Texture type 2 — ANTIPHONAL EXCHANGE (bar 5 pickup → bar 6):
  The second phrase opens with the SSAA choir momentarily prominent
  (S1 and S2 take the melody into the upper register, C#5–E5) while
  the TTBB choir re-enters on the pickup with a lower, supporting
  harmonic figure. The effect is a subtle call-and-response at the
  phrase boundary.

Texture type 3 — CALL AND RESPONSE between outer voices (bars 6–8):
  At the approach to the final cadence, S1 leads the descending line
  (E5–D5–C#5–B4–A4–G#4–G#4) while B1 provides a contrary ascending
  walk (A3–E3–F#3–G#3), with the inner voices sustaining harmonic
  support. This creates a closing-in effect typical of professional
  choral finales, tightening the texture into the D-major plagal cadence.

Dynamic arc: Begin mp (full texture but restrained), grow through bars 5–7
toward mf/f, and settle to mp or p at the D-major phrase ending, ready
for the next stanza. Climax is reserved (E5 in S1 is the peak, not extreme;
this allows stanzas to build with the harp through the verse repetitions).

                 S2 moves to F#4 (step down). Blend emphasis on the repeated
                 tonic note; both voices step away immediately. ACCEPTABLE.

RANGE LOG
----------
S1, bar 5 (beats 2-3): E5 — this is the melodic peak of the piece. Falls at
  the opening of the second (climactic) phrase. Comfortable top is G5; E5
  is well within range. No concern.

B1, bar 8 (final bar): F#2 — the lowest sustained note in the piece.
  Falls at the plagal cadence phrase ending. Within B1 range (C2–A3).
  The D-major chord in first inversion (F#2 in bass) is idiomatic.
  No concern.

T2, bar 1 (beat 3): B3 written = B2 concert. Near the lower comfortable
  limit of T2 (G2–E4, comfortable top C4). B2 is a passing quarter note
  approached and left by step (E3–B2–A2 concert). Brief low note on a
  weak beat; any experienced tenor II can manage this. Slight concern —
  flag for the section leader to ensure adequate support.

A2, bar 2 (beats 1-3): F#3 sustained — the lower comfortable limit for A2
  (D3–B4 range). F#3 is the tonic's 6th degree; it provides harmonic
  support over the bar 2 D-major chord. On a sustained note, this is
  warm and entirely singable. No concern.

Bar (Baritone), bar 5 (beat 1): C4 = the very top of comfortable baritone
  range (E2–C4, comfortable top A3). C4 is above comfortable range by a
  third. Used at the phrase opening for a bright doubling of the 3rd above
  the bass. This falls at a textural peak (the beginning of the second
  phrase). Acceptable for professional singers; alert the section. Flag
  for rehearsal — if strain is heard, adjust Bar pickup and beat 1 to A3
  (A, in bass clef) and compensate by raising beats 2-4 of bar 5 by a
  step.


RULE COMPROMISES
-----------------
1. Parallel octaves S1–B1, bar 2 (beats 1–3):
   Bar 2: S1 = F#4, F#4, F#4 (three beats sustained); B1 = F#3, F#3, F#3.
   These outer voices are a perfect octave apart and both stationary.
   Strict counterpoint prohibits parallel octaves, but in 8-voice choral
   writing with 6 other voices present, sustained unison pitch-class
   between soprano and bass over a long note is conventional and expected.
   The D-major harmony is fully voiced by the inner 6 voices (D, F#, A
   in multiple registers). This is a stylistic concession to the hymn's
   block-chord nature; the rule is relaxed here.

2. Leap of a 4th in A2, bar 1 (pickup→beat 2):
   A2: A3→A3→... the pickup A3 to bar 1 beat 2 A3 is static, but bar 1
   beat 1 to beat 2: A3→A3 (repeated). No problem. Actually checking bar 5:
   A2 moves G,→A,→B,→C (G#3→A3→B3→C#4) — all stepwise ascending. Fine.
   A2 pickup→bar1: A,→A, (step to same = repeated note). Fine.
   No fourth-leap violations found.

3. Baritone peak C4 at bar 5 beat 1:
   Exceeds comfortable Baritone top (A3) by a minor 3rd. Justified by
   the textural climax at the phrase boundary and the fact that professional
   baritones routinely sing C4 in full-voice choral writing. Noted above
   in Range Log with a suggested fallback.

4. Tenor II bar 1 beat 3 — B2 concert:
   Near the bottom of T2 range. Noted in Range Log. Not eliminated because
   the stepwise descent E3–B2–A2 (written E4–B4–A4) creates smooth voice
   leading and is brief (two quarter notes). Relax by raising to D3–C#3 if
   singers have difficulty, adjusting harmonic voicing accordingly.
