# Voicing Rules

## LH (bass clef, up to D4)

- Bottom intervals must be at least a perfect fourth (5 semitones) apart
- Allowed intervals between adjacent LH notes: P4 (5), P5 (7), m6 (8), M6 (9), m7 (10), P8 (12) or wider
- No m2 (1), M2 (2), m3 (3), M3 (4) between bottom notes — too muddy in bass register
- Exception: top note of LH may form m3/M3 with note below — acceptable near middle C where 3rds are less muddy
- Target 3-4 notes per hand
- Open voicing: root-5th-octave-3rd typical

## RH (treble clef, C4+)

- 3 consecutive chord tones above LH top
- Close voicing — 3rds OK in treble register

## General

- Hands never cross — LH always below RH
- Max 12-string span per hand (diatonic steps)
- One chord per measure (downbeat only)
- When same chord repeats into next measure, advance voicing window up the harp
- Harp range: C2 (MIDI 36) to G5 (MIDI 79)
- Diatonic only in RH/LH — melody staff may have accidentals

## Voice Leading

### SATB Voice Roles

- **Soprano** — the melody. Highest voice. What the congregation hears and sings. The other 3 voices exist to support it.
- **Bass** — the foundation. Lowest voice. Typically sings chord roots and fifths. Bass movement by 4th/5th (G→C, D→G) is the strongest progression.
- **Alto and Tenor** — inner voices. Fill in chord tones soprano and bass don't cover. Move as little as possible — by step or common tone. They're the glue.

### Motion Between Two Voices

- **Parallel motion** — both move same direction, same interval (parallel 3rds/6ths good; parallel 5ths/octaves forbidden)
- **Similar motion** — both move same direction, different intervals
- **Contrary motion** — voices move opposite directions (strongest, most independent)
- **Oblique motion** — one voice holds while the other moves

### Single Voice Behavior

- **Stepwise motion** (conjunct) — moving by 2nd (half or whole step)
- **Leap** (disjunct) — moving by 3rd or larger
- **Common tone** — holding the same note into the next chord
- **Passing tone** — non-chord tone filling in between two chord tones by step
- **Neighbor tone** — steps away from a chord tone and steps back
- **Suspension** — holds note from previous chord into new chord, creating dissonance, resolves down by step (4-3 suspension, 7-6 suspension, etc.)
- **Anticipation** — arrives at the next chord's note early
- **Retardation** — like a suspension but resolves up

### Voice Function in the Chord

- **Doubling** — which chord tone appears in two voices (double the root, double the fifth)
- **Spacing** — how far apart adjacent voices are (close position vs open position)
- **Voice crossing** — when alto goes below tenor, etc. (generally avoided)
- **Voice overlap** — when a voice moves past where the adjacent voice just was

### Chord-to-Chord Categories

- **Root motion** — described by interval: by 5th (strongest), by 3rd, by 2nd (weakest/most colorful)
- **Strong progression** — root moves down a 5th (V→I, ii→V)
- **Weak progression** — root moves up a 5th (I→V) — less final, not bad
- **Resolution** — a dissonant chord moving to a consonant one (V7→I)

## Four-Part Harmony Rules (Chorale Style)

The fundamental constraint: four independent melodic lines must combine to produce correct chords at every beat while each line remains singable.

### Chord Completeness

- Every chord must have a root (almost always)
- The third must be present (it defines major vs minor)
- The fifth can be omitted if needed
- When 4 voices share 3 pitch classes, one tone is doubled

### Doubling Rules

- Double the root (preferred)
- Double the fifth (acceptable)
- Never double the third (it's the color tone — doubling it makes it too prominent)
- Never double the leading tone (7th scale degree) — it has a mandatory resolution, and two voices resolving the same way creates parallel octaves

### Spacing Rules

- Soprano to alto: no more than an octave apart
- Alto to tenor: no more than an octave apart
- Tenor to bass: can be wider (up to a 12th or more)
- Voices should not cross (soprano always highest, bass always lowest)

### Voice Leading Rules (chord to chord)

- Move each voice the shortest distance possible
- If a note belongs to both the current and next chord, keep it in the same voice (common tone)
- Move the remaining voices by step
- The leading tone (scale degree 7) must resolve up to tonic (scale degree 1)
- The seventh of a chord must resolve down by step
- Avoid leaps larger than a 5th (octave leaps OK in bass)

### Forbidden Parallels

- No parallel 5ths between any two voices (G→A over C→D)
- No parallel octaves between any two voices
- No hidden/direct 5ths or octaves (similar motion arriving at a 5th/octave, especially in outer voices)

### Implications for Harp Voicing

The SATB voices aren't random notes that happen to form chords. Each voice has been carefully placed to follow all these rules simultaneously. When you split SA→RH and TB→LH, you break apart voice leading pairs that were designed to work together. The current pipeline doesn't preserve these relationships — it just takes the chord and re-voices it for harp. A deeper approach would analyze how each voice moves and try to preserve that motion in the harp voicing — for example, if the tenor resolves a suspension, the LH should reflect that movement rather than just jumping to a new voicing window.

## Beyond Four-Part Harmony (5+ Voices)

The core rules are the same — they get more flexible as voices are added.

### What Changes

- **Doubling becomes tripling** — with 5 voices and a triad (3 pitch classes), one tone must be tripled. Root still preferred.
- **Complete chords are easier** — every chord can have root, 3rd, 5th present plus doublings. No need to omit the 5th.
- **Seventh chords are complete** — 4-part writing often omits the 5th from 7th chords. 5+ voices can include all four tones plus doubling.
- **Spacing rules relax** — inner voices can spread out more. The octave limit between adjacent voices loosens.
- **Parallel restrictions loosen slightly** — parallel 5ths/octaves still forbidden between outer voices (soprano/bass), but some composers allow them briefly between inner voices in thick textures.
- **Voice crossing becomes more common** — with 5-8 parts, inner voices weave around each other more freely.
- **Independent motion is harder** — more voices means more pairs to check for forbidden parallels. With 8 voices there are 28 pairs to monitor.

### What Doesn't Change

- Parallel 5ths/octaves between soprano and bass — always forbidden
- Leading tone resolution — always required
- Seventh resolution — always steps down
- Root must be present
- Third must be present
- Contrary motion is still strongest

### Jazz/Pop Voicing Techniques (beyond classical rules)

- **Extended chords** — 9ths, 11ths, 13ths (stacking more 3rds)
- **Quartal voicing** — built on 4ths instead of 3rds
- **Cluster voicing** — close 2nds stacked together (deliberately breaks the "no small intervals" rule)
- **Drop voicing** — take a close-position chord and drop one voice down an octave (drop 2, drop 3, drop 2+4)
- **Spread voicing** — wide gaps between notes for orchestral color

For hymns, four-part rules are the framework. The OpenHymnal follows them faithfully.

## Algorithm for Adding Voices (4→5→6→7→8 parts)

The algorithm is the same each time a voice is added.

### Step-by-Step

1. **Start with existing voices.** They already follow all the rules.
2. **Determine what the new voice sings.** At each chord:
   - What pitch classes are present? (root, 3rd, 5th, 7th)
   - What's already doubled?
   - What should be doubled? (root preferred, then 5th, never leading tone)
   - New voice gets either: a needed chord tone, or doubles the best candidate
3. **Choose the octave.** Must fit between existing voices without violating spacing. If adding between alto and tenor, stay below alto and above tenor (or cross in thick textures).
4. **Connect to the next chord using voice leading rules.** The new voice must:
   - Move by step or common tone (prefer smallest motion)
   - Not create parallel 5ths or octaves with ANY existing voice
   - Not create hidden 5ths/octaves with outer voices
   - Resolve tendency tones (leading tone up, 7ths down)
5. **Check all pairs.** Each new voice adds N new pairs to check (N = number of existing voices). 4→5 adds 4 pairs. 7→8 adds 7 pairs. Every pair must be free of forbidden parallels.

### How Composers Add Voices in Practice

- **4→5:** Add a second tenor or second alto. The new voice shadows an existing inner voice in 3rds or 6ths (safe parallel intervals) and diverges when doubling rules demand it.
- **5→6:** Split both inner voices — Alto 1/Alto 2 and Tenor 1/Tenor 2. Each pair moves in parallel 3rds/6ths most of the time.
- **6→7→8 (double choir / SSAATTBB):** Two independent SATB choirs. Each choir follows four-part rules internally. The two choirs relate by:
  - Singing the same chords in different voicings (homophonic)
  - Alternating phrases (antiphonal — call and response)
  - Moving independently (polyphonic — hardest to write)

### Key Insight

You don't write 8 independent voices from scratch. You write 4 good voices, then split each into 2 that stay close together. The split voices move in parallel 3rds/6ths (the safe intervals) and only diverge when needed for chord completeness or to avoid forbidden parallels. This is exactly what the SSAATTBB pipeline does — takes SATB and creates a second version of each voice.
